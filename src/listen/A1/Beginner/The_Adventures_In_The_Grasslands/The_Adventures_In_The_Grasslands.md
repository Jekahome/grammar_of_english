<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter One</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Here I Am</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">"Here I am. Here I am. Here I am." Thinks Spotty, a small, handsome, baby giraffe. Mother is looking at him and listening to him. Of course, he can't talk. But she understands his every word.</p>

<p>"Who are you?" Spotty asks.</p>

<p>"I'm your mum," she answers.</p>

<p>"My mum? Who is mum?" he asks.</p>

<p>"Mum is your good friend. Mum is always with you. And you are Spotty, my son."</p>

<p>"Oh, great!" Spotty says and smiles at his mother.</p>

<p>"How are you?" Mother asks.</p>

<p>"I'm hungry," Spotty answers.</p>

<p>"Don't worry. I'm not only your friend, I'm your dining room too. I've got a lot of nice milk for you. Stand under me and drink," she says.</p>

<p>Spotty drinks and drinks. The milk is warm and tasty, and it never ends. Now, he isn't hungry anymore.</p>

<p>Mother opens her mouth, and Spotty can see a long, thick thing inside.</p>

<p>"What's that?" he asks.</p>

<p>"It's my tongue," Mother answers.</p>

<p>"Why is it so long?"</p>

<p>"Because we are giraffes. I think only giraffes have got very long tongues," Mother says.</p>

<p>"Are there many giraffes, or are there only you and me?" Spotty asks. Mother smiles. "Of course, there are a lot of giraffes. Soon, we can visit them. But now, I must teach you to walk and run."</p>

<p>Spotty gets up and starts walking.</p>

<p>"What a clever baby I have got!" Mother laughs. "Now, try to run."</p>

<p>Spotty tries, but falls down. "I've got too many legs," he says. "They can't run altogether."</p>

<p>"Then tell them to run in turn," says Mother. "First, your left legs run, then your right ones."</p>

<p>"And which of them are left?" Spotty asks.</p>

<p>"Can you hear a strange beat inside your body?"</p>

<p>Spotty listens. "Yes. It's here."</p>

<p>"This is your heart. It's on the left. Your left legs are on the left too," says Mother.</p>

<p>"Why have I got four legs and only one heart?" Spotty asks. "And why is it on the left?"</p>

<p>"Nobody knows. But I think you can't run with one leg. And four hearts must be too noisy."</p>

<p>Spotty looks in his mum's eyes. They are big and clever. And they are very kind.</p>

<p>"Let's play, son," she says. They start walking to a tall tree. Spotty can't walk fast, but Mother is walking behind him. When they come to the tree, Spotty cries, "I'm the first!" He is happy.</p>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Two</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">My Family</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">Hi. My name is Jack. I'm a boy, and I'm twelve. I live with my parents in Africa. My parents are scientists. They know a lot about African animals. I have got a sister. Her name is Jill. She is twenty-three. Jill doesn't live with us. She lives with her husband, Bill. I don't like Bill. If Jill wants something, Bill always does it. Sometimes these are very silly or bad things.</p>

<p>One day, I'm visiting my sister and her husband. We're having coffee when suddenly she says, "Bill, I want to have a pet giraffe." Bill's eyes get very big. He hasn't got a face now. Only eyes. "Yes. A pet giraffe," repeats my sister. "It can live in the hall. Our hall is very big." Bill's eyes get normal, and he says, "Okay, dear. I think I can get you a giraffe."</p>

<p>"Where do you want to get it?" I ask.</p>

<p>"In the grasslands, of course," he answers. "We can't buy a giraffe. We haven't got much money."</p>

<p>"You mustn't do that," I cry. "You can have serious problems with the police."</p>

<p>"Why? I can find a stray cat or a stray dog in the street. Why can't I find a stray giraffe?" I look at Bill. Sometimes he is very silly. And it is all because of my sister.</p>

<p>"Jack, don't tell our parents about it," Jill asks, please. And she smiles at me.</p>

<p>"I think you can't hide a giraffe in the house," I say. I stand up, say goodbye to them, and walk to the door. But, suddenly, I have an idea. "Bill, can you take me with you? I can help you to catch the giraffe."</p>

<p>"Great," says Bill. "Tomorrow, at six o'clock in the morning."</p>

<p>This night, I can't sleep. I am thinking about my bad sister and silly Bill. I am thinking about that poor giraffe, too. Giraffes must live in the grasslands. They are tall, big and strong, and they must walk free. They must have a lot of trees around. They love eating fresh leaves. And if people want to look at them, they can watch a film or go on a safari. It is very wrong to catch giraffes and put them in cages or houses. Then I'm not thinking anymore. I am sleeping.</p>

  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>

    <p class="first-para">My alarm clock rings. I wake up. It is sunny and hot. I am happy. It is the first day of the summer holidays. I am free. I can swim or play football. I hope he can't catch it.</p>

Dad says giraffes can see some bright colours. I hope they can see the red colour. I put on a red cap, open the door, and go out. I run to Bill and Jill's house. When I'm at their door, Bill comes out. He's wearing a brown shirt and brown shorts. He has got a green cap on his head and green gloves on his hands. He has got a big green bag on his back. He thinks he looks like a small tree with four green leaves.</p>

<p>"A nice tree," I say.</p>

<p>"Where?" Bill asks.</p>

<p>"Opposite me, of course," I say.</p>

<p>"Aha! I'm very clever," Bill says, and smiles. Suddenly, Bill stops smiling. He's looking at my red costume. "Why are you wearing red?" he asks. "You don't look like a tree."</p>

<p>"But I'm not a tree," I say. "I'm the red light. What do you do when you see the red light in the street?"</p>

<p>"I stop," Bill says.</p>

<p>"I stop too," I say. "Everyone stops. Men and women, boys and girls, cats and dogs, and, of course, giraffes."</p>

<p>"You are great!" Bill cries, and smiles at me again.</p>

<p>"Do birds live on giraffes?"</p>

<p>"How terrible!" Bill cries. "I don't want to be somebody's dining room."</p>

<p>"Insects in my hair?" Bill looks afraid and puts his fingers in his hair.</p>

<p>"Those birds eat insects. They are their favourite food," I say.</p>

<p>"Then I hope she likes insects," I say.</p>

<p>"No," Bill cries. "She doesn't."</p>

<p>Bill starts thinking. "Look, Jack, I can wash our giraffe in the bathroom."</p>

<p>Bill looks at me and thinks again. "Then I can wash it in the garden or take it to the lake."</p>

<p>"You can have a lot of problems with that baby," I say. I'm looking at him and he is looking at me. Poor Bill. He loves my sister. He's ready to do everything for her. Birds can't change it. Even terrible insects can't change it.</p>

<p>"The one next to the bushes," says Bill.</p>

<p>"No, the one behind that tall tree." There are five baby giraffes. Bill looks and looks.</p>

<p>"Let's catch the one between the trees and the bushes," I say. Bill takes the rope and goes to the baby. The baby can see us. It turns and runs to the guard giraffe.</p>

<p>"That's bad," says Bill. "We must catch another one." He goes to the bushes, but another baby looks at Bill and runs to the guard giraffe too.</p>

<p>"They are clever. These silly giraffes," says Bill. He's angry.</p>

<p>"Let's tie the big giraffe to a tree," I say. "Then we can catch a small one."</p>

<p>"No," cries Bill, "not again." He looks at me. I don't like his eyes. At that moment, he sees a third baby. It is opposite a bush with big pink flowers. The baby can't see Bill. It is smelling the flowers. "Aha," cries Bill and runs to the baby. I don't know what to do. Again, I put my hands around my mouth and roar. That is thirteen. What are they doing? It is a terrible roar. But this time, Bill turns too fast. You can see my face and hands around my mouth.</p>

<p>"What are you doing?" he cries.</p>

<p>"I'm sneezing," I say, and roar again. "Again sneezing, I'm sorry, I always sneeze like that." Bill's face is very strange now. He puts his hands in front of him and makes fists. At that moment, we can hear a terrible noise. I look behind Bill. All the big giraffes and their babies are running at us.</p>

<p>"Look out, Bill," I cry. Bill looks round and sees the giraffes and jumps up. Then he starts running. I run too. We run and run. There is that muddy waterhole in front of us.</p>

<p>"Jump," Bill and I cry together. We are with our heads in the mud again. When we're out of the mud, we can't see any giraffes. There are no giraffes next to the trees or bushes. There are no giraffes on the grass. But there are other animals. These animals have got very big ears. And they are very angry. They are hitting trees and the trees are falling onto the ground. They are elephants.</p>

<p>"What are they doing?" asks Bill. He's afraid.</p>

<p>"They're breaking trees," I answer.</p>

<p>"I can see that," Bill says. "Why are they doing it?" I'm thinking what to say. Suddenly, I've got an idea. "They're playing hide and seek with the giraffes. Look. There are no giraffes. They're hiding from the elephants." Bill is surprised. "How do you know?" he asks.</p>

<p>"My dad knows a lot about different animals. Elephants and giraffes are good friends. They often play together. Elephants are very clever. If they break down all the trees and bushes, giraffes can't hide behind them. Then elephants find them."</p>

<p>"And what do they do after that?" asks Bill.</p>

<p>"Then elephants hide from giraffes. Only giraffes are very tall and can see very far. And elephants are too fat. So giraffes can see elephants behind trees and bushes."</p>

<p>"Where do elephants hide then?" asks Bill.</p>

<p>"They jump into a waterhole and hide in the mud," I answer.</p>

<p>"This waterhole?" Bill's eyes get very big and his face changes its colour.</p>

<p>"Any waterhole. Only I can't see another one here," I say. Bill looks at me, then he cries, "Mummy!" And runs out of the mud. I run after him. We run and run, then Bill gets tired and falls on the ground. I sit down next to him.</p>

<div class="book-page">

    <p class="first-para">We're sitting on the grass. "Can you see any elephant?" Bill asks me.</p>

<p>"No, I can't," I say.</p>

<p>"Are there any giraffes?" he asks again. I look around. Now there are some giraffes close to us. I can even see Spotty and his mum. "Yes," I say.</p>

<p>Suddenly Bill says, "We're so dirty. Let's go home now. Tomorrow we can take some carrots and apples with us."</p>

<p>"A great idea," I say. "We should take some cabbages and watermelons too. They're giraffes' favourite food."</p>

<p>"Are you crazy?" Bill cries. "How can we take them? They're so heavy."</p>

<p>"We can roll them," I say. Bill is looking at me. I don't like his face. I think now he understands what I'm doing here.</p>

<p>"Jack," he cries. "Why are you here with me? First you roar like a lioness. Then you have that silly idea to tie the mother giraffe. And now..." he doesn't finish. Because we can hear a terrible roar. And this time it is real. It's the roar of an angry lioness. We look back. We can see her. The lioness looks at us. Then at the giraffes. And again at us. She's thinking which to eat. Bill's face is white. I think mine is white too. We are too afraid. We start shaking.</p>

<p>Suddenly, Spotty's mum runs at the lioness. Her son is running between her legs. When they come up to the angry animal, the mother puts up her front legs. The lioness is afraid. She starts jumping around the giraffes. But the mother always turns with her front legs to her and tries to kick.</p>

<p>Hooray! The lioness is too tired now. She can't fight anymore. She turns round and runs away. Spotty is looking at his brave mother. I look at Bill. He looks strange. Suddenly, he gets up and walks to the giraffes. Does he want to catch them now when they're too tired? But Bill stops and puts his head and half of his body down. Oh, I understand. He is saying "thank you" to the brave mother.</p>

<p>"Mum, it's that strange animal again," says Spotty.</p>

<p>"Yes, but it's smiling at us and it hasn't got a long tongue in its mouth."</p>

<p>"And there's no tree in his neck," says Spotty. "Poor animal. Can't live long without a tongue." Spotty turns to a bush, takes some leaves with his tongue and gives them to the strange animal. "You can always come here," he says. "And I can give you tasty leaves, eat them and live long."</p>

<p>"Look, Jack. Your Spotty is giving me a present. Giraffes are so nice." Bill takes the leaves. He looks so funny. I like him now.</p>

    <p class="first-para">Bill isn't very clever, but he is kind. He can't hunt Spotty anymore. He looks at me. "Jack, you're right. It's wrong to catch giraffes. They must walk free. But what should I tell Jill? She can get very angry and I love her." He sits down on the grass. He's very sad.</p>

<p>"What's the matter with you?" she cries, and then we say, "Dear Jill, there are lots of giraffes in the grasslands. They're all very tall and beautiful. We can't choose only one. If you want a pet giraffe, let's go to the grasslands together. Then you can show us your favourite one. Only don't put on many clothes, put on a swimming costume."</p>

<p>Jill doesn't understand. "A swimming costume? Why do I need a swimming costume there?"</p>

<p>"Because there are angry lionesses there," I say. "When they're hungry, they run at you. They run very fast. You can't run away from them, so you must hide in a muddy waterhole."</p>

<p>"And when you get out of the mud, your clothes are dirty and very heavy," says Bill.</p>

<p>"Yes," I say. "Then you can't walk fast in them. And when angry elephants come and start breaking trees..." oh, Jill stops me. She looks afraid now. "I think I don't need a pet giraffe. I've got no free time. And our house isn't very tall."</p>

<p>"We can make a big hole in the roof," I say.</p>

<p>"Are you crazy?" Jill asks. "I don't want any holes in my roof. I don't need a giraffe. That's all."</p>

<p>"Okay," we say. "Can we go to the bathroom now?"</p>
