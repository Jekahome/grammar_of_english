  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter 1</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Ancient and Medieval History</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">Greetings! I am the spirit of Ashoka the Great. In the third century B.C., I ruled for almost four decades over the Mauryan Empire, the largest empire in India. My emblem, the Ashoka Chakra wheel, is in the centre of the Indian flag and it symbolises movement. Like a wheel, India is always in motion, constantly changing and making progress.</p>

<p>The Indian subcontinent got its name from the word Indus, because its first civilisation, the Indus Valley Civilisation, lived on the Indus River, in today's Pakistan and Northwest India. Being Emperor of India was not an easy job, as the country was so enormous, about 13 times the size of the UK. For centuries, India was divided into lots of small kingdoms, each governed by its own ruler. This led to political problems, as the rulers didn't always see things the same way. With so many rulers governing at the same time, it was very difficult for the emperor to keep universal power over his empire.</p>

<p>Empires rose and empires fell. Some lasted decades or centuries, while others quickly disintegrated because they had weak rulers. All these rulers changed the face of India in some way, but only one of them succeeded in uniting India's kingdoms into one state. His name was Chandragupta Maurya, and he was the powerful and strong warrior who founded the Mauryan dynasty. He ruled as the first Mauryan emperor of India from 321 to 297 BC. By the way, he was also my grandfather.</p>

<p>When Chandragupta died, my father, Bindusara, followed him and ruled from 297 to 272 or 3 BC. He had 16 wives and many children, which meant there was a lot of competition for the throne. After his death, the fighting for the throne continued for four long years until I finally got the most support. I took my father's place as emperor in 268 BC and ruled until 232 BC, expanding India's territories even more.</p>

<p>I believed in Buddhism and dedicated my life to spreading this religion in my country. I was considered one of the most powerful emperors of the Indian subcontinent in ancient times, and mine was one of the most glorious and prosperous periods in Indian history. Maybe that's why my emblem appears on the modern Indian flag.</p>

<p>When I died in 232 BC, my people were inconsolable. According to legend, people saw this as a sign of my greatness. Unfortunately, all good things come to an end, and the Mauryan Empire fell when the last of the Mauryan rulers was killed in 185 BC.</p>

<p>However, the end of one era marks the beginning of another, and there were many other emperors, not as great as my grandfather and I (if I may say so), but who were also admirable and good for the country. Between the fourth and sixth centuries AD, India experienced its golden age, with powerful rulers like Chandragupta I and Chandragupta II. This was a time of many achievements in the fields of engineering, arts, science, technology and religion. During this period, chess was invented, and a monetary system was used for the first time. Later, impressive temples were built. Already in the 11th century AD, builders managed to move an 82,000 kg stone to the top of a temple. How did they do that amazing thing? We still don't know.</p>

<p>Archaeological evidence shows that water conservation for irrigation was another important achievement of ancient India. Can you believe that people still use this ancient system today?</p>

<p>Now let's move on to another important empire. Have you ever wondered why so many Indians are also Muslims? The first Muslims arrived in India in the 8th century and slowly began to conquer territory. Muslim invaders founded the Mughal Empire in 1526. They were a strong empire, particularly during the reign of Akbar the Great. The Mughals also built impressive monuments like the Taj Mahal and the Red Fort and introduced an effective tax collection system. Although they were Muslims, they were influenced by the Hindus and respected their culture.</p>

<p>Unfortunately, not all the Mughal emperors were like Akbar. Many of them were useless rulers who killed their own families to win the throne. Aurangzeb, the sixth emperor of the Mughal Empire, was particularly cruel. He imprisoned his father and ordered his servants to cut off his elder brother's head, which he sent to his father in a basket. Despite his cruelty, Aurangzeb was the last of the great emperors of the Mughal dynasty. By the time he died, after ruling for almost 49 years (1658-1707), the Mughal Empire was already disintegrating. His death marked the end of an era and the beginning of the modern or colonial period. There were other emperors after him, but the wars of succession and bad administration led to the collapse of the Mughal Empire in the mid-18th century.</p>

<p>I would love to share more of India's fascinating ancient and medieval history with you, but I must continue in my search for self-knowledge, so I'm leaving now. It's time for you to learn about India's modern history, Mahatma Gandhi and India's fight for independence.</p>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter 2</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">The Road to Independence</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para"><strong>Colonial rule.</strong> In the 1600s in Europe, tea and spices were in high demand. India produced these, so Queen Elizabeth I of England sent merchants to start a trading company in India, the British East India Company (EIC). It controlled all trade between England and Asia. For more than 100 years, the company traded mostly with India, buying cheap cotton cloth, silk, sugar and spices, which they sold for a lot of money. The Mughal emperors permitted the British to build factories in Bombay, Madras and Calcutta, and as more and more British traders went to live near the factories, these areas developed into colonies.</p>

<p><strong>The Marathas and growing British influence.</strong> The Marathas were Hindu warriors who were at war with the Mughals. They had existed since the 14th century, but as the Mughal Empire continued to decline, they started to become more powerful and they established their own Maratha Empire in 1674. After the last Mughal emperor with any real power died in 1748, the Marathas easily took control of most of northern India. However, they didn't have an emperor. They had several Peshwas, Prime Ministers governing different areas. This weakened the power of the Maratha Empire, making it easier for the growing British population to take control.</p>

<p>There were many problems between the British and the Governor of Bengal, Siraj-ud-Daulah, who had taken Calcutta from the British. The tension led to the Battle of Plassey in 1757. The British defeated Siraj-ud-Daulah's army and then bribed their candidate Mir Jafar to become the Governor of Bengal. This victory in the Battle of Plassey led to the expanding of British rule in India, which lasted for almost 200 years.</p>

<p>In 1775, there was a fight for power among the Peshwas, and the British East India Company was implicated. This British interference in the affairs of the Marathas led to the first of three Anglo-Maratha Wars. In the Third War, in 1818, the British defeated the Marathas. Now British rule was even stronger, and they influenced daily life in India. Because the British needed more money, taxes and territories to support their large armies, they continued to fight. They conquered territory after territory, and their empire grew.</p>

<p>The Indian people soon got tired of British rulers, who had no respect for their religions, and who tried to change some of their traditions. In 1857, the British officers went one step too far by ordering Indian soldiers to load ammunition that had cow and pig fat on it, even though they knew that cows are holy to Hindus and pigs are repulsive to Muslims. Many people condemned the East India Company for allowing this to happen, and it led to the Indian Mutiny, which took the lives of hundreds of thousands of soldiers. At this point, Queen Victoria finally realised that a trading company couldn't rule an empire, and in 1858, she made India part of the British Empire.</p>

<p><strong>British rule and the struggle for freedom.</strong> In 1905, the British government adopted a "divide and rule" policy to stop Indians from uniting against British rule in India. They divided Bengal into West Bengal, mainly Hindu, and East Bengal, mainly Muslim, because if the Hindus and Muslims were united, they would have too much power and could challenge British control over India. India was called "the jewel in the crown of the British Empire".</p>

<p>In 1915, Mahatma Gandhi became president of the Indian National Congress, also known as the Congress Party, founded in 1885 to fight against British rule in India. He led the freedom struggle and believed in Ahimsa, the practice of non-violence, and used this in the fight for independence. In 1919, the British government approved a law allowing them to silence the press and imprison political activists without a trial. Things got worse when hundreds of innocent Indian civilians were massacred at political meetings. Now the Indians hated the British more than ever and were even more determined to fight them.</p>

<p>Gandhi's answer was to create the Non-Cooperation Movement in 1920 to continue the struggle for Indian independence. This movement was one of Gandhi's first organised acts of civil disobedience. In this same year, the Indian National Congress reorganised itself and performed acts of civil disobedience like the Salt March in 1930 to protest against British taxes on salt. Gandhi decided to march 388 kilometres from his home to the Arabian Sea. As he marched, others joined him. By the 5th of April 1930, they were several thousand. Gandhi picked up salt from the ground; the others took salt too, but the British saw this act as a crime. The Indians were supposed to buy salt from them, not take it for free. They sent Gandhi to prison for this.</p>

<p>In 1942, Gandhi started the Quit India movement, which demanded the end of British rule over India. Marches, protests and freedom speeches followed, with many people arrested or killed. Fighting started between the Muslims and the Hindus, leading to the Indian Independence Act in 1947, by which the UK partitioned British India into two new independent dominions, India and Pakistan. In the end, Gandhi achieved his objective. The British flag was lowered and India was independent.</p>

<p>Unfortunately, India didn't live in peace with its neighbour Pakistan from 1947 to 1999, mainly because of territorial conflicts over the Kashmir region. Tragically, on 30 January 1948, Mahatma Gandhi, the father of the Indian nation, was assassinated in New Delhi by a Hindu fanatic. Ironic that the man who had dedicated his life to non-violent protest was himself defeated by a cruel act of violence.</p>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter 3</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">The People of India</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">Imagine approximately 1.35 billion people living in a country only three times bigger than the USA. It's not surprising that India is so overpopulated. It has the second largest population in the world. More than 80% of the population is Hindu, so you'd expect Hinduism to be the official religion. But in fact, India doesn't have an official religion. Hinduism, Islam, Buddhism, Sikhism and Jainism are just some of the many religions followed by the people. India is also the world's largest democracy, and the slogan "One Man, One Vote" is taken very seriously. Everybody has the right to vote, even if they live deep in a forest like Bharat Das Darshandas, an elderly temple priest. He is the only voter in the remote Gir Forest in the state of Gujarat. Two policemen travel through the lion-infested forest to get his vote.</p>

<p><strong>The Caste System.</strong> For more than 3,000 years, India has had a caste system which most people believe came from Brahma, the Hindu god of creation. This system divides the population into four main categories, according to social importance and profession: Brahmins, Kshatriyas, Vaishyas and Shudras. The Dalits, also called Untouchables, weren't even part of the caste system because they were so low. Each caste is represented by a different part of the body of the Brahma statue.</p>

<p>Jawaharlal Nehru, the first Prime Minister of India, came from the Brahmin caste. This is the highest caste for priests and teachers. Siddhartha Gautama, the Buddha, was a Kshatriya, the second highest caste. Kshatriyas were warriors and rulers of towns and cities. Mahatma Gandhi was born to a family of Vaishyas, the caste which were mainly farmers, merchants and traders. Chandragupta I, ruler of the Gupta dynasty, was a Shudra from the lowest caste. Shudras were labourers without any social and economic rights. However, Chandragupta I worked hard and became a Kshatriya. Ram Nath Kovind was born a Dalit or Untouchable, the people who cleaned the streets and the toilets. He came from a poor family and grew up in a poor hut with six brothers and sisters. He didn't even have a bicycle, so he walked eight kilometres to school every day. But believe it or not, he became President of India in 2017.</p>

<p>The caste system remained unchanged for centuries, trapping people into a classification system from which it was very difficult to escape. You were born into a caste and could only marry a person of the same caste. It was very difficult to move to a higher or lower caste. In rural communities, the upper and lower castes usually lived in segregated colonies and they didn't drink from the same springs. Brahmins would not accept food or drink from Shudras. Today, the caste system still exists, mainly in rural areas, but it has much less influence. In the cities, different castes live side by side and even intermarry. Some Dalits and low caste Indians have advanced to occupy important positions in the country, like Ram Nath Kovind.</p>

<p><strong>Widows.</strong> It might be hard to imagine, but India has another group of people who are treated even worse than the Untouchables: widows. There are approximately 55 million widows in India and their lives are very difficult. In the past, when a married man died in India, his widow used to kill herself on his funeral fire. Now widows are not expected to do this, but they are forced to mourn their dead husbands for the rest of their lives. They are rejected from society and people think they bring bad luck. Many widows are thrown out of their homes by their husbands' families, who consider them a financial dependent, or by their children who see them as a burden. They can't marry again or go to weddings, even of their own children. They must wear white because in Hinduism, white is the colour of mourning. Even worse, widows are not allowed to work and provide for themselves, so they are forced to live in poverty, singing prayers in temples or begging for food and a roof over their head. Considered useless without a husband, thousands of them escaped to Vrindavan, known as the "city of widows" in the north of India. This is home to more than 20,000 widows who depend on charity and are simply waiting to die. Some organisations are trying to help the widows of Vrindavan and give them some money every month, but as many can't read or write, they can't even manage to go to the bank to take their money out. Clearly, there is a long way to go to change the attitude to this vulnerable and neglected section of Indian society.</p>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter 4</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Religion, Customs and Language</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para"><strong>Religions.</strong> There are four main religions in India: Hinduism (80%), Islam (14%), Christianity (2%), and Sikhism (2%). Buddhism and Jainism are also important and are followed by one percent of the population.</p>

<p><strong>Hinduism.</strong> To me, Hinduism is more a way of life than a religion. It developed from the religion of the Aryans who arrived in India around 1500 B.C. Like all Hindus, I believe in one God named Brahman. Many people think all Hindus are vegetarian, but that's not true. We are allowed to eat meat, although I prefer to be a vegetarian.</p>

<p><strong>Islam.</strong> I come to you in the name of Allah, the one and only God of the Muslims, to teach Islamic law. In Islam, we don't believe in the caste system. We have our own social classes. We live according to the laws of the Quran, which prohibit us from eating pork.</p>

<p><strong>Christianity.</strong> I am a Roman Catholic, one of many Christian denominations in India. I believe in one God, and that every human being is His child, and a brother or sister of Jesus Christ. I want to break down the caste system.</p>

<p><strong>Sikhism.</strong> I am a follower of the Sikh religion, which was founded by Guru Nanak in the late 15th century. We believe in one God, and we believe you don't have to pray or go to a priest to connect with him. In the Sikh religion, everyone is equal. We don't have social classes and don't follow the caste system. When it comes to food, we can choose to eat meat or not.</p>

<p><strong>Jainism.</strong> If you can't live without your mobile phone, you wouldn't make a very good Jain. We don't believe in material things or showing violence to any living being. We have got many gods. We don't believe in the caste system, and we're vegetarian. Actually, I don't even eat onions or potatoes, because when you pull them up from the earth, it damages tiny organisms in there.</p>

<p><strong>Buddhism.</strong> Prince Siddhartha Gautama, the Buddha, is my hero. He became enlightened and founded Buddhism over two and a half thousand years ago. That is my mission: to have my eyes opened to see how the world really works. I go to a special place called an ashram to do yoga and meditation and to teach religion. I'm vegetarian, but it's not a rule among Buddhists. I don't believe in the caste system, and I don't believe in the existence of just one God.</p>

<p><strong>Customs and Beliefs.</strong> "Holy cow" is a way of showing surprise in English, but did you know that cows really are holy to Hindus? In Hinduism, the mother figure is like a goddess. Since cows give milk and have a gentle nature, they are associated with mothers. They are considered holy, and Hindus idolise them. In India, there are more than 3,000 institutions which look after old or sick cows.</p>

<p><strong>Symbolism of the Snake.</strong> You might think snakes are scary and dangerous, but not in India. There the snake is holy, a symbol of rebirth, death and mortality, because it sheds its skin and is reborn. All around India, you can see cobras made from wood or stone. Some Indians believe that if you accidentally kill a cobra, it must be cremated like a human. In addition, both Hindus and Buddhists believe in the holiness of a serpent god called Naga, and every year they have a procession in its honour. Hindus idolise snakes in temples, offering them milk, incense and prayers.</p>

<p><strong>Language.</strong> Hindi and English are the official languages of India, although Sanskrit is spoken by Hindus, Buddhists and Jains. At the time of British rule in India, the country was divided into many states with their own dialects, and 22 different languages were spoken. English was a common language for people of all the different areas to communicate with each other. It remained the language of communication after the British left the country. English is widely spoken in the country today, and ironically, India is the largest English-speaking nation in the world. Most colleges and universities use English for teaching, and it has become a language of status. If you want to study at university and get a good job, you must know English. However, don't assume that all Indians speak English fluently. They don't. Also, understanding their accent can sometimes be quite a challenge.</p>

<p>The elephant holds a special place in Indian culture, particularly in the ancient temples of Kerala, south-western India, which are home to hundreds of captive elephants. Hindus believe elephants are holy animals, the reincarnation of Lord Ganesh, a god with an elephant head who rides on a tiny mouse. Many tourists come to Kerala to admire the elephants, particularly during festival season (December to May), when the animals are decorated with ornaments and exhibited through the streets. It's an extravagant and colourful spectacle, but many people think it's cruel, as the elephants are hobbled.</p>

<p><strong>Lifestyle.</strong> Indian culture is full of unique traditions and customs. One of these practices is the habit of being late. In India, time has little meaning. People say the only time Indians aren't late is for cricket matches, and that's probably because the umpires aren't Indians. Indians have their own particular way of dressing. Women often wear a traditional dress called a sari, which is wrapped around the body and over the shoulder. Men in villages still wear traditional clothes. A kurta is a shirt that reaches the knees and is worn over a skirt-like garment. The salwar kameez, tapered trousers and a long tunic, is another type of dress that has become very popular with the younger generation.</p>

<p>Traditional values are very important to Indians. Both in the past and present, an Indian's family name is based on his caste, place of origin or family occupation. Men are still considered the head of the family, but today women work too and contribute significantly to the family's financial situation.</p>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter 5</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Geography</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">Great things often happen by accident, and Bharat is one of them. Bharat is the official name of the Republic of India, the seventh biggest country in the world. Around 50 or 60 million years ago, this enormous peninsula crashed into Asia, forming the Himalayas and Hindu Kush mountains. The Himalayas in the north form the world's highest mountains. Many of India's rarest animals and plants live there, and if you're very lucky, you may even see a snow leopard.</p>

<p>The Indian peninsula is surrounded by the Bay of Bengal in the southeast, the Arabian Sea in the southwest and the Indian Ocean in the south. It's bordered by Bhutan, China and Nepal in the northeast, Myanmar and Bangladesh in the east, and Pakistan in the west. One of the things that make India so unique is its strategic location, halfway between east and west Asia. From India, Africa is easily accessible via the Indian Ocean, and Europe via the Suez Canal. The Strait of Malacca is one of the most important shipping routes between the Indian Ocean and the Pacific Ocean.</p>

<p>Before 1911, the capital of India was Kolkata, formerly known as Calcutta, but on the 12th of December 1911, the British King George V, who was also Emperor of India, announced that the capital should be in Delhi instead, as this was the political and financial centre of the country. The new capital, New Delhi, was inaugurated in 1931. Today, New Delhi has a population of almost 19 million people.</p>

<p><strong>Climate.</strong> There is a variety of terrain in India. In the Thar Desert, in the west, the weather is hot and dry, but if you travel to the northeast, you'll find jungles. In northern India, there's an area called the Ganges Plain. It's very fertile because water from natural springs runs down the Himalayas, taking fertile sediments with it. This is very important for growing crops, since half the country depends on agriculture. When there are floods in the Ganges Plain, the farmers use the water for irrigation. Floods are common in India because it has so many rivers and is a peninsula. There are also the monsoons, which occur from June to September, when the direction of the wind changes causing a lot of heavy rain to fall.</p>

<p><strong>Wildlife.</strong> In some parts of India, don't be surprised if you see an elephant having a massage. In Guruvayur Elephant Sanctuary, there's a spa exclusively for elephants, where they get royal treatment, luxurious baths, relaxing massages, and a special diet. The elephants here are much more fortunate than those who work for the tourist industry. India is home to a wide range of animals thanks to its varied climate. It's the only country in the world where you can find both lions and tigers. On the coast of the Bay of Bengal lies the Sundarbans, the world's biggest mangrove forest. Here you can find tigers swimming in the same rivers as dolphins, sea turtles, sharks, and saltwater crocodiles. Sadly, this area is being destroyed by man, who hunts animals and cuts down trees for firewood.</p>

<p><strong>The Ganges River.</strong> The Ganges got its name from the Hindu goddess Ganga. This 2,500 kilometre long river plays a very important role in the lifestyle of the people who live on its banks. They grow crops along the river because the earth is very fertile and they use its water for irrigation. Varanasi, the holiest city in Hinduism, is located on the banks of the Ganges. People throw the ashes of their dead relatives into the river as they believe it sends the ashes to heaven. The Ganges is the fifth most contaminated river in the world, but that doesn't stop it from being India's most important and holy river to more than one billion Indians. Locals bathe in the Ganges, drink from it, and perform purification rituals in it. At the Kumbh Mela festival, millions of people come to wash away their sins. The festival takes place in Allahabad, a city in northern India where three important rivers meet. Crowds of people stand on the riverbank at Sangam, the meeting point of these three rivers, reciting Hindu scriptures as they wait for their turn to enter the cold water. Religious leaders from Hindu monasteries lead the ceremony. They throw flowers at the people in the water shouting, "Long live the Ganges!"</p>

<summary>Chapter 6</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter 6</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Famous Places and Attractions</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para"><strong>The Taj Mahal.</strong> The Taj Mahal in Agra is one of the seven wonders of the modern world. You may think $827 million is a lot to spend on someone you love, but Emperor Shah Jahan didn't think twice. In the 17th century, he built the Taj Mahal as a symbol of love in memory of his third and favourite wife, the Persian princess Mumtaz Mahal. Shah loved Mumtaz with all his heart, and when she died while giving birth to their 14th child, he was heartbroken. He wanted to do something to honour her, so he designed and ordered the building of the world's most beautiful tomb on the banks of the Yamuna River. It took 20,000 workers almost 22 years to build this spectacular mausoleum from white marble, with floral decorations made from precious stones. Every year, around 6 million people visit the Taj Mahal and admire its splendour.</p>

<p><strong>Mumbai.</strong> Mumbai is the most overpopulated city in India. It's busy, crowded and unpredictable. Don't be surprised to see a poor hut next to a multi-million dollar home, or a national park in the middle of the city with wild animals walking around freely. Almost 14 million people live in Mumbai. That's more than twice the number of inhabitants per square kilometre than in New York. What's more, by 2020, it's estimated that Mumbai will have a population of 27 million people. Mumbai and New York have their similarities, but pace isn't one of them. If you're in a hurry, don't try to send a package to a foreign country from the Indian post office. A special person, called a "package wallah", puts your package in a cloth sack and seals it with a wax stamp, which takes over an hour. Riding a train in Mumbai during rush hour is an experience you'll never forget. The Indian Railway Company tries to fit 500 people into a train carriage meant for 188. The rest sit on the roof. Is that why around 3,000 people die in train-related accidents every year?</p>

<p><strong>Film City.</strong> Who hasn't heard of Bollywood, the Hindi language film industry? The name comes from a combination of Bombay (now known as Mumbai) and Hollywood, the American equivalent. Unlike Hollywood, Bollywood isn't an actual place, but Film City is. It's located in Mumbai and almost all Bollywood films are made there. At Film City, there are many different types of film locations, including lakes, gardens, villages, a temple, a prison, a court and a waterfall. You can take a guided tour and visit film sets. Those who prefer to be in a Bollywood film and not just visit the set of one can spend time in a popular shopping and dining district, where foreigners are often asked if they would like to be extras. Maybe 800 rupees (around 10 euros) isn't much for a day's work, but the experience is certainly worth a lot more.</p>

<p><strong>Varanasi.</strong> For some tourists, it's quite a shock to see the sight of unburned body parts floating on the Ganges. That's a sure sign that they're in Varanasi, the city of 23,000 temples. Almost two and a half million people visit Varanasi every year. Many of them are close to the end of their life and they come to bathe in the holy river. In Hinduism and Buddhism, Nirvana is the highest state that someone can reach. It's a state of enlightenment, a place of perfect peace and happiness. Hindus believe that dying in Varanasi brings salvation and Nirvana, so they travel to the city as soon as they realize they are close to death. After death, they are purified in the Ganges and then cremated. Any unburned body parts are thrown into the river. Ghats are places with steps that lead down to the banks of the river. In Varanasi, some Ghats are for bathing or religious ceremonies, while others are only used for cremations.</p>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter 7</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Festivals</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">Indians celebrate a multitude of festivals that contribute to the country's rich culture, traditions and spirituality. Some of the festivals are celebrated all around the country, while others are only celebrated in specific regions. Being a part of any of these festivals is a colourful experience.</p>

<p><strong>Diwali.</strong> Horrendous traffic jams and a spectacular fireworks display announce that Diwali, the Hindu festival of lights, has arrived. This is India's biggest and most important holiday. The name Diwali means "row of lighted lamps", and it's easy to see why. The whole country is illuminated by lamps called diyas, candles and fireworks, as Hindus celebrate the triumph of light over dark and of good over evil. Hindus believe in one true God, Brahman, but they have many other gods too. One of the most important gods is Lord Rama, who had many adventures. In one of these, he killed the Demon King Ravana, who had captured Rama's beloved wife Sita. The lamps that illuminate India during Diwali are to help Rama and Sita find their way home after the defeat of the demon.</p>

<p>In Dharavi, a very poor area in Mumbai, 2,500 potter families spend days making diyas before the festival starts. Men make them from clay and bake them in ovens. After that, women paint them and children help to pack them for sale. Everybody plays a part in the festivities. Like Christmas, Diwali is a holiday where families come together to share sweets and gifts. It's celebrated for five days, and on each day, different prayers are recited and different rituals are performed. In certain regions of Rajasthan, women participate in a special ritual, dancing in a circle with a flaming pot on their heads. In Goa, people build big, scary statues of the legendary tyrant Narakasura, a cruel and unpopular leader who tried to kill one of the beloved gods, Lord Krishna. Lord Krishna cut off Narakasura's head, and in celebration of this, the huge statues are burnt on the night before Diwali.</p>

<p><strong>Durga Puja.</strong> Durga Puja is both an arts festival and a religious celebration. It is celebrated mainly in West Bengal, in honour of the goddess Durga, the symbol of divine feminine energy and her victory over the evil buffalo demon Mahishasura. Kolkata's artisans spend a year preparing for the festival. They make special platforms and display ten-armed statues of the goddess Durga on them. During the festival, people go from platform to platform to see these works of art, and on the last day, they join a procession to the Ganges River, where the statues are submerged in the water. Durga Puja has a number of religious rituals. In one of them, a small banana plant is dressed in a sari, bathed in a nearby river and then used to attract Durga's energy and holy presence.</p>

<p><strong>Holi.</strong> Holi, known as the festival of colours, celebrates the arrival of spring and the victory of good over evil. In most places, Holi is celebrated for two days, and if you don't mind getting wet and dirty, it can be a lot of fun. The first night, Holika Dahan starts with a public fire symbolising the burning of Holika, the devil. People have parties and dance under water sprinklers. Rangwali Holi starts the next morning. People put colour all over each other's faces and throw water on each other while saying, "Don't be offended, it's Holi." Some of them first put coconut oil on their skin, which makes it easier to clean, so they won't look like a walking rainbow for a week after the festival. The British band Coldplay filmed the video clip of their song "Hymn for the Weekend" at the Holi festival.</p>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter 8</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Food, Glorious Food</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">India's multinational population and a significant role in the spice trade explain why Indian food explodes with flavour. A variety of factors have influenced Indian cooking, like the chilli pepper brought from Mexico by the Portuguese, the chutney developed by British housewives and their Indian cooks, and new food ideas introduced by Indians returning from their travels. Many of the dishes are based on rice, grains and pulses. Although the food differs from region to region, you can be certain that everything is made with plenty of spices, like garam masala, a combination of dried spices. Chutney is an important ingredient in Indian dishes. Many of us have eaten sweet mango chutney, sour mint chutney or hot chilli chutney, but you've probably never tried chutney, red ant chutney. A tribe from Bastar in Chhattisgarh collects tons of stinging, biting ants and their eggs, which are crushed, dried and ground into a powder. Tomatoes, coriander, garlic, ginger, chilli, salt and sugar are added to make the hot, spicy orange-coloured chutney. If you don't mind eating ants, chutney is very healthy because ants contain an acid with medicinal properties.</p>

<p><strong>Exotic Foods.</strong> If you think Indian food is only about spices, curries and vegetables, think again. In Assam, people eat a delicacy called eri polu. It's made from the pupa of silkworms and served with chorisa, a dish made from fermented bamboo shoots. Another exotic dish, sorpotel, is a kind of stew that's eaten in Goa. It looks quite harmless, but it's made from a pig's internal organs. Dishes made from dried fish are another delicacy in many parts of India. Nakham takes the prize for being the smelliest of all the fish dishes. It's a curry made from dried fish mixed with vegetables and distilled ashes.</p>

<p><strong>The Thali.</strong> Indian restaurants offer a multitude of dishes, so the thali (the Hindi word for a large plate) is the perfect solution for indecisive people, and it's popular in many regions of the country. It consists of a selection of curries, vegetables, sauces and yogurt. The food is served in small bowls on a round tray with a mountain of rice in the middle. It comes with chutney, pickles and poppadums, a thin round crispy bread. The dishes in the thali are eaten in a special order. For example, you start with the vegetables, followed by rice mixed with a spicy lentil sauce called sambar. Next, the spiciest of the sauces is served, and then the rasam, a sauce made from tomatoes. You eat a small portion of rice with each of the sauces. By this time, your palate will be on fire, so you'll be ready for the yogurt mixed with rice. For dessert, the thali sometimes includes something sweet. Thali is good for your digestion, because it's a balanced meal.</p>

<p><strong>Eating habits.</strong> It's not just the food in India that's unusual, but also the way it's eaten and served. In Europe, eating with your hands is not considered very polite, but in India it's a tradition. In fact, when you eat with your hands, you enjoy your food more, because it stimulates the senses. Indians believe that touching your food helps you feel the taste, texture and aroma, but you must only use your right hand because the left hand is considered unclean. In the south of India, it's a tradition to serve food on banana leaves instead of plates. Firstly, it's healthier because when you put hot food on a banana leaf, it releases nutrients that are good for you. Secondly, it tastes and smells better. Thirdly, it's environmentally friendly, but the best part is that there are no plates to wash at the end of the meal. Maybe we should all try this idea.</p>

  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para"><strong>Mahatma Gandhi.</strong> Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi was known to his people as Mahatma, which means "Great Soul", or Bapu, which means "Father". Gandhi was born in Porbandar, India, in 1869. He came from a well-respected family, and his parents arranged for him to marry Kasturba Makanji when he was thirteen. This may seem strange to you, but for an Indian boy it was expected. His parents wanted him to become a lawyer, so he studied law in London and then went to South Africa to find employment. While he was travelling on a train one day, Gandhi was asked to leave the first-class carriage even though he had a valid ticket. The reason? His skin colour. He was furious, and this marked the start of his fight against racial discrimination. In 1894, Gandhi founded the Natal Indian Congress and protested against injustice to Indians in South Africa.</p>

<p>Gandhi stayed in South Africa for twenty years, and then returned to India, but things weren't better there. The British discriminated against the Indians, and Gandhi refused to accept this, so he dedicated his life to fighting for equality. He organised non-violent civil disobedience campaigns in which people didn't go to work or eat, sat in the streets, or boycotted the courts. These acts may seem small, but when thousands of people did them at the same time, it had a huge impact. Gandhi was arrested many times and imprisoned, but his many followers and worldwide support made sure that he didn't stay in prison for long, especially because the British were afraid of what might happen if he died in prison. Finally, Gandhi's efforts paid off, and he won the battle for independence for the Indian people. Sadly, Gandhi the pacifist was shot and killed by a Hindu fanatic on his way to prayers in New Delhi on the 30th of January, 1948.</p>

<p><strong>Mother Teresa.</strong> Mother Teresa won the Nobel Peace Prize in 1979. In 1985, she received the Medal of Freedom, the highest honour awarded by the President of the United States to a civilian. She devoted her life to charity work and was declared a saint in 2016. Mother Teresa was born in Skopje, Macedonia, in 1910. At the age of 18, she joined a group of nuns in Ireland, where she learned English. After training, she was sent to India, where she became a nun in 1931. In that year, she started teaching in a school for girls in Kolkata. At 36, she discovered her mission in life: to help the sick and the poor. She learned how to give medical care and started doing charity work. It wasn't easy, and there were times when she herself had to beg for food. In 1950, Mother Teresa founded The Missionaries of Charity, an organisation that cared for the poor, hungry, homeless, disabled, and anyone who was not accepted in society. She opened many charity houses around the world, but is best known for her work in India. Mother Teresa died at the age of 87, on the 5th of September 1997. At the time of her death, the Missionaries of Charity were running 610 missions in 123 countries.</p>

<p><strong>Sachin Tendulkar.</strong> People say that if cricket were a religion, Sachin Tendulkar would be the god. The retired cricketer and captain is the greatest and most admired Indian cricketer of all time. No other player has ever scored so many runs in international cricket. Tendulkar received numerous awards for his notable achievements in sport, and after his final match in 2013, he was awarded the Bharat Ratna, the highest award given by the Indian government to a civilian. No other sportsperson has ever received this award. Tendulkar was born in Mumbai in 1973. He started playing cricket when he was 11 and practised for hours, showing his incredible batting skills while still at school. When he was 14, a famous Indian batsman called Sunil Gavaskar inspired him. Gavaskar made his international debut when he was 16, and 20 years later, Tendulkar broke Gavaskar's record. Tendulkar was highly motivated and became a champion cricketer, representing Mumbai and India in cricket for almost 24 years. In 2011, he won the Cricket World Cup with his team, making India very proud.</p>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter 10</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Sport</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para"><strong>Cricket.</strong> There is no doubt that cricket is India's most popular sport, thanks to the British. In a cricket match, there are two teams with 11 players in each team. They take turns batting and fielding. The objective of the batting team is to score as many runs as possible. The fielding players run around the cricket field to catch the ball and stop the batsman from scoring runs. The winner is the team with the most runs in an innings, which is when every player in the team has batted.</p>

<p>Cricket became popular in India in the 17th century. During British rule, the Maharaja, King of Patiala, played and supported cricket. He was captain of the Indian cricket team in 1911 and played in first class cricket matches between 1915 and 1937. The Maharaja's summer residence, Chail Palace, had the world's highest cricket ground, which was 2,250 metres high with forests all around. India's national team won the Cricket World Cup in 1983 and 2011, as well as other important trophies. India also holds the world record for winning all the ICC (International Cricket Council) tournaments when MS Dhoni was the captain.</p>

<p><strong>Field Hockey.</strong> Most Indian sports fans consider field hockey the national sport of India due to their excellent performances in past Olympics. In field hockey, there are 11 players in each team. The objective of the game is to score more goals than the opposing team by hitting the ball past the goalkeeper and across the goal line. Players aren't allowed to kick, hold or carry the ball. They use wooden hockey sticks instead. In the beginning, the Indian team was invincible, taking home six Olympic gold medals from 1928 to 1956 and two more in 1964 and 1980. In addition, India has won one silver and two bronze Olympic medals, more hockey medals than any other team in the world. They also won the 1975 World Cup tournament in Kuala Lumpur, Malaysia. But after 1980, they lost their supremacy, mainly because of a change in the rules. Until the early 1970s, hockey was played on grass. Then, in the mid-1970s, the International Hockey Federation decided to make it obligatory for all international hockey tournaments to be played on Astroturf. This change was implemented in the 1976 Montreal Olympics. And guess what happened? India didn't even win a bronze medal. There aren't many Astroturf grounds in India because they are expensive to lay and difficult to play on. However, in Western countries like Holland, Germany and Australia, there are lots of Astroturf grounds, so Europe and Australia have now become the leaders in field hockey. Today, field hockey is much more popular than it used to be, both in India and other countries. Women also play field hockey and they are very good at it. According to the International Hockey Federation, the Indian Women's Hockey Team is ranked 13th and has won three gold medals consecutively in international competitions.</p>

<p><strong>Kabaddi.</strong> Kabaddi is a very exciting team sport that is popular in Indian villages. It dates back to ancient times and was played by princes to prove how strong they were. Kabaddi is played on a small field divided into two halves. There are two teams of seven players and each team takes turns sending a raider (an attacking player) into the other half. The raider has to eliminate his opponents and then return to his half of the field without being stopped by the defenders. At the same time, he has to hold his breath and chant "Kabaddi, Kabaddi, Kabaddi" the whole time. The team that eliminates more opponents wins. The founder of Buddhism, Prince Siddhartha Gautama, played Kabaddi with his friends. The game requires both intelligence and physical strength. You also have to be in good shape and be able to anticipate what the opponent is going to do. India has been very successful at this sport, winning all the Kabaddi World Cup tournaments and gold medals in all the Asian Games.</p>

  <div class="chapter-title">Films: More Fact Than Fiction</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">In the past, Indian films were mainly the Bollywood romantic musicals of the Hindi language film industry. But more recently, English films about India expose the Western world to some of the country's more serious problems, like poor sanitation, poverty, pollution, child marriage and the terrible treatment of widows. Many of these films are based on true stories and paint a realistic picture of daily life, projecting images that remain in the audience's mind even after they leave the cinema. These films show facts that cannot be ignored.</p>

<p><strong>Toilet: A Love Story.</strong> The inspiration for Toilet: A Love Story (2017) came from the true story of Anita Nari, a poor Indian woman who discovered after she got married that her new house didn't have a toilet. This may seem trivial to you, but to Indian women who are in danger of being sexually assaulted if they relieve themselves outside, it's a serious problem. For this reason, many women go out in groups when they need to go to the toilet and wait until it gets dark so that nobody will see them. In the film, Anita almost divorces her husband, but in the end he builds her a toilet in their home and their marriage is saved. People relieving themselves in public places is a terrible problem in India, and according to UNICEF, more than half a million people still do that. The government has initiated a campaign to try and solve this problem. A new mobile phone app tells people where to find the nearest toilet, and thousands of toilets have been installed around the country, but unfortunately, many of them don't actually work.</p>

<p><strong>Water.</strong> Deepa Mehta's Water (2005) examines the social restrictions imposed on widows and exposes an ancient Hindu law that a widow must either marry her dead husband's brother or kill herself on her husband's funeral fire. This film caused a lot of controversy in India. On more than one occasion, the film sets were destroyed and Deepa's life was threatened, so the Indo-Canadian director moved the filming from India to Sri Lanka. Water is set in 1938 at a time when child marriage was common in India, and even young child widows were discriminated against. It centres on the lives of Chuyia, a little Indian girl who is widowed at the age of eight. After the death of her forty-year-old husband, Chuyia is sent to an ashram where her hair is cut off and she has to wear white. She believes that her mother will come and take her home, but in reality, she is destined to remain in the ashram forever because she is now a widow.</p>

<p><strong>Lion.</strong> The award-winning film Lion (2016) is based on the true story of Saroo Brierley, a lost little boy who travels 1,600 kilometres alone by train to Kolkata trying to find his big brother. Saroo is put in an orphanage and finally adopted by an Australian couple who live in Tasmania. Every year, more than 80,000 children go missing in India, and over 11 million children spend their lives on the streets. Saroo is one of them, and we see his struggle to survive as a street child, sleeping in train stations on a cardboard box and eating food from rubbish bins. As an adult, he lives a good life with his adopted family, but is tormented by memories of his childhood. After 25 years, Saroo decides to try and find his biological mother. With the help of Google satellite maps and faint memories of the night he lost his brother, he struggles to try and locate the village where he was born and the family he left behind.</p>

<p><strong>Slumdog Millionaire.</strong> Slumdog Millionaire (2008) is based on the life of a real person named Sushil Kumar. The word "slumdog" refers to someone who lives in a slum, a very poor area of the town. Sushil, 26, came from a very poor family that didn't even have money to buy a TV set, but his life changed forever when he won $1 million on the Indian version of the TV show "Who Wants to Be a Millionaire?". Through a series of flashbacks, Slumdog Millionaire shows the events that led Sushil to win the competition. We see the terrible living conditions and poor sanitation in Dharavi, a famous slum in contemporary Mumbai. For six children, two of whom came from the slums, acting in this film gave them a chance for a new life. Director Danny Boyle arranged for them to be educated after the filming and made sure that they attended the Oscars in 2009, where the film won several awards.</p>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter 12</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">India Today</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">Jawaharlal Nehru, India's first Prime Minister, had high hopes for his country. The night before India was declared a republic, Nehru expressed his feelings of triumph but warned the Indian people that their new freedom was going to bring responsibility. He warned them not to rest in the future, but to keep trying to make things better. Today, India has the fastest growing economy in the world, and it is predicted that by 2050, it will have the second largest economy after China. The country is, indisputably, an emerging superpower, but the road to achieving Nehru's hopes for the future is long. Despite India's growing power, many obstacles still need to be dealt with. Poverty, lack of sanitation, road accidents, and pollution are everywhere. The government is taking measures to deal with these issues, and the progress is significant, but it isn't happening fast enough.</p>

<p><strong>The Obstacles.</strong> India has the world's largest poor population. Between 1993 and 2011, the number of poor people dropped 25%, but there are still around 270 million people living below the poverty line. Around 45% of these people can't read or write, which leads to poverty because they can't find jobs easily. The situation is improving though. Between 2001 and 2011, the number of illiterate people was reduced by 31 million. With regard to sanitation, 80% of the poor live in rural areas and have little access to basic services. Only 21% have proper toilets, 61% have electricity, and only 6% have access to tap water. As a result, sanitation is poor, and diseases spread easily and quickly. The good news is that things are changing. Today, the average life expectancy is 69, whereas in 1960 it was 41, so we can see that India has made a lot of progress in this area.</p>

<p>Another problem in India is the frequency of traffic accidents, which is amongst the highest in the world. According to a government agency report, more than 135,000 people in India die in traffic accidents every year. One cause of accidents is drivers speeding while talking on their mobile phones. A group of Indian researchers have developed a low-cost device which prevents drivers from receiving or making mobile phone calls while driving, but allows other passengers to call. Other causes of accidents are driving under the influence of alcohol, not using seatbelts and bike riders not using helmets. Overcrowded roads in bad condition don't help the situation either.</p>

<p>A visit to Agra will confirm how serious India's air pollution problem is. The once-white marble exterior of the magnificent Taj Mahal is gradually turning yellow. In New Delhi, a 15-metre high mountain of rubbish supports the claim that the capital is one of the world's most polluted cities. There aren't enough disposal areas for all of India's rubbish, and the few existing ones can't possibly hold any more, so people burn waste outside their homes. This results in a blanket of smoke over many of India's villages, towns and cities. In addition, rubbish is thrown from passing trains, and the waste from train toilets is released directly onto the tracks. Rubbish is everywhere, even in the sacred Ganges River. How is the government dealing with this problem? Firstly, they are paying people to sort the rubbish into categories to be recycled, redistributed or disposed of. People receive a salary and keep any additional money made from selling the things they find in the rubbish. This new measure reduces the rubbish significantly, helping the environment and reducing poverty. Secondly, new rules for waste disposal were introduced in 2016 to help the problem, but they haven't been very effective because they aren't enforced.</p>

<p><strong>Cheap Labour in India.</strong> Have you ever wondered why so many companies outsource their call centres to India? It's because India has a high number of English speakers who are ready to work hard for a low salary, but they don't only work in call centres. When you buy something on Amazon or book a ticket on Virgin, a lot of the computer processing work is done by people in India as this is more lucrative.</p>

<p><strong>Tomorrow's India.</strong> By 2087, India will have the world's largest population. Today, half of this population consists of young people under the age of 25, many coming from underdeveloped places in the north of the country without education or qualifications. However, these are India's future leaders, so the government would like to alter the situation. They created a plan called "Skill India", hoping to provide 500 million people with skills by 2022, but this ambitious project met many obstacles and has now been abandoned. At the moment, it's impractical because so many people don't always have access to schools, banks and other services, but the digital age brings hope as more services and education are available online, so maybe things will improve in the future.</p>

<p>Despite all these obstacles, India attracts larger numbers of tourists every year. It's difficult not to be captivated by India's spectacular landscapes and architecture, exotic cuisine and incredible spirituality.</p>

<p><strong>Auroville.</strong> What do the English band, the Beatles, have in common with Mira Alfassa, a French-born spiritual leader known as "the Mother"? Both imagined a world without any countries, nothing to kill or die for, no religion and people living life in peace. The Mother's vision led to the founding of Auroville, City of Dawn, an experimental town in Tamil Nadu, India. Alfassa founded Auroville in 1968, transforming a useless piece of land into a green forest. Her dream was to promote human unity, practise sustainable living and provide for the future. Is Auroville a reality? Auroville is trying to be a self-sufficient town. Farms produce organic food and energy systems generate solar, wind and gas energy. The people who live in Auroville lead a unique lifestyle without politics or religion, which they believe can divide people. But not everything is perfect in Auroville. The original idea was for the population to work for the community and for the community to take care of them. But this hasn't worked too well. Aurovilleans need help from volunteers; otherwise, they can't maintain the community. Although everybody receives basic health care and education, some people have found ways to make extra money and live a better lifestyle. Despite the problems, the dream is still alive to have a community that doesn't waste the Earth's resources and lives in peace and harmony. Would you like to live there?</p>

<p><strong>The Yoga Community.</strong> From Hollywood film stars to the New Zealand rugby players, yoga is becoming more and more popular. In fact, in the USA, there are more yoga studios than Starbucks coffee shops. Today, people in the Western world practise yoga to improve flexibility, alleviate stress and get physically fit. But 5,000 years ago, people from the Indus Sarasvati civilisation practised yoga for a different reason: to discover their true nature. To do this, people try to control their thoughts. We all think thoughts, and they control how we feel. They can make us feel happy or depressed. By practising yoga, people focus on their thoughts for a long period of time and can begin to understand more about themselves. The special technique of controlling your thoughts is called meditation, and it existed as early as 1500 BC.</p>

<p>In ancient times, people went to an ashram, meaning "a shelter". In those days, the ashram was the house of the guru. A guru is a spiritual teacher who guides his or her disciples to a deeper understanding of themselves. In the past, children as young as 5 went to the guru's house to learn how to behave like Brahman, the one true God. Can you imagine leaving your parents at the age of 5? Very talented children graduated and received a Brahmadhvaja degree. In the course of time, these children became gurus and went on to teach others. But it was a very slow process, and often took more than 20 years.</p>
