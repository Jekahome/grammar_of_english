<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter 1</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">My First Home</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">I live in a big meadow with a pond and trees around it. During the day I run in the meadow with my mother, and at night I sleep next to her. There are six other colts in the meadow. I run with them too and have great fun. But sometimes they kick and bite me. My mother always says, "These colts are good, but they're not polite. You come from a family of famous horses. You must always be good and gentle, and never bite or kick."</p>

<p>My mother's name is Duchess, and she is old and wise. She is our master's favourite horse, and she always takes him to town in a small carriage. Our master is a kind man. He gives us a lot of oats, a big carrot, and a warm place to live. He always says nice things to us. Sometimes he gives us some sugar. We all love him.</p>

<p>One day I see a lot of dogs running in another green meadow, and they are barking loudly.</p>

<p>"What's happening, mother?" I ask.</p>

<p>"The dogs are running after a hare," she says. "There is a hunt. The dogs run fast, and the hare does too. There is a lot of noise."</p>

<p>Finally, the dogs catch the poor hare and kill it. Then another sad thing happens. Two horses and two men fall to the ground. One man gets up, but the other does not. The dogs are silent now, and everyone is looking at the man and the two horses.</p>

<p>"It's young George Gordon, the landowner's son," says mother, sadly. "The poor young man is dead." Only one horse gets up, but the other cannot, because his leg is broken. Someone runs to the master's house and gets a gun. There is a loud shot, and the horse is dead.</p>

<p>I am four years old now, and I am a handsome colt. I have a nice black coat and a white star on my forehead. One day, the landowner, Mr Gordon, comes to look at me. He looks at my eyes, my teeth, and my legs.</p>

<p>"When you break him in, I want to see him," says Mr Gordon.</p>

<p>"Very well, Mr Gordon," says my master.</p>

<p>He smiles at me and says, "Tomorrow, I must break you in. I must teach you to wear a saddle and bridle, and carry a person on your back. Then I must teach you to pull a carriage."</p>

<p>"Tomorrow is an important day," says mother, quietly. "Remember, you must always listen to your master, and never bite or kick. And you must never jump when you're happy, or stop when you're tired. Always be a good horse."</p>

<p>The next day, my master comes and talks to me quietly. Then he gently puts the bit into my mouth and the bridle on my face. The bit hurts my mouth and feels terrible, but my mother and other adult horses wear it too. Then my master puts a saddle on my back and says kind words to me. He slowly gets on the saddle and rides me around the meadow. I am happy when I carry him. After some time, he takes me to the blacksmith. The blacksmith puts on horse shoes. They are very heavy metal shoes, and I do not like them.</p>

<p>"Today we're going to visit my friend Simon," my master says.</p>

<p>At Simon's house, I stay in a meadow with cows and sheep. Suddenly, a big train goes by, and it makes a lot of noise and smoke. I run to the other side of the meadow because I am afraid, but the cows and sheep do not move. A lot of trains go by, but they do not hurt me, so I am not afraid of them.</p>

<p>Sometimes, I pull the carriage with my mother, and she teaches me a lot of things. "There are many kinds of masters," she says. "Some are good, but others are bad and cruel. You must always listen to them and remember the good name of your family."</p>

<p>In May, one of Mr Gordon's men comes and takes me away. My master says, "Goodbye, my friend. Be a good horse and always do your best." I cannot say goodbye, so I put my nose in his hand.</p>

<p>My new home at Mr Gordon's is lovely. There is a big stable, and I have a comfortable stall. There is another horse in the next stall.</p>

<p>"Hello, what's your name?" I ask.</p>

<p>"My name is Merrylegs," says the horse. "I carry the young ladies on my back. Are you my new neighbour?"</p>

<p>"Yes, I am. Do you bite?" asks Merrylegs.</p>

<p>"No, I don't," I answer. "I only bite grass."</p>

<p>"Oh, good. Because the horse in the other stall sometimes bites," says Merrylegs. "She's a brown horse, and her name is Ginger."</p>

<summary>Chapter 2</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter 2</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">The Bridge</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">My coachman's name is John Manley, and he lives near the stables. John is a friendly man, and he always gives me a good grooming. He is a good rider, too.</p>

<p>"Good morning, John," says Mr Gordon. "How's the new horse?"</p>

<p>"He's fast and easy to ride, sir," says John. "He comes from a good family."</p>

<p>"What's his name?" asks Mr Gordon.</p>

<p>"I don't know, sir," says John.</p>

<p>"Well, he's a beauty," says Mr Gordon. "Let's call him Black Beauty."</p>

<p>I like my new name and my new master.</p>

<p>After a few days, John says to me, "Today you're going out on a carriage with Ginger." I am not happy about this, because Ginger is not friendly. But after a few rides in the carriage, we start talking.</p>

<p>"Sometimes I bite and kick, but I'm not a bad horse," says Ginger, sadly. "In my life, I can only remember cruel masters, bad coachmen, and the whip."</p>

<p>"Oh, I'm sorry, Ginger," I say. "Are you happy here?"</p>

<p>"Yes, I am," says Ginger. "Because the master and the coachman are both kind. They understand horses." Now we are good friends, and I like her a lot.</p>

<p>They also like Merrylegs, because he is always happy and friendly. He is the favourite horse of Miss Jessie and Flora. They are Mr Gordon's daughters.</p>

<p>One cloudy autumn day, the master says, "John, get the carriage ready with Black Beauty. We must go to town on business." This is the first time for me to go to town with the master and John. I am excited. I want to do my best and please the master.</p>

<p>The master and John are in the carriage, and it starts raining, and it is very windy. We get to town, and the master goes to see some people. John and I wait for him in the rain. When he finishes his business, he says, "Let's go home, John. Perhaps we can cross the bridge before it is dark."</p>

<p>"It's a dangerous bridge when it rains," says John.</p>

<p>We take the country road to the bridge, and it is raining hard. I am very careful, because the road is bad. When my feet touch the bridge, I know something is wrong. I cannot move, and I stop.</p>

<p>"Go on, Black Beauty," says my master.</p>

<p>I cannot move, because I feel there is danger on the bridge.</p>

<p>"There's something wrong, sir," says John. "Beauty doesn't want to cross the bridge, but we must cross the bridge. It's late," says the master.</p>

<p>At that moment, a man runs out of his house and cries, "Stop, stop, go back, don't cross the bridge. It's broken in the middle."</p>

<p>"Broken in the middle?" says the master. "Oh, thank you, Black Beauty."</p>

<p>"Yes, thank you, Beauty," says John.</p>

<p>We take another country road and get home late.</p>

<p>"What a rainy night," says the mistress when she sees us. "I'm happy you're home."</p>

<p>"You can thank Black Beauty," says the master. "He's a very wise horse."</p>

<p>That night, John dries my wet coat and puts on my warm blue blanket. Then he gives me a good dinner, and I can finally sleep.</p>

<p>The next day, a new stable boy comes to work. His name is Joe Green, and he is 14 years old. I like him because he has a gentle hand, and he talks to me.</p>

<p>One night, the stable bell rings loudly, and all the horses wake up. I hear John's voice. "Beauty, wake up. The mistress is very ill, and we must go and find Dr White." He puts a saddle on my back and gets on. I gallop fast because I want to help the mistress. It is a dark, cold night.</p>

<p>When we get to Dr White's house, John calls him. A window opens, and the doctor puts his head out and says, "What's the matter at three o'clock in the morning?"</p>

<p>"Mrs Gordon is very ill," says John. "Please come at once."</p>

<p>"Can I use your horse?" asks the doctor. "My horse has a bad leg."</p>

<p>"Yes, of course," says John.</p>

<p>The doctor is a heavy man and not a good rider, but I do my best and gallop home. He runs to see the mistress, and Joe takes me to the stable. I am very wet and cold. Joe dries me and gives me some water and oats. But he forgets to put my warm blue blanket on. Soon I start shaking, and I am very cold. I feel terrible.</p>

<p>The next day the master comes to see me. "Thanks to you, Black Beauty, the mistress is feeling better," he says. "But now you are ill, my poor beauty. We must call the vet at once."</p>

<p>I am very ill now, but the master's kind words make me feel better.</p>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter 3</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Lord and Lady Waverley</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">My life at Mr Gordon's is happy, but things are changing. The mistress is ill, and the master is sad and worried. Dr White comes here often and says, "Mrs Gordon must go to a warm country for two or three years. Then perhaps she can get better." Everyone is sad, because we must soon say goodbye.</p>

<p>One day Jessie and Flora come to the stable. "Goodbye, dear Merrylegs," they say. "Thank you for all the wonderful rides." They hug him like an old friend. "Don't be sad, Merrylegs," says Jessie. "Father is selling you to the old vicar. He is a kind man and loves horses."</p>

<p>Then the master comes to the stable and talks to Ginger and me. "I am selling you to Lord Waverley," he says. "He's a good man and lives in the country. Goodbye, Black Beauty and Ginger." He pats us on the neck for a long time. I cannot say goodbye, so I put my nose in his hand.</p>

<p>Ginger and I are very sad to leave our master and our stable. But this is the life of a horse.</p>

<p>Lord Waverley has a beautiful house with a green meadow and big stables. John and our new coachman, Mr York, take us to our stalls. He looks at us carefully and says, "They look like very good horses, Mr Manley. What can you tell me about them?"</p>

<p>"Black Beauty is gentle and wise and always does his best," says John. "He is also very fast. Ginger's a good horse too, but sometimes she's nervous."</p>

<p>"Do they wear the bearing rein?" asks Mr York.</p>

<p>"No, they don't," says John.</p>

<p>"Well," says Mr York. "Here they must wear it. The Lord and I like a snaffle rein, but Lady Waverley wants the bearing rein. The horses must keep their heads high."</p>

<p>"Horses don't like it," says John. "It's very uncomfortable and it hurts their head and neck."</p>

<p>"You're right," says Mr York. "But the Lady isn't kind."</p>

<p>I do not know what a bearing rein is, but it sounds terrible.</p>

<p>The next day Ginger and I pull the Lady's carriage and we must wear it. It is very uncomfortable because we cannot put our heads down. It is a cruel thing for a horse.</p>

<p>"I hate the bearing rein," says Ginger one evening. "My head, neck and mouth hurt terribly. People don't wear it, so they can't understand."</p>

<p>"I hate it too," I say. "But what can a poor horse do?"</p>

<p>"We can do nothing, Beauty," says Ginger sadly.</p>

<p>We wear the bearing rein every day. The Lady comes down the stairs in a beautiful dress and says, "York, take me to the Duchess of Boston. And I want the horses' heads high, very high." She gets into the carriage and sits down. York prepares Ginger and me. He comes to me first and pulls the bearing rein. It feels terrible. Then he goes to Ginger and does the same. Ginger gets angry and starts kicking wildly. York falls to the ground and the Lady in the carriage screams. Two grooms come from the stable and take poor Ginger away. She does not ride with me anymore. The master uses her for the hunts because she is fast.</p>

<p>My new carriage partner is Max, a quiet grey horse. I think about my old home a lot. Here I have food and a clean stall, but I don't have a lot of friends. York is a good man, but he is not my friend.</p>

<div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ * ~ </span></div>

<p><strong>The Royal Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals.</strong> In the 1800s, animals are often mistreated by cruel owners and no one protects them. In 1824, Richard Martin opens the Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals in London. It is the first national animal protection society in the world. In 1840, Queen Victoria gives her permission to call it the Royal Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals, the RSPCA. People begin to understand that animals are living creatures and it is wrong to mistreat them. Today, the RSPCA is an important organisation with more than 400 inspectors. They visit homes, pet shops, farms and laboratories. They work with schools to teach kindness towards animals. They also work with the government to pass laws that protect animals. The RSPCA has animal hospitals and centres for abandoned animals all over England and Wales.</p>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter 4</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Lizzie</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">Lady Anne is a friend of Lord Waverley's family. She is young and beautiful and a perfect rider. I am her favourite horse and I like carrying her. She is kind to me and always brings me a carrot. She often rides with her friend, Lord Robert. He is a handsome young man with dark hair. He likes horses and often talks to me. He rides Lizzie, a young, nervous horse.</p>

<p>"What a lovely spring day," says Lord Robert. "Let's go riding, Lady Anne."</p>

<p>"All right," she says. "But I want to ride your horse today."</p>

<p>"Lizzie is a good horse, but she is too nervous for a lady," says Lord Robert.</p>

<p>"What nonsense," says Lady Anne. "Today I am riding Lizzie and you are riding Black Beauty."</p>

<p>Lord Robert is a good rider, but I prefer Lady Anne because she is small and light. We gallop across the meadows and woods and have a wonderful time. Then we stop at Dr Ashley's house.</p>

<p>"I must give a letter to the doctor," says Lord Robert.</p>

<p>"Lizzie and I can wait here with Black Beauty," says Lady Anne.</p>

<p>After a few minutes, some colts run by. Lizzie is nervous and she starts kicking. Lady Anne is afraid and suddenly Lizzie gallops away. I know Lizzie is dangerous, so I neigh loudly and Lord Robert hears me. He runs to me and jumps on my back.</p>

<p>"Let's go, Beauty," he cries. "We must help Lady Anne."</p>

<p>We follow Lizzie and Lady Anne. I gallop quickly because I want to help my mistress. Lizzie gallops up a hill and down into a valley. Lady Anne's green hat falls off and her long brown hair flies in the wind. Then Lizzie jumps across a small river and Lady Anne falls off.</p>

<p>"Oh no," cries Lord Robert.</p>

<p>I jump across the river and see my poor mistress on the grass. Her face is white and her eyes are shut. She does not move or speak. Is she dead?</p>

<p>"Lady Anne, can you hear me?" asks Lord Robert, but she does not answer.</p>

<p>A young farmer is walking by and says, "The poor lady! Can I help her?"</p>

<p>"Yes," says Lord Robert. "Please go to the village and come back with the doctor. You can ride my horse, Beauty. He's very fast."</p>

<p>The farmer gets on my back and I gallop to the village. He finds a doctor and we return to Lady Anne. She is still on the grass and the doctor examines her carefully.</p>

<p>"Don't worry, she's not dead," he says. "But we must take her home right now and she must rest."</p>

<p>I feel happy when I hear this because I like my young mistress.</p>

<p>That evening in the stable Ginger and I talk about Lizzie. Two days later Lord Robert comes to my stall and says, "Lady Anne is better now and she wants to ride you soon. You are an intelligent horse, Beauty, because you understand a lot of things."</p>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter 5</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Reuben Smith</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">When Mr York goes to London, Reuben Smith becomes the coachman. He is a good, honest man and he understands horses. But he has one big problem. He drinks a lot and he is often drunk.</p>

<p>One morning he comes to my stall and says, "We're going to town on business, Beauty." He puts my saddle on and we go. When we arrive in town, Smith leaves me at a stable.</p>

<p>"My horse is hungry and thirsty," says Smith to the stable boy. "I'm coming back at four o'clock."</p>

<p>The boy gives me oats and water and looks at my horse shoes too. "The nails in your front shoes are loose," he says to me. "You can't run with loose nails. I must tell Mr Smith."</p>

<p>But Smith does not come at four o'clock and he does not come at five o'clock. The boy is worried and I am too. At nine o'clock in the evening, Smith comes back. He is angry and his voice is loud. He cannot walk well.</p>

<p>"The front shoe of your horse has loose nails, sir," says the boy. "It's dangerous to ride a horse with loose nails."</p>

<p>Smith looks at him and shouts, "It's late. I'm going home."</p>

<p>It is a dark night and the moon is behind the clouds.</p>

<p>"Come on, Beauty. Gallop!" he cries and hits me with his whip. I gallop fast but my front shoe is loose. I feel the whip again. "Gallop, Beauty!" cries Smith again.</p>

<p>At the bridge my front shoe falls off. My foot hurts a lot. There are a lot of sharp stones on the road. I try to gallop but I can't. At last I fall heavily on my knees and Smith falls off. I get up slowly and look at Smith. He is not moving. What can I do?</p>

<p>Soon I hear Ginger coming. I neigh loudly and she answers. Then I hear men's voices and they stop in front of Smith.</p>

<p>"It's Reuben," says one man.</p>

<p>"He's dead," says another man.</p>

<p>"Dead?"</p>

<p>"Yes," says the first man sadly. "Poor Reuben Smith. Look at his head." Then the man looks at me. "Look at this poor horse. It's Black Beauty. His knees are badly hurt."</p>

<p>The men slowly take me back to the stable but I cannot sleep. The next day the vet and the blacksmith come to see me.</p>

<p>"Loose horse must rest for a few months," says the vet.</p>

<p>So now I stay in the green meadow with its sweet grass. But I am worried about the future because my knees are not perfect.</p>

<p>After a month Ginger comes to the meadow too because she is very tired. I like talking to my old friend under the apple tree.</p>

<p>One summer day Lord Waverley and York come to the meadow. They look at my knees.</p>

<p>"I'm sorry, York, but I must sell Black Beauty," says the Lord. "His knees are not perfect anymore. I can't keep a horse with those knees in my stables. I'm very sorry because I like Beauty. He's a wonderful horse but his knees..."</p>

<p>When they go away Ginger says sadly, "You're the only friend I've got and soon you must leave. What a difficult life."</p>

<p>I look at her and say, "Ginger, you're my true friend."</p>

<p>The Lord sells me to the town livery stables. It is not a nice place. Anyone can hire a horse and carriage at the livery stables. Most of the drivers are bad and know nothing about horses. And of course they know nothing about driving a carriage. They make a lot of mistakes and then get angry with the horse and use the whip. In the evening I cannot talk about my day because I do not know the other horses. I am very lonely and think about Ginger.</p>

<summary>Chapter 6</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter 6</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">A London Cab Horse</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">After a year at the livery stables, the master takes me to a horse fair. He wants to sell me. A horse fair is an interesting place. Some of the horses at the fair are young and handsome. But most of them are old and tired. Their eyes are sad and they are thin and hungry. I am still a strong horse but my knees are not perfect anymore. People come to look at me and they like me. But when they see my knees, they go away.</p>

<p>At the end of the day, a tall thin man comes and looks at me. Then he talks to my master.</p>

<p>"I'm looking for a gentle horse," he says. "I want to ride him in the fresh air every morning. This black horse is perfect," says my master. "He's very gentle."</p>

<p>My new master is Mr Barry and he lives in Bath. He knows nothing about horses but he is a kind man. He buys a lot of food but the stable boy steals it and takes it home. He is dishonest and lazy. He never cleans my feet or my stall. Mr Barry knows nothing about this. One day Mr Barry's friend says, "Your horse is very thin. What's the matter with him?"</p>

<p>"I don't know," says Mr Barry. "I always buy a lot of food for him."</p>

<p>"Go and see what's happening in the stable," says his friend.</p>

<p>My master discovers that the stable boy steals my food and my stall is very dirty. He is angry and calls the police. They take the stable boy to prison for two months. Then Mr Barry comes to my stall and looks at me sadly. "I'm sorry, Beauty," he says. "Let's sell you at the next horse fair. I'm not lucky with horses." I am sorry too because I do not like changing masters.</p>

<p>At the next horse fair a short man with kind grey eyes comes to look at me. He pats my neck gently and smiles.</p>

<p>"I like this horse," he says to the man. "I can pay twenty-four pounds for him."</p>

<p>"Very well. The horse is yours," says the man.</p>

<p>My new master is a cab driver and lives in London. His name is Jerry Barker and his wife's name is Polly. They have got two children, Dolly and Harry. They are a very happy family and they like me. Captain is Jerry's other horse. He is a big white horse and he seems friendly.</p>

<p>Our cab stand is in the centre of London and there are many horses. Jerry is an excellent driver and he never uses the whip or the bearing rein. Our stable is clean and we are never hungry. I am lucky to have a kind master. Sunday is the best day because I can rest all day and talk to Captain. He is an old military horse and tells me exciting stories about different wars. The life of a cab horse is difficult. London is a big city with a lot of noise, people, carriages and cabs. The weather is often wet and cold. At the end of the day I am very tired and my knees hurt. Captain has the same problem. In the winter Dolly often brings her father a hot lunch. He eats it at the cab stand. Jerry's life is difficult too.</p>

<p>One day our cab is waiting outside a park. Another cab stops next to us. An old, tired brown horse is pulling the cab. The poor horse has a long, thin neck and her eyes are almost shut. I can see her bones through her dirty coat. She looks at me with her sad eyes and says, "Is that you, Black Beauty?"</p>

<p>"Ginger, my old friend," I answer.</p>

<p>"Yes, it's me, Black Beauty. It's good to see you," says Ginger. "I feel terrible. I am old, weak, hungry and ill. I can't run or gallop anymore. I must pull this heavy cab for my cruel master and I eat very little. I work all week and never have a day of rest. Every bone in my old body hurts."</p>

<p>"How terrible," I say. I put my nose near hers. "Dear Ginger, I'm very sorry."</p>

<p>A week later I see a cart with a dead horse in it. It is Ginger. I feel very sad when I see her body in the cart. Why are people cruel to animals? They do not understand us. We only want some love and kindness.</p>

<div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ * ~ </span></div>

<p><strong>Horses.</strong> Horses are beautiful animals. They are intelligent and friendly. And they learn quickly too. Their favourite foods are oats, carrots and apples. They do not eat meat. Horses love conversation. You can talk to a horse and he listens to you happily.</p>

<p>There are three types of horses: light, heavy and ponies. Each kind of horse has a lot of breeds. Breeds are groups of horses with different colours and sizes.</p>

<p><strong>One, light horses.</strong> They have small bones and thin legs. And they weigh about 650 kilograms. Saddle horses are light horses. People use them for riding. Some important breeds of saddle horses are thoroughbred horses, American saddle horses and Arabian stallions.</p>

<p><strong>Quarter horses.</strong> They are fast, strong and intelligent. They work well with other animals.</p>

<p><strong>The Lipizzana horses.</strong> They are beautiful show horses. They have a white coat and strong legs. These horses are part of the Spanish Riding School in Vienna, Austria. It is a very famous riding school. You can visit it and watch the Lipizzana horses dance to music.</p>

<p><strong>Two, heavy horses.</strong> They have big bones and big legs. And they weigh around 1,300 kilograms. Draft horses are very tall, heavy and strong. People use them for heavy work and in shows. Some important breeds of draft horses are Clydesdale horses, Shire horses and Percheron horses.</p>

<p><strong>Three, ponies.</strong> They are small, short horses and they weigh around 400 kilograms. Ponies are gentle. They are good horses for young riders. They are strong, clever animals. Some important breeds of ponies are Shetland ponies, Welsh ponies and Dartmoor ponies.</p>

<p><strong>Horses at work.</strong> Today cars, buses, trains and aeroplanes take people everywhere. But horses are still part of our lives. We ride horses for fun and sport. We watch them perform at the circus. People go to the horse races and watch their favourite horse. Cowboys ride bucking broncos at rodeos in the United States. Policemen and policewomen ride horses in big cities like New York City. Cowboys in North American ranches ride quarter horses when they work with cattle. A ride in a horse and carriage is a big tourist attraction in some towns and cities. In poor countries, people use horses to work on farms and to travel. In some North European countries, horses and ponies pull sledges in the snow.</p>

<summary>Chapter 7</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter 7</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">My Last Home</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">Christmas and New Year's Eve are happy times for some people but not for cab drivers and their horses. During that time, we work hard and stay out late. Often, we must wait for hours in the rain or snow while people are at parties.</p>

<p>On New Year's Eve, Jerry and I take two gentlemen to a party in a big house.</p>

<p>"Come back for us at eleven o'clock and don't be late," says one of the gentlemen.</p>

<p>At eleven o'clock, we are in front of the big house but the gentlemen are not there. It is a very cold night and it is snowing.</p>

<p>"The church bell says it's midnight," says Jerry, "but the gentlemen are not here." We continue to wait in the cold. At a quarter past one, the door of the big house opens and the two gentlemen come out. They do not say a word to Jerry and we take them home.</p>

<p>When we get home, Jerry cannot speak and he coughs a lot. He dries me and puts a warm blanket on my back.</p>

<p>The next morning, Harry comes to give me oats and water. He pats my neck gently and says, "My father is very ill. He can't drive his cab anymore. He must find another job. We must sell you to another cab driver. I'm very sorry, dear old friend." Dolly comes and kisses me. Then she starts crying. "Goodbye, old friend."</p>

<p>I am very sad to leave Jerry and his family.</p>

<p>My new master is Nicholas Skinner. He has black eyes and black hair and a loud voice. He drives an old cab in a bad part of London. He often whips my back and my head. He is a cruel master, but I always do my best. I eat little and work twelve hours a day, seven days a week. There is no time to rest or to talk to other horses. My life is very unhappy.</p>

<p>One morning, we go to the railway station and pick up a lady, a man, a young girl and a little boy. They have a lot of heavy luggage. Skinner puts all the luggage in the cab. The young girl comes and looks at me.</p>

<p>"Father," she says, "this poor horse can't take us and all our luggage. He is weak and thin. Please come and look at him."</p>

<p>"What nonsense?" says the man. "Get in the cab at once and be quiet."</p>

<p>I start to pull the heavy cab to Ludgate Hill. Then I try to climb the hill, but I feel weak. Suddenly, I fall to the ground. I try to get up, but I cannot.</p>

<p>"Poor horse!" cries the young girl. "We are cruel."</p>

<p>A young man throws some cold water on my head, and I slowly get up. Skinner takes me back to the stables and says, "You're working very hard. Rest for a week, and then I must sell you at the horse fair. You're not a good horse for my cab."</p>

<p>The horses at the fair are old and tired, and the people are not rich gentlemen. They are farmers. A farmer and his grandson stop in front of me.</p>

<p>"Look at this horse, Willie," says the farmer. "He is tired and thin, but he is handsome. He comes from a good family. Look at his neck and ears." The young boy pats my face, and I put my nose in his hand.</p>

<p>"Grandfather," says Willie. "He likes me."</p>

<p>"Do you like him?" asks the grandfather.</p>

<p>"Yes, I like him a lot," says the young boy happily.</p>

<p>The grandfather looks at my teeth and my legs. "Well, I'm buying him for you, Willie," says the grandfather. "You must look after him. You must feed him and groom him every day. You can make him feel better. He is your responsibility."</p>

<p>"Oh, thank you, grandfather," says Willie, kissing him. "I promise to look after him."</p>

<p>Willie and Mr Thoroughgood take me to their stables. I have a big, clean stall with lots of oats and fresh water. There is a small meadow outside where I can eat grass and enjoy the sun. Young Willie is a perfect master.</p>

<p>"Now you must rest, Beauty," says Willie. "Soon you can run in the meadow with the other horses."</p>

<p>Mr Thoroughgood works on the farm and sometimes he comes to see me. He looks at my legs and my teeth. Then he talks to me and gives me some sugar.</p>

<p>"Black Beauty is feeling better," says Mr Thoroughgood to Willie. "His legs and knees are strong now. You're doing a good job with this horse."</p>

<p>Willie smiles and says, "Thank you, grandfather. I love Black Beauty."</p>

<p>"I do too," says Mr Thoroughgood. "He's a wonderful horse."</p>

<p>The Blomefield sisters are friends of Mr Thoroughgood and they are looking for a good horse.</p>

<p>"Black Beauty is feeling fine now," says Mr Thoroughgood. "Let's take him to the Blomefield sisters and see what they say."</p>

<p>"Yes, grandfather," says Willie. "What a good idea."</p>

<p>One summer day, Willie and Mr Thoroughgood take me to visit their friends. Ellen Blomefield and her two sisters are happy when they see me.</p>

<p>"This horse has a good face and he's very handsome," says Ellen. "I like him. Can I try him for a week?"</p>

<p>"Yes, of course," says Mr Thoroughgood happily.</p>

<p>A stable boy takes me to a big stable and starts grooming me. Every morning he comes to my stall and pats me and talks to me.</p>

<p>"Good morning, Beauty," he says. "What a lovely day. Then go to the meadow today." He grooms me and gives me an apple. Then he takes me to the meadow. I like the tall trees and the beautiful flowers. I feel better every day.</p>

<p>"This is the same star Black Beauty has," says the young man, looking at the white star on my face. "And this is the same white foot too." He looks at my back and sees a little white spot. "Oh, this is Black Beauty," he cries. "Black Beauty. Do you remember me? I'm Joe Green, the stable boy at Mr Gordon's."</p>

<p>I put my nose in his hand. Yes, it is Joe Green. He is very happy and tells the Blomefield sisters about me. They are happy too and they want to keep me forever. Now I am part of the Blomefield family. Ellen rides me almost every day and I am her favourite horse. Sometimes I take her sisters to town in the carriage. My good friends Mr Thoroughgood and Willie often come to visit me. I am finally happy in this wonderful home because everyone loves me and I love them.</p>
