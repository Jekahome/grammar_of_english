<summary>Chapter One</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter One</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">The Young Woman</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">In a town in England, there was a building often found in most towns in England. It was a workhouse. There Oliver Twist was born.</p>

<p>The doctor and an old nurse looked at him. They were not sure if he would live or die. However, at last he began to cry, like any other baby.</p>

<p>"Let me see my baby," said the young woman, who was Oliver's mother. She held the baby in her arms and kissed him, then she lay back and died.</p>

<p>She was a beautiful girl. "Where did she come from?" asked the doctor.</p>

<p>"She was brought here last night," answered the old woman. "She was found in the street. She was very tired and her shoes were broken after walking for a long time. Nobody knows where she came from or where she was going to."</p>

<p>The doctor lifted the left hand of the young woman. "The old story," he said, shaking his head. "No wedding ring, I see. The young woman must have run away from home. She could not tell her family that she was going to have a baby because she was not married."</p>

<p>Oliver was now an orphan. He was crying loudly while the old nurse was raising him in the closet of the workhouse.</p>

<p>When Oliver Twist was ten months old, he was sent to a smaller workhouse. There were twenty or thirty other young orphans lying on the floor. They did not have many warm clothes or much food to eat.</p>

<p>An old woman called Mrs. Mann was given seven-and-a-half pence each week to look after each child. Unfortunately, she took most of the money and spent very little on the children. As a result of this, Oliver Twist was thin and weak. He was also rather small.</p>

<p>On his ninth birthday, Oliver and two other boys told Mrs. Mann that they were hungry. She locked them in the cellar as punishment. While they were there, Mr. Bumble, the master of the workhouse, arrived to see Mrs. Mann.</p>

<p>"Goodness! Is that you, Mr. Bumble, sir?" she said, looking out of the window. "How happy I am to see you, Mr. Bumble."</p>

<p>Mr. Bumble was very fat and he walked slowly towards the front door. "Come in, sir," said Mrs. Mann. "I told the dear children that you would visit them today."</p>

<p>"Well, well, Mrs. Mann, let's go inside," answered Mr. Bumble. "I am here on business and I have something important to say."</p>

<p>Mrs. Mann took Mr. Bumble into her small kitchen and gave him a drink of gin. "And now about business," said Mr. Bumble. "The child that we called Oliver Twist is nine years old today. We still do not know where his mother came from, or who his father is."</p>

<p>"Why is he called Oliver Twist?" asked Mrs. Mann.</p>

<p>"I decided to call him that. We named the orphans in alphabetical order. The one before him was S. This one was T. So I named him Twist," replied Mr. Bumble.</p>

<p>"You are very clever, Mr. Bumble," said Mrs. Mann.</p>

<p>"Well, well, perhaps I am," replied Mr. Bumble. "Oliver is too old to stay here now, so I am going to take him back to the large workhouse. Let me see him at once."</p>

<p>Mrs. Mann took Oliver into the room. "Will you come with me, Oliver?" asked Mr. Bumble. Oliver was going to say that he would be happy to leave when he saw Mrs. Mann looking at him angrily. Oliver tried to look sad. Mrs. Mann kissed him goodbye.</p>

<p>Mr. Bumble took him to see the owners of the workhouse. "Do you know that you are an orphan?" asked the fattest gentleman. "What is that, sir?" asked Oliver. "The boy is very stupid," said another gentleman. "You will begin to work tomorrow morning at six o'clock."</p>

<p>Life in the workhouse was very hard. The children had to work and were given three bowls of thin porridge every day. Oliver and the other boys were always very hungry.</p>

<p>One day, a tall boy said that if he was not given more porridge, he would eat the boy who slept next to him. The other boys were frightened. They decided that one of them should ask the master for more. Oliver was chosen.</p>

<p>In the evening, Oliver got up from the table. "Please, sir, I want some more," he said. "What?" said the master angrily and hit Oliver on the head with his ladle.</p>

<p>When Mr. Bumble heard this, he said to the owner, "That boy will be hanged one day." The next day, a notice was put outside the door. It said that five pounds would be given to anyone who would take Oliver away.</p>

<p>For the next week, Oliver was locked in a dark room. This was because he had asked for more porridge. At night, he put his hands over his eyes because he was afraid of the dark. Every evening, Oliver was taken to the large hall and beaten while the other boys watched.</p>

<p>One morning, a chimney sweep called Mr. Gamfield saw the notice. He needed a boy to climb up inside chimneys. On the way to the magistrate, Mr. Bumble told Oliver to look very happy and say that he would like to be a chimney sweep. But Oliver said that he did not want to go. "Be quiet," said the magistrate. "I will not sign these documents."</p>

<p>The next day, a notice was put outside the door again. It said that five pounds would be given to anyone who would take Oliver away.</p>

<summary>Chapter Two</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Two</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">The Undertaker</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">A few days later, Mr. Bumble was returning to the workhouse when he met a tall, thin man called Mr. Sowerberry, who was an undertaker. "I have measured the two women who died last night," said Mr. Sowerberry. "The prices for the coffins are very small, and the wood is expensive, sir."</p>

<p>"Do you know anyone who wants a boy?" asked Mr. Bumble, pointing to the notice about Oliver. "Yes," said Mr. Sowerberry. "I think I will take the boy to work for me."</p>

<p>That evening, Mr. Bumble took Oliver to Mr. Sowerberry's shop. "He is very small, but he will grow now with food and drink," said Mrs. Sowerberry. She took Oliver downstairs into the kitchen and gave him some old pieces of meat left for the dog. Oliver ate the meat quickly and hungrily. "You will sleep in here, with the coffins," she said.</p>

<p>That night, Oliver was left to sleep in the undertaker's shop. A coffin stood in the middle of the room. When Oliver saw it, he was afraid. He was thinking about dead people and ghosts as he fell asleep.</p>

<p>In the morning, Oliver heard someone knocking loudly at the front door. He slowly opened the door and saw an old boy sitting on a wall, eating bread and butter. "Do you want to buy a coffin, sir?" asked Oliver. "Don't you know who I am?" said the boy. "I am Mr. Noah Claypole, and you work under me."</p>

<p>One evening Mr. Sowerberry said that Oliver looked sad and would be good at children's funerals. "Well, Oliver, how did you like it?" "Not very much, sir," replied Oliver. "You'll get used to it in time," said Mr. Sowerberry.</p>

<p>One day, Oliver and Noah were alone in the kitchen. Noah was jealous of Oliver and began to make fun of him. "You know your mother was a bad woman," said Noah. "It's a good thing she died, because now she would be in prison."</p>

<p>Oliver jumped up, grabbed Noah by the throat, and knocked him to the ground. "Help! He's murdering me!" shouted Noah. Charlotte screamed and Mrs. Sowerberry ran into the kitchen. They both grabbed Oliver and hit him. Noah got up and began to hit Oliver too. Then they took him to the cellar.</p>

<p>"What shall we do with him now?" asked Mr. Sowerberry. "Run to Mr. Bumble, Noah, and tell him to come here immediately." Mr. Bumble came and they pulled Oliver out. Oliver was not afraid. He looked at Noah and said, "He called my mother names." "She deserved it," said Mr. Sowerberry. "No, she didn't. It's a lie," said Oliver. Mr. Sowerberry beat Oliver and Mr. Bumble hit him with his stick. Then they locked him in the room with the coffin.</p>

<p>As Oliver sat there, he thought about running away. He slowly got up, carefully unlocked the door and opened it. The cold, dark night was outside. Oliver was afraid of the dark. He decided to wait until morning. When it was light, Oliver got up, opened the door and went outside into the street.</p>

<summary>Chapter Three</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Three</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">To London</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">Oliver didn't know where to go. He walked on until he came to the small workhouse for young boys. He saw a friend there, working in the garden. "Hello, Dick," Oliver said quietly. "You mustn't say that you saw me. I'm running away." "Goodbye and God bless you," said Dick. Those were the first kind words Oliver had in his life.</p>

<p>Oliver walked until he was nearly five miles away. He sat down to rest and saw a sign. It said, "London, seventy miles." He continued walking for seven days, and kind people gave him some bread and water. Eventually he came to Barnet, a small town not far from London.</p>

<p>Suddenly a boy appeared in strange clothes. He had a long coat and a large hat. "Hello. What's wrong with you? You look very hungry. Come with me, and I'll give you something to eat. My name is Jack Dawkins, but my friends call me the Artful Dodger."</p>

<p>Dodger took Oliver to an ugly and dirty part of the city. He opened the door of a house and pulled Oliver inside. The room was very dirty. An old man with long red hair was standing near the fire, cooking sausages. There were silk handkerchiefs on a line beside him. Four or five boys were sitting at the table, smoking pipes and drinking gin.</p>

<p>"This is my friend, Oliver Twist," said Dodger. The old man smiled at Oliver, shook his hand, and said that his name was Fagin. "They are very happy to see Oliver," said Fagin. Then they all sat down to eat. Fagin gave Oliver a drink of gin, and soon he felt very tired.</p>

<p>The next morning, Oliver saw Fagin opening a secret hole in the floor. He took out a small box with a gold watch and other jewelry. Suddenly he saw Oliver watching him. "Did you see any of these pretty things? They are mine, Oliver. They are all I have for when I am old."</p>

<p>Just then the Dodger returned with another boy called Charlie Bates. "What did you get?" said Fagin. "Four handkerchiefs," said Charlie. "Very good. Teach Oliver how to pick out the labels with a needle," said Fagin.</p>

<p>After breakfast, Charlie and Dodger played a strange game with Fagin. The old man put a watch, a wallet, and some handkerchiefs into his pockets and walked up and down the room. Suddenly the Dodger stepped on his foot and Charlie pushed him, and the two boys quickly took all the things from Fagin's pockets. Oliver enjoyed watching the game very much.</p>

<summary>Chapter Four</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Four</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">The Theft</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">The next morning, they left the house and began to walk slowly on the streets. The Dodger and Charlie walked so slowly that Oliver began to think that they did not want to go to work at all.</p>

<p>Suddenly the Dodger stopped. "Follow me," he said. The boys quickly crossed the road and began to follow an old gentleman who was looking at a book. The Dodger put his hand into the old gentleman's pocket and took out a handkerchief. Then the Dodger and Charlie ran away.</p>

<p>Oliver stood on the pavement, looking at the old gentleman. Then the old gentleman realized that he did not have his handkerchief. Oliver was afraid. He decided to run away too. "Stop, thief!" shouted the old man. Other people began to chase Oliver. At last a man caught him. Oliver lay on the ground, covered with mud and dust, his mouth bleeding.</p>

<p>"Poor boy," said the old gentleman. "He's hurt." A policeman arrived. "Come on, get up," he said. He took Oliver to the police station. The old gentleman's name was Mr. Brownlow. He told the magistrate that he was not sure that Oliver was the thief. Oliver looked very pale and felt very sick.</p>

<p>At that moment, the owner of the bookshop arrived. "I saw everything. It wasn't this boy who stole the handkerchief, it was another boy." "Now everybody out!" shouted the magistrate. Oliver was carried outside. "Call a coach!" said Mr. Brownlow. The bookshop owner got into the coach and they drove to Mr. Brownlow's house.</p>

<p>A short time later, they arrived at a neat house on a quiet street. Oliver had a fever and for many days lay asleep in bed. An old lady, Mrs. Bedwin, sat near his bed and watched him. When he finally awoke, he saw a picture on the wall of a lady's face. "She is so pretty," said Oliver.</p>

<p>Mr. Brownlow came in. "How do you feel, Tom White?" "My name is Oliver Twist, sir," answered Oliver. Suddenly Mr. Brownlow exclaimed, looking at the picture. "Mrs. Bedwin, look here! The boy's face looks like the face in the picture. The eyes, the mouth, and the head are the same." Oliver fainted.</p>

<p>Everybody was kind to Oliver, and soon he became well. One evening Mr. Brownlow asked Oliver to tell him about his life. But just then his friend, Mr. Grimwig, arrived. When Oliver left the room, Mr. Grimwig said that Oliver would never tell the truth about his life.</p>

<p>At that moment, some books arrived from the bookshop. "Let me take them back for you, sir," said Oliver. Mr. Brownlow gave him five pounds. "I'll be back in twenty minutes," said Oliver. "You expect him to come back?" asked Mr. Grimwig. "The boy has new clothes, new shoes, valuable books, and five pounds in his pocket. He'll return to his old friends and laugh at you." The two men sat looking at the clock, waiting for Oliver to return.</p>

<summary>Chapter Five</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Five</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Nancy</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">When the Dodger and Charlie returned to Fagin's house, they told Fagin about Oliver's arrest. "I'm afraid he may say something about us to the police," said Fagin. They decided to send Nancy to the magistrate's office. She learned that Oliver was at Mr. Brownlow's house.</p>

<p>Oliver was walking to the bookshop when suddenly Nancy saw him. "Oh, my dear little brother, where have you been?" she shouted. She grabbed Oliver. Fagin's companions forced him into a dark street. Nobody heard his cries.</p>

<p>At Mr. Brownlow's house, Mrs. Bedwin, Mr. Brownlow, and Mr. Grimwig wondered why Oliver had not returned. Nancy took Oliver to Fagin's house. They took his new clothes and shoes and gave him old ones. The Artful Dodger found the five pounds in Oliver's pocket. Then they decided to sell the books. Oliver fell down at Fagin's feet. "No, they belong to the old gentleman," cried Oliver. "Please send him back the books and the money."</p>

<p>Fagin picked up a stick and hit Oliver. Oliver ran towards the door, but they caught him. Nancy ran forward and grabbed the stick from Fagin's hand. "Leave him alone!" she cried. "Leave him alone, or I'll kill you! I was wrong to help you bring him." She struggled, but Fagin grabbed her and she could not break free.</p>

<p>For the next few days, Oliver was kept prisoner. One dark night, Fagin went to visit Bill Sikes, a terrible thief. "When are you going to break into the house at Chertsey, Bill?" asked Fagin. "Can you give me a small boy to help us?" "Oliver will help you. He's just the right size," said Fagin. "If he doesn't, I'll kill him," replied Bill.</p>

<p>That night Nancy took Oliver to Bill's house. Nancy showed Oliver some marks on her arms and neck made by Bill. It was a dark, wet night. The house was dark and looked empty. The two robbers held Oliver by each hand and went out. They stopped in front of a large house.</p>

<p>Bill and Toby forced a small window open. "Now listen," Sikes said. "You are going to climb through the window. Then you must go to the front door and unlock it." They lifted Oliver up and pushed him through. Oliver decided to run upstairs to warn the family. But a noise came from upstairs. A light came on. Two men appeared. One of them fired a gun. Oliver fell down with a cry. Sikes pulled him back through the window. Oliver was wounded. Sikes and Toby left him on the ground and escaped over the wall.</p>

<summary>Chapter Six</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Six</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">The Maylies</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">The two servants from the house ran after the robbers. They were afraid to go any further and returned to the house. Oliver was lying unconscious on the ground in the rain. He lay there for several hours. At last he awoke. He could not move his left arm. He got up slowly and walked towards the house. He knocked on the door and fell down on the doorstep.</p>

<p>A young woman appeared at the top of the stairs. "Is the poor boy badly hurt?" she asked. "He might die, Miss," said a servant. The young woman told the men to bring Oliver upstairs and find a doctor. They carried Oliver to her bedroom.</p>

<p>At Fagin's house, Toby Crackit arrived. "We didn't get the silver," he said. "They fired a gun and hit Oliver. We left him lying on the ground. I don't know if he is alive or dead." Fagin ran to a bar where criminals met. "Tell Monks that he must come to me tonight," said Fagin. Monks came, and Fagin told him about the robbery. "If he is dead, it is not my fault," said Monks. Just then Monks saw a woman's shadow on the wall, but they found no one there.</p>

<p>At the house in Chertsey, the doctor, Dr. Losberne, arrived. He went upstairs to look at Oliver. Mrs. Maylie and Miss Rose expected to see an evil criminal. Instead they saw only a child sleeping with a bandage across his chest. Several hours later, Oliver woke and was able to tell them his story.</p>

<p>Oliver's arm was very painful, and he had a fever. He lay in bed for several weeks. One day Miss Rose asked Oliver, "Would you like to work for us as a servant? The peace and quiet of the countryside will help you recover." "I would be very happy to work for you," said Oliver.</p>

<p>Two weeks later, the Maylies and Oliver moved to a summer cottage in the countryside. Oliver began to enjoy a new life there. Miss Rose taught him to read and to write. He went for walks and listened to her play the piano in the evenings. This happy life went on for three months, and Oliver felt like one of the family.</p>

<summary>Chapter Seven</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Seven</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Monks</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">One day Mr. Bumble went to a bar. A tall, dark stranger spoke to him. "Twelve years ago, a boy was born in the workhouse. He ran away to London. You mean Oliver Twist," said Mr. Bumble. "I want to know who looked after his mother," said the stranger. "My wife knows something important," said Mr. Bumble. "Meet me tomorrow evening at the old factory," said the stranger. "What is your name?" "Monks."</p>

<p>Mr. and Mrs. Bumble went to the old factory. Monks let them in. "You were the old woman who looked after Oliver's mother on the night she died," said Monks. "Yes," she answered. Monks counted out twenty-five pounds. "Now tell me the story." "Oliver's mother gave old Sally some jewelry and a document. She asked her to give everything to the boy."</p>

<p>"Where is this jewelry now?" asked Monks. "Here," answered Mrs. Bumble. She opened a small bag. It contained a gold wedding ring, a gold chain and two locks of hair. Monks suddenly opened a trap door in the floor. Mr. Bumble stepped back quickly. Monks put the jewelry back into the bag, tied it to a piece of metal, and dropped it through the door into the river. "Now there is nothing that can be used against us. Get away from here," said Monks.</p>

<p>One night, Nancy hid and listened as Fagin and Monks talked. She heard Monks say that Oliver was his younger brother and that he wanted to destroy all proof. The next day, Sikes noticed that Nancy looked strange. He fell asleep, and Nancy quietly left the house. She ran to a small hotel where she knew Rose Maylie was staying.</p>

<p>"My life is in your hands," Nancy said to Rose. "I came to tell you about Oliver. Do you know a man called Monks? He knows who Oliver is. He promised Fagin a large sum of money if Oliver is made a thief. Last night, I heard Monks say that the only proof of the boy's identity was at the bottom of the river. Monks said he didn't want a younger brother."</p>

<p>"What can I do?" asked Rose. "Do you promise to meet me alone? Every Sunday night, from eleven until midnight, I will be on London Bridge," said Nancy. Then she left.</p>

<p>The next morning, Oliver told Rose that he had seen Mr. Brownlow in the street. Five minutes later, they were in a coach on their way to his house. Rose told Mr. Brownlow everything. "Thank God," cried Mr. Brownlow. "Where is he now?" "He is in the coach outside." Mr. Brownlow hurried down the stairs to see Oliver. "I knew you would come back to me," cried Mrs. Bedwin, holding him in her arms.</p>

<summary>Chapter Eight</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Eight</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">The Death of Nancy</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">On Sunday evening, Nancy waited until eleven o'clock. "I want to walk in the street," she said. "You cannot. Sit down," said Sikes angrily. "Please let me go," she cried, falling on her knees. Sikes pulled her into another room and threw her into a chair. She stayed there until twelve o'clock. Fagin secretly thought Nancy wanted to meet another man. He decided to have one of the boys follow her.</p>

<p>The following Sunday, Nancy went to London Bridge. Rose Maylie and Mr. Brownlow met her there. Nancy told them everything she knew about Monks. "We can send you to a quiet place," said Mr. Brownlow. "I cannot leave my own life. Bill needs me," said Nancy. A boy who had followed Nancy heard everything and ran back to Fagin's house.</p>

<p>Fagin told Sikes what Nancy had done. Sikes jumped up. "I'll kill her!" He ran home, locked the door, and grabbed Nancy by the head and throat. He hit her hard on the face. She fell down, and blood flowed from her head. He picked up a heavy stick, covered his eyes, and hit her again.</p>

<p>The sun shone into the room where Nancy lay dead. Sikes sat looking at her body. He threw the stick into the fire and tried to wash the blood from his hands. He cleaned his clothes and his dog's feet. Then he left the house, taking the dog with him. He walked away from London as far as he could. After two lonely days, he decided to return to London to get money from Fagin to go to France.</p>

<p>But he thought about the dog. If someone saw a man with the dog, they might know who he was. He decided to drown the dog. He picked up a large stone to tie to its neck. "Come here," he called. But the dog knew that something was wrong. It moved away from him. "Come back!" shouted Sikes. The dog came back a little, then turned and ran away as fast as possible. Sikes called again, but the dog did not come back.</p>

<summary>Chapter Nine</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Nine</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">The End</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">Mr. Brownlow found Monks and brought him to his house. "I was your father's oldest friend," said Mr. Brownlow. "Do you know you have a brother? After your parents left each other, your father fell in love with a young woman. He was going to marry her, but he died without leaving a will. All his money was left to your mother and you. But before he left, he gave me a picture of the young woman. Oliver looked like that picture. Now I know that Oliver is your brother. You got his mother's jewelry and threw it into the river so that nobody would know. Now you want to hurt Oliver." Monks agreed to sign a document saying that Oliver was his brother.</p>

<p>In the poorest part of London, three men talked about Fagin. "He was arrested at lunchtime," said one. Suddenly, Bill Sikes's dog came into the room. It lay on the floor and fell asleep. Three hours later, Sikes knocked at the door. "Is it true that Fagin is caught?" he asked. Then they heard horses and footsteps. A crowd of people was looking for Sikes. "I'll give fifty pounds to the person who catches him alive!" cried an old gentleman. The crowd broke down the front door and ran up the stairs. Sikes tried to get down from the roof, but he slipped and fell. He was dead.</p>

<p>Days later, Oliver travelled with Mr. Brownlow, Mrs. Maylie, Rose, and the others to the town where he was born. Monks was waiting for them. Mr. Brownlow told Oliver that Monks was his half-brother. Mr. and Mrs. Bumble were brought in. Mr. Brownlow asked them about Oliver's mother, Agnes. Two old women from the workhouse saw Mrs. Bumble take the jewelry from old Sally. "Yes, I sold it to Monks," cried Mrs. Bumble. "But you will never get it. It is at the bottom of the river."</p>

<p>Mr. Brownlow turned to Rose. "I have documents that prove you are the youngest sister of Agnes. You are Oliver's aunt." "I want to call you my sister," cried Oliver, taking her in his arms. "My aunt, whom I loved from the moment we met."</p>

<p>The courtroom was full of people waiting to see what would happen to Fagin. The jury returned with a single word: "Guilty." Fagin was taken back to the prison cell to await his hanging. Mr. Brownlow and Oliver went to see him. Fagin whispered that the papers were hidden in a hole in the chimney.</p>

<p>The story is nearly finished. Rose was married soon after. Monks and Oliver were given three thousand pounds each. Monks left England, spent all the money, returned to a life of crime, and eventually died in prison. Oliver went to live with Mr. Brownlow as his son, near Rose and Mrs. Maylie, where he could see his dear aunt as much as possible. Mr. and Mrs. Bumble lost their jobs and had to live in the workhouse where they used to work. There is a white gravestone in the old village church, and only one word on it: Agnes.</p>

## Глава 1 (Рождение и работный дом)

* Где родился Оливер Твист и что случилось с его матерью сразу после родов?
  <details> 
  <summary>ответ:</summary>
  Он родился в работном доме (workhouse) в одном из английских городов. Его мать умерла сразу после родов.
  </details>

* Как Оливер получил свою фамилию?
  <details> 
  <summary>ответ:</summary>
  Мистер Бамбл (Mr. Bumble) давал детям имена в алфавитном порядке. Предыдущего сироту назвали на букву S, а этого — на букву T, поэтому он выбрал фамилию Twist.
  </details>
