<summary>Story One</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Story One</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">I Do Not Remember My Name</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">I do not remember my name. This is the first thing that I think when I wake and I look around nervously, confused by the dark and by the thick fog which surrounds me. I raise a hand to my face and feel a short nose and a small mouth. I try to remember my face. I try to remember the colour of my hair or what my eyes look like. I try to remember anything. But I cannot. I have no idea who I am.</p>

<p>I am on the ground, lying on cold grass which is wet from the fog. And I am alone. Why am I here? I ask myself. But I have no answer. I do not even know where this is because the fog is so thick that I can only see for a few metres in any direction.</p>

<p>I try to stand but then I realise that my head hurts and that there's a sharp pain behind my right ear. I carefully lift my hand and touch the large lump which is there. It hurts to touch, and I shout in the fog, the sound lost in the dark of the night. When I pull my fingers away they are wet, and even with no light the blood is bright and easy to see.</p>

<p>I begin to feel more than nervous now. I begin to feel scared. I imagine I can see shapes and figures in the fog, and I want to run. I feel that I need to run, that there is somewhere I must be, somewhere I must remember. But before I move I need to know something. So I push myself up and sit on the ground. I look carefully at my clothes. But they mean nothing. The jeans are new. But now they're dirty. Muddy stains cover the legs from the wet grass. The t-shirt is not familiar either, just simple and black. So I empty my pockets, and at first there is nothing helpful there — no wallet, no keys and no phone.</p>

<p>But then I see the picture and I stop. It's a woman, and even in the dark and the fog I recognise her. Her long blonde hair is beautiful, and her kind, friendly blue eyes are perfect. Yes, I know her. And the thought is so strong that I smile despite the pain in the back of my head. But what's her name?</p>

<p>I pull the last item from my pocket. It's a red serviette with a single name written on it. "Catherine," I say quietly into the fog, and the sound of my voice seems strange and cold. Catherine. I am sure that I know her. But I still do not remember why. Is she my wife? My girlfriend? I think that she is. And I suddenly feel afraid for her.</p>

<p>"Catherine!" I shout into the dark. But there is no reply. I'm about to shout again when suddenly I remember something. I remember Catherine's face. But she's not smiling like she is in the picture, and her blue eyes look scared and desperate. I try to remember the image, and I see that there's a piece of cloth in her mouth so that she cannot speak and that she's tied to a large grey stone by thick white ropes.</p>

<p>"No!" I cry out and I push myself to my feet despite the pain in my head. I do not know what to do for a moment. I only know that I must find her. That I must help her. I think about the lump on the back of my head and the fresh blood on my fingers. Is there somebody in the fog? Somebody who has Catherine? Someone who wants to hurt her? Who wants to kill her?</p>

<p>I want to shout again. But then I do not. If the person who has Catherine hears me, she is in danger. And another image comes to me. It's the face of a man. An ordinary face with small dark eyes and dirty brown hair. There's nothing cruel about the face, but I know instantly that this is the man that has Catherine, and I hate that face with all of my heart.</p>

<p>I begin to walk slowly at first, unsure of the direction that I must go. It seems like the ground is moving slowly up, and I believe that this is right. The image I remember of Catherine tied to the grey stone is in less fog, and I think that it must be on higher ground. I begin to walk faster but I'm soon running. The fog surrounds me and the dark is without end. I try not to think of anything but the direction I'm moving in, but I'm starting to remember things now.</p>

<p>I remember Catherine. She has a black uniform which she always wears at work in the restaurant, and on the uniform is a badge with her name. "Hello, handsome," she always says to me. "Another day at the office." And I never say much, but I do not have to. We understand each other without words, and I remember that after I eat I wait for her in the car park until she finishes work, and she's surprised to see me there. But I suppose that's just who I am. A romantic.</p>

<p>Suddenly there's a sound like a gunshot and I fall. For a moment I think that I'm dead and that Catherine is alone tied to the grey stone in the fog. But then I see the light in the sky, and I realise that the shot was a flare. I watch the light fall and illuminate the hillside. Is it the man with the dark eyes and dirty brown hair? Does he know I'm here? Good. If he looks for me he's not with Catherine, I think, and I get up again and run faster now.</p>

<p>Do I hear voices in the fog behind me? I try to turn to look but the fog behind me is too thick, and I only see the occasional light in the distance. But in front of me the fog seems to be thinner, and I begin to slow. I'm scared now because I know that I'm near, and in the dark I begin to see large grey stones standing like giants on the top of the hill. In amazement I think that I recognise this circle of ancient stones.</p>

<p>"Stonehenge," I say.</p>

<p>"Yes," a voice replies, "and this is where it ends." And a tall man steps from behind one of the silent giants. I expect him to have dark eyes and dirty hair, but he does not. His hair is blonde, his eyes light, and I think that this is not the man who has Catherine. Maybe he's his friend, his partner, and I'm about to run when I see the small gun in his hand.</p>

<p>"I just want Catherine," I say, but I can see the hate in the man's eyes, and I know that the only thing I can do now is run to the stone where I know she's tied and try to escape into the fog with her.</p>

<p>"Don't," says the man as if he can see my intention in my eyes, but I have to. At first I think that maybe I'm quick enough, but then I hear the snap of the gun and feel the explosion in my back. For a moment more I run, and I can see the shape of the stone where Catherine is tied, and I fall to the floor in front of it.</p>

<p>"Catherine!" I shout. But there is no reply. When I look to see why I see that she is not there. The white ropes are still tied to the stone, but she is not.</p>

<p>"Catherine," I say again, smiling because she's all that matters, and I can rest now because I know that she is safe.</p>

<p>"You got him, Detective?" a voice says behind me.</p>

<p>"Yeah, that's him," the tall man says. "He matches the girl's description perfectly. Dirty brown hair, small dark eyes. And look — the blood behind his ear is where she hit him before she escaped."</p>

<p>"Good for her."</p>

<p>"Yeah. We think she's his third victim this year. So why didn't he run? Why wait here for us?" The tall man is silent for a moment. "Who knows? I don't want to understand the mind of these psychopaths. I'm just happy Catherine is okay. Catherine is okay. Catherine is okay."</p>

<p>I do not understand anything else that they say, but I do understand these three words, and I smile again. Catherine is okay.</p>

<summary>Story Two</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Story Two</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Rob from the Rich</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">The sun is high above Canary Wharf, high and hot, and looking down on the crowds of people which wait on both sides of the empty road. We are standing there, the three of us in front of the bank. We are standing there in front of the glass doors, and no one is looking at us. They are all watching the street outside because a race is coming. The London Marathon starts soon.</p>

<p>I look at our reflections. We all have the same white and orange uniforms, the same caps, the same silver sunglasses. I almost do not recognise the two men beside me, and I think that from a distance people cannot see that I am a woman.</p>

<p>"Okay, this is it. Ten minutes exactly. Remember your jobs and remember no real names. I'm Robin, your little John," one of the men says to the other. "And your Marion," he says to me, the only woman, and he gives me a quick smile.</p>

<p>Little John looks up. "The camera is still in position. You cannot see us enter or leave."</p>

<p>I feel sweat running down my neck.</p>

<p>"Here he comes. Remember the music, Marion?" Robin says, and then the guard is at the door, a short fat man who looks at us, smiles and then opens it without a single question.</p>

<p>"You're quick. The air conditioning only broke this morning."</p>

<p>"We know," Little John says. "We broke it." And the guard's smile disappears, but it's too late. Robin pushes him back into the bank and John follows with the ladders on his shoulders. I can see Robin's gun appear in his hand, and then I'm inside too and I pull the door shut.</p>

<p>"Music, Marion?" Robin says. There is no stopping now. So I pull the small stereo from one of the black bags on my shoulder and I press play. Beethoven's Piano Concerto Number Two fills the marble stairs. We run up the stairs together, Robin with his gun at the guard's back. Then Little John stops to cover the first camera. He climbs up the ladder in a second and we run into the main room of the bank.</p>

<p>It's a large room with oak tables, a dark marble counter and expensive leather sofas. Behind the counter there are two women.</p>

<p>"Out here, now!" I shout at them. To my left I see the short fat man sit down on the floor with his hands on his head. Then Robin runs to the office and kicks open the door. The two women move but slowly. One of them looks down at the counter where I know there is a secret alarm.</p>

<p>"Not if you want to live," I shout. "On the sofa." I pull two blindfolds from a bag and cover their eyes. Then I tie their hands, and I look at my watch.</p>

<p>"Seven minutes," I shout to Little John, and he covers the last camera with plastic. I take a deep breath. The music is so loud and the bank so hot. Can we do this? Can we really do this?</p>

<p>Then Robin leaves the office and he has the manager, Mr Charles M. Hastings. Hastings. I hate the name and I hate the man. Tall, arrogant, dressed in his expensive suit with a watch that costs more than most people make in a year.</p>

<p>"What is this?" he says, and he looks nervous but not nervous enough.</p>

<p>"What do you think?" Robin asks, and he tells him to sit at one of the tables. Hastings looks around. "You can't be serious. A bank robbery." And for a moment he looks surprised. But then he laughs. "You idiots. This is an investment bank. There's no money here. Everything is done by transfer." And he looks at us all like we're children with that arrogant expression on his face. "Good Lord. You should leave now before the police get here, and maybe you can still escape." He laughs again. "But I doubt it."</p>

<p>Little John puts a blindfold on the guard. Only Hastings can see now. But we stand behind him. And for a few seconds there is only the sound of Beethoven as the music begins to reach its moto allegro.</p>

<p>"That's okay," Robin says and he picks up one of the bags and puts it on the table. "We brought our own money. £20,000 in small notes. Banks are difficult to rob but houses aren't. Even big houses with alarms and safes. Like yours."</p>

<p>"What?" Hastings says. And then he looks at the bag. "My house. My money. You robbed my house."</p>

<p>I can see sweat on Hastings's face now. But it's on mine too. I look at my watch. Four minutes.</p>

<p>"But what do you want? You have my money."</p>

<p>Robin laughs. "We don't want it. Not that. We're not common thieves." And he throws the bag to Little John. "Get it ready," he says. And Little John takes the ladders back down the stairs to the glass doors.</p>

<p>"Then what?" shouts Hastings.</p>

<p>And this is my part. I take the laptop from the bag and put it in front of him and open it. "Do you know what this is?"</p>

<p>He looks. "It's a bank account. A transfer. So?" he says arrogantly.</p>

<p>"So we need you to enter your details and then transfer £240,000 to this account. Do it. And then we go." And he looks more than nervous now. He looks scared.</p>

<p>"No, I can't. I don't have that sort of money. I can't."</p>

<p>I put the newspaper down on the computer. "Really?" He looks at the newspaper for a moment that says nothing.</p>

<p>"One minute," says Little John.</p>

<p>"Read it," Robin shouts. Hastings is silent. But Robin points the gun at him. "Read it!" he shouts again.</p>

<p>"Okay, okay."</p>

<p>"Bank manager receives £240,000 salary despite bank failure." He stops and tries to turn to look at us. But Robin pulls the trigger back on the gun.</p>

<p>"You don't understand," Hastings shouts. "It's more complicated than that."</p>

<p>"We understand that hundreds of local businesses are in serious trouble because of you and this bank," I say. "And you now have a decision to make, because we have no time to enter the details or..." I say, and I know that this is it. It has to be now. Beethoven fills the silence again. And I look at Robin and see that there's sweat on his face too. But Hastings does not move.</p>

<p>"Then goodbye, Mr Hastings," Robin says. And he moves the gun.</p>

<p>"But no, please. Look!" And Hastings enters his details.</p>

<p>"I do not believe it," I say, and I take the computer.</p>

<p>"Well," Robin says, "it's done. It's gone. Direct. No one can trace it." Then the music stops. And I want to smile, but there is no time. In the distance I hear the sound of a gun and the crowd outside cheers.</p>

<p>Robin takes the last blindfold from the bag and puts it over Hastings's eyes.</p>

<p>"And what now?" Hastings says. "You think you can just walk out of here?"</p>

<p>Robin laughs. "Something like that," he says quietly.</p>

<p>"The police are out there. They'll see you. They'll stop you."</p>

<p>Robin then puts his mouth to Hastings's ear. "Not if there's a big enough distraction," he says. "Oh, and just to let you know, there are no bullets in any of our guns." Hastings starts to shout, but we do not listen. This is it. We have to go now. We take our uniforms off and put them in the bag. Then we're at the doors.</p>

<p>Outside we hear the crowd cheer, and we see the first runners pass by. Then there are more. Many more. Hundreds more. Thousands more. And now we start to smile because we know we're nearly out.</p>

<p>"Now," says Robin. And Little John presses the control on the air conditioning. For a moment there is nothing. Then the fans start. And then money fills the air outside. £20,000. It flies over the heads of the crowd, into the runners and into the road. And then there is nothing but chaos. And no one sees us walk slowly from the bank. And no one remembers us later. And maybe you want to know what we plan to do with the money. Unfortunately, this I can never tell you.</p>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Story Three</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">The Loch and the Monster</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">"No, darling. She means nothing to me. Less than nothing. I hate her. It's only you, Emily. I promise."</p>

<p>"What? Divorce her? That's impossible."</p>

<p>"Why? Because of the money, the house, the cars? No, Emily, of course you're more important than them. No, no, listen. Don't go. Listen to me. When I return to London, everything is going to be different. I have a plan, darling. Darling, I have a plan. Emily?"</p>

<p>But there is no answer. And Oliver Stern puts his mobile back into his coat pocket and looks around. There is no one near to him to hear. He is alone on the jetty. Apart from the boat attendant at his chair twenty metres away, his feet on a boat and a cigarette in his mouth. And he cannot hear from there. Oliver is sure. But just to be safe, he decides not to phone Emily again. Perhaps the less she knows, the better.</p>

<p>A plan. Yes. He has a plan. A good plan. And a simple plan. He looks out across the horrible grey loch. The sun is almost down behind the green, snowy mountains now. And it's colder than before. What sort of person wants to have a holiday in this remote, ugly part of the world? Then he hears the sound of his wife's laughter from the terrace bar of the hotel. And he shakes his head. The sort of person like my monster of a wife.</p>

<p>He tries to picture Emily. Her eyes are so loving, her laugh so light and pretty. But when his wife laughs, she sounds like a donkey. He looks at the loch again, at the jetty and the small boats waiting there for the tourists in the hotel. And he asks himself once more if he can do it.</p>

<p>She brays again. And he knows that he can. It's ten to six now. The boats stop going out at six. And the sun is almost gone. She needs to come now, he knows, but he cannot shout for her. No, it's her idea to go on a boat. Everyone in the bar knows she wants to go. I just need to wait for her, like a good husband, he thinks. The type of husband who is happy to go out on the huge, horrible loch in the dark with his monster of a wife. But can she still swim? He's not sure. He thinks that with her clothes and her coat on she'll be too heavy, and that the cold grey water of the loch will pull her down. But is that enough? He looks at the bag by his feet, the romantic picnic, filled with all the food his wife loves and the bottle of champagne that he knows she cannot resist. Oliver smiles. It is a heavy bag. All he has to do is make her hold it for a second, perhaps quickly wrap the straps around her hands. Then, no more monster.</p>

<p>But it's nearly six now. The attendant is about to finish for the night.</p>

<p>"Excuse me?" Oliver shouts with a smile. He pulls his cigarette packet from his pocket and walks over. "Sorry to bother you, but do you have a lighter?"</p>

<p>The attendant has a hard face and fierce red hair. He looks at Oliver for a moment, nods, pulls his lighter from his pocket and offers it.</p>

<p>"Look," continues Oliver, "my wife wants to take a boat out. She's going to be here in a second."</p>

<p>The man looks at the sky. "A boat, man? We want a boat?"</p>

<p>"Dinner?"</p>

<p>He cannot do it. The idiot does not speak English.</p>

<p>"Bloody country. I'm sorry, I don't understand. I'm from England."</p>

<p>The man laughs, stands up and says nothing. Oliver tries to stay calm, but he knows there's too little time.</p>

<p>"Don't you speak English? My wife wants to go out now. She's only going to be one minute," he shouts, and the attendant stops and gives him a long, hard look with his cold blue eyes.</p>

<p>"Oliver, here I am." A loud, cheerful voice says. And the two men turn to see Mrs Sylvia Stern. She smiles that stupid cow-like smile at them, and Oliver sees the idiot attendant smile back. She is dressed in a horrible tartan skirt and woollen jumper. And in her arms she has the heavy wax coat. Good, Oliver thinks.</p>

<p>"Darling," he says with a smile. "This good man wants to close. I think we must wait until tomorrow now."</p>

<p>"Oh, no, surely not." She turns to the man and smiles again. "Can't we just go out for a few minutes, please? Just to enjoy the sunset?"</p>

<p>And the attendant ignores Oliver but returns her smile. "Aye. Well, just for ten minutes then."</p>

<p>"Oh, thank you," Sylvia says. And the attendant helps them into the small rowing boat and passes Oliver the picnic bag. He then gives him another long, hard look. But Oliver does not care.</p>

<p>"Bloody idiot," he says to Sylvia as they slowly move away from the jetty onto the dark water. "I don't understand a thing."</p>

<p>But Sylvia does not seem to hear. And instead looks out at the dark loch.</p>

<p>For five minutes, Oliver rows. And soon the jetty and the hotel look much smaller, while the attendant is almost impossible to see in the half light of the new evening. And so this is it. Oliver thinks about loving, ironic Emily again. Then he looks at his monster of a wife. Oh yes, this has to be it. No more listening to that awful laugh. No more looking at her boring face. And perhaps more importantly, no more sharing his bank account with her.</p>

<p>"Darling," he says, "shall we have a little drink?"</p>

<p>"Oh yes, that would be very nice." She smiles, but in the half light Oliver thinks he sees something strange in her expression.</p>

<p>"Can you pass me over the bag? Then you can come and sit here next to me," he says as casually as he can.</p>

<p>"Of course." Sylvia says and she stands up, which makes the boat rock. Oliver thinks that this is going to be very easy indeed. Just one push. And then he can shout for the attendant and pretend to help, but really just watch the monster go down into the loch.</p>

<p>Just one look back at the jetty to be sure no one can see, he thinks. And he turns his head. And then it happens. Pain explodes at the back of his head and his eyes go dark. For a second he does not know where he is. Then he feels hands on him. Pushing him and moving him. As he tries to say something, he sees the broken champagne bottle at his feet. Then he falls from the boat. And the ice cold water takes the words and the air from his mouth.</p>

<p>For a moment he goes down, but then he kicks his legs and he comes to the surface and looks up at Sylvia in the boat. She has an oar in her hands, and she's reaching it out to him.</p>

<p>"Yes, closer, help me," he shouts. And it's then that he sees the strange expression in her face again.</p>

<p>"Sylvia, darling..."</p>

<p>"Don't you dare call me that," she says in a tone as cold as the water. "You're a cheating, lying, horrible monster of a man. You can call that stupid girl of yours darling, but not me. Never again, Oliver Stern. Never again." And then the oar hits him on the head, and he cannot think and he cannot swim, and the water takes him.</p>

<p>For a few moments he sinks slowly into the great loch. But then somehow, miraculously, he kicks again and he comes to the surface once more. Sylvia and the boat are gone. And the half light is complete darkness now. He tries to swim, but his clothes and his coat are too heavy. The jetty, the attendant. He has to still be there. If he hears me, he can bring a boat out.</p>

<p>"Help me," he shouts into the darkness. "Help me. For the love of God, help me."</p>

<p>And on the small jetty on the bank of Loch Ness, the attendant hears. He takes the cigarette from his mouth and smiles.</p>

<p>"Sorry, lad," he says quietly to the loch. "I can't understand the word you're saying."</p>

<summary>Story Four</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Story Four</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Follow Me</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">"Follow me," says Detective Branwell with a sigh. His shift finishes in five minutes, but there's no one around in Haworth's quiet police station. And so he leads the woman to the first interview room and tries to suppress the image of a pint of beer in the Black Bull pub. The interview room is cold, the light weak and grey. But he offers the woman a seat and hopes that this will be quick.</p>

<p>As she takes her coat off, Branwell watches her. She has long brown hair that right now looks a little untidy. And she has deep green eyes that at the moment are tired and nervous and show something which he does not completely recognise.</p>

<p>"Right," he says, and tries to smile to relax her. "What's all this about then? The officer at the front desk says you need to speak to someone."</p>

<p>"I do," she says, and her voice is thin and tense.</p>

<p>"About what?"</p>

<p>She waits for a moment, uncertain, and then she takes a breath. "About a crime."</p>

<p>Branwell pulls his notepad from his pocket and places it in front of him on the table. He has a bad feeling about his pint in the Black Bull pub.</p>

<p>"I see," he says. "Maybe you can tell me your name first. You're address too."</p>

<p>She puts her hands on the table, and he sees that her red fingernails are chipped and bitten.</p>

<p>"Jane Thornton. I live at Brow Top Cottage."</p>

<p>"Brow Top? Oh yes, I remember. You have that little red sports car, don't you?"</p>

<p>"Not now, no," she says, and she picks up her handbag. "Can I smoke?"</p>

<p>"No, sorry, love. Rules and regulations. I can get you a tea if you like." But she shakes her head and puts the bag at her feet.</p>

<p>"And maybe you can tell me why you're here," he suggests. Again, she waits. And again Branwell thinks that he sees something in her deep green eyes.</p>

<p>"There's a man who follows me," she begins. "Every evening. Every evening when I return home from work."</p>

<p>Branwell nods. "I see."</p>

<p>"No, you don't. Every evening. Every single evening. For almost a year now. He's always there, Detective. Sometimes he's so close that I think he can touch me, and other times he's far away. But I still know that he's there. And Detective, you might think that I'm crazy, but I know what this man wants."</p>

<p>"What?" Branwell says. "What does he want?"</p>

<p>She pauses for a second and he sees her hands shake. "He wants to kill me."</p>

<p>Branwell says nothing but watches the woman's face. She is scared. Very scared.</p>

<p>"Does he speak to you, this man?"</p>

<p>"No, never. And when I try to speak to him, me —"</p>

<p>"Yes?"</p>

<p>"He disappears."</p>

<p>Branwell opens his notebook and takes his pen out. "Where do you work, Mrs Thor — Miss Thor?"</p>

<p>"Miss Thorne. I work in Leeds. I have a small media company."</p>

<p>"In Leeds. Then where does this man follow you from?"</p>

<p>"From the station at Keighley."</p>

<p>"You work in Leeds, but you don't drive? I think your sports car can get you there a lot faster than the bus to Keighley and then the train to Leeds."</p>

<p>"I told you, I don't drive it. Not now."</p>

<p>"Right. So he followed you. From Keighley?"</p>

<p>"I see him at the train station sometimes. Then he's there again when I get off the bus in the town centre here in Haworth. And then he follows me to my cottage."</p>

<p>"You walk from here to Brow Top, up over the moor road, every evening? That's a dangerous road. And it must be more than three miles."</p>

<p>"Dangerous. Yes." But she does not say anything else, and Branwell continues.</p>

<p>"Can you describe the man?"</p>

<p>"Easily," she says. But again she pauses and Branwell sees her turn quickly to look over her shoulder. "He's tall with dark hair. His skin is pale and his eyes cold. Dead," she says. "He has dead eyes. And they watch me every day, and I think that they watch me at night too."</p>

<p>Branwell feels a shiver run over his back. "You think that this man enters your premises? That he enters your cottage during the night?"</p>

<p>"I do. Sometimes I wake and I know that he's there. I turn on the lights and I see nothing, but when I go to the windows I can see him in the field. He stands there like a scarecrow in the dark, and he accuses me every day. His cold dead eyes accuse me."</p>

<p>Branwell shakes his head. Is she crazy or just terrified?</p>

<p>"Miss Thorne, if this man enters your house we can arrest him. But to do that we need evidence. Has he broken in a window or entered the property by force?"</p>

<p>She lets out a laugh, a laugh that is horrible to Branwell. "No. Of course not. There's nothing. And he never says anything threatening. He never says that he wants to hurt you."</p>

<p>She looks over her shoulder again, quickly and automatically as if she does it a hundred times a day. "No. He never says a thing. But I know. I know what he wants. I know what he wants." And she begins to cry.</p>

<p>Branwell puts the notepad away. "I'm sorry, Miss Thorne, but there's very little that we can do. Let me make you that tea. A nice tea to calm you down. We can start again and see if there are any other details."</p>

<p>She seems to nod, and he stands up and goes to the door.</p>

<p>"We need a better description, you see, if we want to identify the man."</p>

<p>"What?" she says before he can open the door. "Oh no, no. You don't understand, Detective. I know who the man is. I know his name. I know everything."</p>

<p>Branwell stops.</p>

<p>"You mean?"</p>

<p>She continues. "And if I tell you his name, you can help me. You can stop this," she says desperately. "You know his name. Do you promise? You can stop it."</p>

<p>Branwell does not sit. He sees that expression in her eyes again. "I can't promise, love. No. But I can try."</p>

<p>She nods and bites her red fingernails for a moment. "His name is William Gray."</p>

<p>William Gray. Branwell knows the name. William Gray. And he remembers something horrible from more than a year ago. An accident on the road across the moors. William Gray. A local cyclist and a good man.</p>

<p>"Miss Thorne, William Gray is dead. He was hit by a car last year while he cycled across Brow Top road. We never found the driver, but William died instantly, they say."</p>

<p>"Oh, I know," she says, and he sees that something in her deep green eyes. And he thinks that he knows what it is now. "I know, Detective, because I am that driver."</p>

<p>Then there is only the sound of her crying to fill the cold interview room. And Branwell sits down again and picks up his pen and notepad.</p>

<p>"I think you should tell me everything," he says, and he can see clearly now the guilt that fills her deep green eyes.</p>

<p>An hour later Branwell closes the door of the interview room and sighs. He does not feel any sympathy for her. He cannot. Not for someone who can do that. To leave someone on the grey moors to die. No, he cannot feel any sympathy for her. But maybe now she can find some rest. Maybe now she can forget the ghost of William Gray.</p>

<p>He walks up the corridor to the front desk and is about to shout for an officer when he sees the tall figure standing at the door of the police station. He stops. His heart suddenly beating faster. The man's dark hair is wet from the rain outside. His skin pale and his eyes cold and dead. It's him. Branwell knows it. But he cannot believe it.</p>

<p>"Do you have her confession?" the figure says.</p>

<p>"What?"</p>

<p>"Do you have Miss Thorne's confession?" the figure repeats.</p>

<p>Branwell nods. "Yes, I do. She's in the interview room now."</p>

<p>The figure nods. "Good. Then it's over." And the impossible figure turns to leave. Branwell still cannot believe it. Yet the similarity is perfect.</p>

<p>"Wait, Mr Gray. William, wait. I need to ask you —"</p>

<p>But the figure turns and smiles. It's a cold smile, but Branwell sees that the man's eyes look a little more alive now.</p>

<p>"My name is Gray, Detective, but not William. My name is Matthew. Matthew Gray. William was my brother," he says as he opens the door to the rain. "My twin brother."</p>

And of sixty thousand pounds of uncut diamonds.</p>

    <p class="first-para">The sky is not there. In its place are a thousand adverts on a hundred television screens. A sea of lights that constantly move and change. Red, white, blue, green, orange, gold. Red, white, blue, green, orange, gold. They never stop. And on the television screens there are giant faces, clips from movies, news headlines, commercials for cars and perfume, chocolate and beer. The sounds never stop. The horns of the big yellow taxis, the music from the bars and the restaurants. And the voices. So many voices. So many that they are impossible to understand. And so many languages. English, Spanish, Chinese, Italian, German, Portuguese. They all mix together and are little more than noise to an onlooker. But the smells are the worst. One moment it's the scent of hot dogs, onions, tomatoes, fries or doughnuts. Delicious smells that make the stomach rumble. But then it's cigarettes, alcohol, garbage and vomit. <p>Jake feels really sick now. The sun is somewhere up there above the skyscrapers of Times Square. And the afternoon is hot. The smells and sounds make a horrible mixture. He thinks about home for a moment. But that is too far away now and too long ago.</p>

<p>"You want to eat, don't you, kid?" Jake nods. "And you remember where the cameras are? The NYPD ones? The others are nothing. But don't let the NYPD ones see you, or you're not coming back here. Not with me."</p>

<p>"How did you know, kid?" a voice says. But this time it's not Nick.</p>

<p>"I didn't," he manages to say. "It's just the wrong bag."</p>

And you do not tell yourself to return to your calm and gentle dreams.</p>

<p>The blanket of your bed is covering all of you except for your head. And part of you thinks that this is good. Part of you wants to cover your head too. To hide. The alarm of a car. A baby crying in the next house. Or something closer. Something inside the house. The sound of breaking glass. The noise of the front door opening. A voice in the dark.</p>

<p>So you do not move. But you let your eyes adjust. And soon the complete darkness is not so complete. And you can see your room now. Though everything seems strange and different. The lamp in the corner of the room looks like the tall silent figure of a murderer. And the coat on the back of your door has hands that almost touch you. But this is just your imagination. Your fear. And you know that. And try to stay calm and focused.</p>

<p>So you listen. And you think that everything seems to be normal. Inside the room everything is silent. Apart from the slow and constant tick of the small clock on the wall. The darkest hours of the night when even the lively and vibrant city of Manchester sleeps. Yes, outside the house the streets are silent. There are no shouts or loud voices coming from university students returning home from the pubs and bars. There is not yet the sound of the milk van or rubbish collectors.     <p class="first-para">The back streets of Dublin are quiet, and the sun is slowly setting on the old city. Barry Brennan stands in the doorway of an empty shop and waits. He smokes his cigarettes continuously and enjoys the taste of each one. He enjoys another, sweeter taste too. The taste of freedom. But he has to be careful. He knows that. The Garda are everywhere. And they are looking for him. And if he wants to keep his freedom he has to be clever. He has to be patient.</p>

<p>And so he watches the street. And the small red brick houses. Most importantly, though, he watches the church. His own parish church. The church of his childhood. St Anthony's. How long ago was his last visit to this cold stone building? More than twenty years? Well, that's not important at the moment. No. The only thing that's important is something that the church can offer him right now. The one thing he needs if he wants to keep his freedom and get the suitcase that's buried in his brother's old garage. The suitcase that can help him escape the country and live the rest of his life on a tropical island in the Bahamas. Sanctuary.</p>

<p>And so he waits. And the evening becomes darker. And he thinks that there are now only one or two people left in the small old church. He decides that it's time. He pulls the collar of his stolen coat up to cover his face. And he checks that no one can see the grey prison uniform that he's still wearing beneath it. He finishes his last cigarette and stops to listen. In the distance he can hear the siren of a Garda car in the city centre. But he's sure it's not for him. So he crosses the road slowly and casually, walks up the stone steps of the church and looks around once more before he pushes open the wooden doors and goes inside.</p>

<p>St Anthony's. It smells no different, he thinks as he enters. And it looks identical too. The same cold grey walls. The same uncomfortable heavy pews. And an old man who looks at Barry for a moment before returning to his prayers. But not one of them is interested in him. Good, Barry thinks. The place is practically empty. He looks to the right wall and sees what he's looking for. A large wooden box with two doors. Quietly he walks past the pews and stops near the box. He pauses for a moment to try and remember what he has to do next. But then the door to the church opens again and three women enter. As he does not want them to see his face, he pulls open the left door to the box and enters.</p>

<p>A confessional. As soon as he's inside he hates it. It's small, so small, and it reminds him of his prison cell back in the Joy.</p>

<p>"Father," he says. But there is no reply, and he can see no movement through the grill. He makes himself comfortable on the small chair. But after a few minutes he realises how tired he is. And he thinks that maybe he can close his eyes for a moment and even rest his head against the grill. But he mustn't sleep, he tells himself. He mustn't. But he does. And he dreams of the suitcase buried under his brother's old garage. And he dreams of the money inside and the paradise in the Bahamas.</p>

<p>"Hello?" says a voice. And Barry wakes up and looks around in confusion and fear. "Is there someone there?" says the voice.</p>

<p>Barry remembers where he is. "Yes, I'm here," he says. And for a moment there is silence before he remembers what to say. "Bless me, Father, for I have sinned." And again there is a long silence. Barry tries to see the face through the grill. But there is only a dark shape. Finally, the voice replies.</p>

<p>"I see. Go on."</p>

<p>"First, Father, I need to know something. I need to know that anything I say here is not to be repeated. That's how this works, isn't it?"</p>

<p>"That is true, my son," the voice says. And Barry thinks that it's a calm and honest voice.</p>

<p>"Is that Father O'Connor?" he asks, remembering his old priest.</p>

<p>"O'Connor? No, son. My name is Keen. I believe Father O'Connor passed away nearly ten years ago."</p>

<p>Barry nods in the dark space. "But does it matter that you're not my priest? Can you still listen?"</p>

<p>"Any man can listen," says the quiet voice.</p>

<p>"But can you promise not to tell anyone?"</p>

<p>"I promise, my son. I will not tell a soul."</p>

<p>And Barry nods again and feels ready to speak. "Father, I need to ask for something from you. I need to ask for sanctuary."</p>

<p>"Sanctuary?"</p>

<p>"Yes, Father. I need somewhere to hide for a few days, a week maybe. Can you do that? Can you help me hide here in the church?"</p>

<p>There is another silence. "Maybe. But why, my son? I can only help you if you tell me why. God, you see, is in the detail. If you want to confess and receive God's forgiveness, you must tell me everything."</p>

<p>"Okay, Father. But remember, you promise not to tell a soul?"</p>

<p>"I do."</p>

<p>Barry nods. "My name is Barry Brennan. Do you know the name?"</p>

<p>Another pause. "I do, my son. It's on every radio and every television. You are the prisoner, the one that escaped from Mountjoy Prison. My son, you should let me take you to the guards."</p>

<p>"No," Barry shouts. "You promised, Father. No one can know."</p>

<p>And this time the silence is heavy and tense. And Barry can hear the voices of the women as they leave the church. He wonders if there are still any people there. But he thinks there are not.</p>

<p>"Now, son, I told you that any man can listen, didn't I?" it says. "And I also told you that God is in the detail. And an important detail — a very important detail — is that you're in the wrong side of the confessional. I just stopped in to confess about stealing another bottle of whiskey from the shop. But you know, I think I can do that next time. Yes, I think I might have a lot more to confess tomorrow."</p>

<p>"No, you said... Howth Road..."</p>

<p>And then there is only the sound of Barry Brennan's shouts. And there is no one in the church to hear those. Well, apart from God, I suppose.</p>

<p>"It's illegal." Owen thinks about what to say. "Well, it is, son. But only like downloading films for free. That's a crime. That's illegal. But you still do that, don't you? <p>Junior nods. "I suppose so. But what if someone sees us?"</p>

<p>"Ah, good question, son. Now, not many people come to this side of the forest at this time of the year. But if you see someone, you just put the gun down gently in a bush or under some leaves. Remember to do it nice and gently and to say hello. If you have no gun, then there's no problem. We can come back for the gun another day. Okay? So remember, if you see or hear someone..."</p>

<p>Junior looks a bit happier now. And for twenty minutes, Owen talks to him about how to hold the gun and how to walk with it. While his son listens carefully and asks sensible questions that show a real interest. For the first time in many years, Owen and Junior seem to be exactly what they are, father and son. And Owen notices that the boy actually looks a little bit like him after all. And that he also smiles sometimes. Sure, he likes films and television and not sports. But maybe this can be their thing. It doesn't have to be every weekend if Junior doesn't want to. But occasionally, then maybe in a few years, they can start to go for a pint like Owen and his father did.</p>

<p>"Right then, son," he says, feeling extremely happy and enjoying the excitement in the boy's eyes. "Now, I'm going to load the guns, then we can follow the path there into the forest. Do you remember the rules about the gun, son?"</p>

<p>"Yes, Dad. Keep the gun pointed down to the ground unless I see a rabbit. Never point the gun at you or anyone else. Keep to your left, but not directly behind you. Don't shoot unless I can clearly see that there's no one near the target. And don't forget there are two shots, and that I should empty the gun before I give it back to you."</p>

<p>"Exactly," says Owen. And he feels full of pride and thinks what a wonderful day this is, out in the beauty of the Welsh countryside with his son.</p>

<p>And for a while they walk. And he's happy about how carefully Junior holds the gun and how he listens and does exactly what he should. But then they reach another clearing and they hear something behind the trees.</p>

<p>"Dad," Junior whispers, and he looks scared. "What's that noise? Is it the police?"</p>

<p>And Owen signals him to be quiet. He tries to see through the trees, but he cannot. He thinks there is someone or something moving. And it's big, bigger than a rabbit or a hare, but he does not think it can be a person. He never sees people up here at this time of year. In the summer maybe, but not now. Could it be a park ranger from the Forestry Commission? Did someone see the car parked in the trees and called the police?</p>

<p>There is a snap from behind the trees, and Owen thinks that maybe he should put his gun down in a bush, nice and carefully, like he told Junior to do. Behind him he can hear Junior, who sounds like he is very scared. But then Owen sees what is behind the trees. Brown hair, big gentle eyes. He laughs and turns back to Junior to tell him to stay calm. But before he can do this, Junior, who does not know what is making the noise, remembers his dad's words. If you have no gun, then there's no problem. But he does not remember the other words. Put it down gently.</p>

<p>Owen sees the boy throw the gun and tries to shout that it's just a deer, but it's too late. There is a loud bang. Owen feels an incredible pain in his behind, and he screams and falls to the floor. As he lies there, shouting and screaming and trying to pull the pieces of shot from his skin, he looks up at the beautiful blue sky and green trees and magnificent forest. And he thinks that maybe next time they should just go to the cinema instead. Or maybe play a computer game or watch television.</p>
