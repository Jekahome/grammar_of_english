<summary>Chapter One</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter One</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Mystery Girl</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">It all began on a beautiful spring morning in a village called Whistler in Canada, a pretty little village in the mountains of British Columbia. There was a café in the village with tables outside, and at one of these tables sat a young man. He finished his breakfast, drank his coffee, looked up into the blue sky and felt the warm sun on his face.</p>

<p>Nick Lortz was a happy man. The waiter came up to his table.</p>

<p>"More coffee?" he asked.</p>

<p>"Yeah, great!" said Nick. He gave the waiter his coffee cup.</p>

<p>The waiter looked at the camera on the table. "On vacation?" he said. "Where are you from?"</p>

<p>"San Francisco," Nick said. He laughed. "But I'm not on vacation. I'm working. I'm a travel writer and I'm doing a book on mountains in North America. I've got some great pictures of your mountain."</p>

<p>The two men looked up at Whistler Mountain behind the village. It looked very beautiful in the morning sun.</p>

<p>"Do you travel a lot then?" asked the waiter.</p>

<p>"All the time," Nick said. "I write books and I write for travel magazines. I write about everything. Different countries, towns, villages, rivers, mountains, people."</p>

<p>The waiter looked over Nick's head. "There's a girl across the street," he said. "Do you know her?"</p>

<p>Nick turned his head and looked. "No, I don't."</p>

<p>"Well, she knows you, I think," the waiter said. "She's watching you very carefully." He gave Nick a smile. "Have a nice day." He went away, back into the café.</p>

<p>Nick looked at the girl across the street. She was about twenty-five and she was very pretty. "She is watching me," Nick thought.</p>

<p>Then the girl turned and looked in one of the shop windows. After a second or two, she looked back at Nick again. Nick watched her. "She looks worried," he thought. "What's she doing? Is she waiting for somebody?"</p>

<p>Suddenly the girl smiled. Then she walked across the street, came up to Nick's table and sat down. She put her bag down on the table. The bag was half open.</p>

<p>"Hi, I'm Jan," she said. "Do you remember me? We met at a party in Toronto."</p>

<p>"Hi, Jan," said Nick. He smiled. "I'm Nick, but we didn't meet at a party in Toronto. I don't go to parties very often — and never in Toronto."</p>

<p>"Oh," the girl said. But she didn't get up or move away.</p>

<p>"Have some coffee," said Nick. The story about the party in Toronto wasn't true, but it was a beautiful morning and she was a pretty girl. Maybe it was a party in Montreal or New York.</p>

<p>The girl laughed. "Okay, maybe it was. And yes, I'd love some coffee."</p>

<p>When she had her coffee, Nick asked, "What are you doing in Whistler? Or do you live here?"</p>

<p>"Oh, no," she said. "I'm just, er, just travelling through. And what are you doing here?"</p>

<p>"I'm a travel writer," Nick said. "And I'm writing a book about famous mountains."</p>

<p>"That's interesting," she said. But her face was worried, not interested. And she looked across the road again.</p>

<p>A man with very short white hair walked across the road. He was about sixty years old, and he was tall and thin. The girl watched him.</p>

<p>"Are you waiting for someone?" asked Nick.</p>

<p>"No," she said quickly. Then she asked, "Where are you going next, Nick?"</p>

<p>"To Vancouver, for three or four days," he said.</p>

<p>"When are you going?" she asked.</p>

<p>"Later this morning," he said.</p>

<p>There was a letter in the top of the girl's half-open bag. Nick could see some of the writing, and he read it because he saw the word Vancouver. "And we can meet at the Empress Hotel, Victoria, Vancouver Island, on Friday afternoon."</p>

<p>"So she's going to Vancouver too," he thought.</p>

<p>Suddenly the girl said, "Do you like movies?"</p>

<p>"Movies? Yes, I love movies," he said. "Why?"</p>

<p>"I know a man, and he loves movies, and going to the cinema," she said slowly. "People call him Mr. Hollywood." She smiled at Nick. "Can I call you Mr. Hollywood too?"</p>

<p>Nick laughed. "Okay," he said. "And what can I call you?"</p>

<p>She smiled again. "Call me Mystery Girl," she said.</p>

<p>"That's a good name for you," said Nick.</p>

<p>Just then, the man with white hair came into the café. He did not look at Nick or the girl, but he sat at a table near them. He asked the waiter for some breakfast, then he began to read a magazine. The girl looked at the man, then quickly looked away again.</p>

<p>"Do you know him?" Nick asked her.</p>

<p>"No," she said.</p>

<p>She finished her coffee quickly and got up. "I must go now," she said.</p>

<p>Nick stood up too. "Nice to..." he began, but the girl suddenly took his face between her hands and kissed him on the mouth.</p>

<p>"Drive carefully, Mr. Hollywood. Goodbye," she said, with a big, beautiful smile. Then she turned and walked quickly away.</p>

<p>Nick sat down again and watched her. She walked down the road and into a big hotel.</p>

<p>"Now what," thought Nick, "was that all about?"</p>

<p>The man with white hair watched Nick and waited. After four or five minutes, Nick finished his coffee, took his books and his camera, and left the café.</p>

<p>His car was just outside the girl's hotel, and he walked slowly along the street to it. The man with white hair waited a second, then quickly followed Nick.</p>

<p>From a window high up in the hotel, the girl looked down into the road. She saw Nick and the man with white hair about fifty yards behind him. Nick got into his car, and the man with white hair walked quickly to a red car across the street. Five seconds later, Nick drove away in his blue car, and the red car began to follow him. When the girl saw this, she smiled, then went to put some things in her travel bag.</p>

<summary>Chapter Two</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Two</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">A Hand in the Back</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">That evening, in his hotel room in Vancouver, Nick could not stop thinking about the girl in the Whistler café. Why did she come and sit with him? She didn't know him, and that story about a party in Toronto wasn't true, and she was worried about something. But what? And that kiss? It was nice, of course, but why did she do it?</p>

<p>"Maybe she liked my face," Nick thought, "or my brown eyes. But I'm not going to see her again, so it doesn't matter. Forget it."</p>

<p>He put some money in his pocket, and went downstairs to the hotel restaurant. But there were no free tables, so he walked down to Gastown and found a restaurant there. After dinner, he went for a walk.</p>

<p>Vancouver was a friendly city, and Nick liked walking through Gastown and Chinatown, looking in the shops and watching the people. It was nearly dark now, and it was a busy time of the evening. There were a lot of cars, and a lot of people.</p>

<p>After a time, Nick began to walk back to his hotel. He came to a busy street, and waited with a small crowd of people to go across. A tall woman in a blue dress stood next to him. She turned and smiled at him.</p>

<p>"It's the first warm evening of spring," she said. "It's nice to be out after the long, cold winter."</p>

<p>"Yeah," said Nick. "It's great."</p>

<p>Suddenly, there was a hand in his back, and the hand pushed Nick into the road. Nick fell on his face in front of a big, green car. People screamed, but the green car stopped only inches from Nick's head. The woman in the blue dress ran into the road and pulled Nick to his feet.</p>

<p>"Are you okay? What happened?" she said.</p>

<p>The driver of the green car shouted angrily at Nick, but Nick did not hear him.</p>

<p>"Somebody pushed me," he said to the woman. "I didn't fall. Somebody pushed me."</p>

<p>"Pushed you?" said the woman. "Oh, I didn't see anybody."</p>

<p>Nick looked at the faces of the people near him, but he didn't know them. Then he saw a man's back. The man was tall and thin and had very short, white hair. He walked quickly away down the street and did not look back.</p>

<p>"Hey, you!" Nick shouted. "Wait!" But the man did not stop, and he was soon lost in the crowds.</p>

<p>"Did he push you?" asked the woman in the blue dress.</p>

<p>"I don't know," Nick said.</p>

<p>"Do you know him?" she asked.</p>

<p>"I don't know his name," Nick said. "But I know that short, white hair. Now where did I see it before?"</p>

<p>The woman began to move away. "I must get home," she said. "Are you okay now?"</p>

<p>"Yeah, I'm okay," Nick said. "And thanks. Thanks for your help."</p>

<p>"That's okay," the woman smiled. "Be careful now."</p>

<p>Back in his hotel, Nick sat on his bed and thought. "It was an accident. Nobody pushed me. It was an accident. Nobody wants to kill me. And there are hundreds of men in Vancouver with white hair."</p>

<p>It was one o'clock in the morning, but Nick couldn't sleep. He listened to the cars in the road, and he looked at the night sky through his hotel room window. Then he sat at the table and tried to write some more of his book about mountains. But he couldn't think about his work. He got back into bed.</p>

<p>There were four or five magazines in the hotel room. They were not very interesting, but Nick sat in bed and opened one — and saw a photo of Mystery Girl. He looked at the picture very carefully. Yes, it was her. Jan, the girl from the Whistler Café.</p>

<p>She was next to a man of about fifty or fifty-five, and they were in the garden of a big, expensive house. They smiled at the camera, and they looked very happy. "Canadian millionaire Howard Hudson and his daughter Meg," it said under the picture. "At their home in Toronto."</p>

<p>Meg Hudson — not Jan, not Mystery Girl. Meg Hudson, the daughter of a millionaire.</p>

<p>Nick read it again. "Why did she come and sit with me in the café at Whistler?" he thought. "Millionaires' daughters don't sit with strangers in cafés, and then give them a big kiss when they leave. Why did she do it? What did she want?"</p>

<p>He thought back to the café in Whistler, and the girl next to him at the table. Then he remembered something. He remembered a man at a table near them in the café. A tall, thin man, about sixty years old. A man with very short, white hair.</p>

<p>Nick didn't sleep much that night.</p>

<summary>Chapter Three</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Three</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">A Walk in the Park</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">The next day was Thursday. Nick stayed in his hotel room and wrote about mountains all morning. Then he drove to Stanley Park in the afternoon. He sat and read a book for an hour. Then he went for a walk under the tall trees. There was nobody here. It was quiet, and he could walk and think.</p>

<p>He thought about Meg Hudson and about the man with white hair. Did he know Meg Hudson? Did she know him? He remembered Meg Hudson's last words. "Drive carefully, Mr. Hollywood." Why did she say that? Why did she call him Mr. Hollywood? He didn't understand any of it.</p>

<p>Suddenly, he heard a noise. He stopped. "That was a gun," he thought. "There's somebody in the trees with a gun." Then something hit the tree over his head.</p>

<p>"Somebody's shooting at me," Nick thought. He turned and ran. And somebody began to run after him.</p>

<p>Nick ran through the trees. There was no sun in here, and it was half dark. And there were no people. Nobody to help him. "I must get to my car," Nick thought. "Find some people, the police." He ran on. He could still hear the gunman behind him, so he ran faster. After three or four minutes, he stopped and listened.</p>

<p>Nothing. It was all quiet.</p>

<p>Nick was afraid. "What's happening?" he thought. "Why is somebody shooting at me? First a hand pushes me in front of a car, and now somebody shooting at me."</p>

<p>He waited another second or two, then walked quickly back to his car. He was very careful. He looked and listened all the time. But nobody came out of the trees, and nobody shot at him.</p>

<p>Then he saw people. Women with young children. Some boys with a football. Two men with a dog. He began to feel better. "Nobody can shoot me now," he thought. "Not with all these people here."</p>

<p>Ten minutes later, he was back at his car. There was a letter on the window. Nick read it. It said, "I'm going to kill you, Mr. Hollywood."</p>

<p>Nick drove to the nearest police station. He waited for half an hour. Then a tired young policeman took him into a small room. Nick told his story, and the policeman wrote it all down.</p>

<p>"So, what are you going to do?" asked Nick.</p>

<p>"Nothing," said the policeman.</p>

<p>"Nothing?" said Nick. "But somebody shot at me..."</p>

<p>"Mr. Lortz," the policeman said tiredly, "how many people are there in this town with guns? I don't know," said Nick.</p>

<p>"But you didn't see the gunman. Was it a man? A boy? A woman? Colour of eyes? Long hair? Short hair? You don't know, because you didn't see anybody. Maybe it was an old girlfriend. Maybe somebody doesn't like your travel books, Mr. Lortz."</p>

<p>"But what about the man with white hair in Whistler?" said Nick. "The girl, Meg Hudson, called me Mr. Hollywood in the café. And this man heard her. And now I get a letter to Mr. Hollywood on my car. Who is this Mr. Hollywood?"</p>

<p>"We all want answers to our questions, Mr. Lortz," the policeman said. "But we don't always get them. Questions, but no answers."</p>

<p>Nick walked out of the police station and drove to his hotel. He was angry and afraid. "How did the man with white hair find me in Vancouver?" he thought. "Did he follow me from Whistler? Is he following me now? Maybe he's staying at my hotel, too. In the next room, with his gun."</p>

<summary>Chapter Four</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Four</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">The Man with White Hair</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">Nick stopped his car in front of the hotel. He looked carefully before he got out. But there was nobody with white hair near the hotel. He half-ran through the hotel doors and went to the desk inside.</p>

<p>"I'm looking for a man with very short white hair," he said to the woman behind the desk. "He's staying here, I think. He's about sixty years old and he's tall and thin."</p>

<p>The woman did not look very interested. "There are a lot of visitors in the hotel," she said. "Do you know his name?"</p>

<p>"No, I don't," Nick said. "He's a friend of a friend, you see. He arrived in Vancouver yesterday and I must find him. It's very important. Please help me."</p>

<p>The woman looked at him. "There are three hundred and fifty rooms in this hotel," she said. "And maybe thirty or forty men with white hair. How can I remember all their names?" She turned away to answer a telephone call.</p>

<p>Nick walked away from the desk. "A drink," he thought. "I need a drink." He went into the hotel bar, got a drink, and sat down at a table.</p>

<p>"So, what do I do now?" he thought. And then he remembered something. A letter in the girl's half-open bag in the Whistler Café. "And we can meet at the Empress Hotel, Victoria, Vancouver Island, on Friday afternoon."</p>

<p>And tomorrow was Friday. "I'm going to Victoria on Vancouver Island," he thought, "to the Empress Hotel."</p>

<p>Nick had dinner in the hotel that evening. He finished eating and got up from his table — and saw the man with white hair. Nick moved quickly. The man was at the hotel desk. Nick could see the white head above the other heads near the desk.</p>

<p>"Excuse me," said Nick. He pushed past the people in the hotel restaurant. A small boy ran in front of him, and Nick ran into him. The boy and Nick fell down on the floor. The boy began to cry.</p>

<p>"Hey!" said a woman behind Nick.</p>

<p>"I'm very sorry," said Nick. He got up and helped the boy to his feet. "Are you okay?" he asked the boy.</p>

<p>"Be more careful next time," said the woman.</p>

<p>Nick moved away quickly. But when he looked back at the hotel desk, he couldn't see the man with white hair. He pushed through the crowd of people.</p>

<p>"That man!" he shouted at the woman behind the desk. "That man with short white hair! Where did he go?"</p>

<p>The woman looked at Nick. "Mr. Vickers?" she said. "I don't know."</p>

<p>"Vickers? Is that his name?" said Nick. "What's his room number?"</p>

<p>"I'm sorry, I can't tell you that," the woman said.</p>

<p>"But I need to..." began Nick.</p>

<p>The woman turned away to answer the telephone. After a second or two, Nick went upstairs to his room.</p>

<p>"Vickers," he thought. "Does Meg Hudson know Mr. Vickers? I need some answers, and I need them quickly."</p>

<summary>Chapter Five</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Five</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Vancouver Island</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">Tsawwassen was about twenty-three miles south of Vancouver. Nick drove there in his car the next morning for the one o'clock ferry to Vancouver Island. Every five minutes, he looked behind him. The road was busy. Black cars, white cars, red cars, green cars. Maybe Vickers was in one of them.</p>

<p>At Tsawwassen, Nick drove his car onto the ferry. There were a lot of cars and crowds of people. Nick got out of his car and walked up and down the ship. He looked for a man with white hair, but he didn't see one. Soon the ferry began to move, and Nick felt better. He found the ferry restaurant and got something to eat.</p>

<p>More people came in. Nick looked at the faces of all the older men. Some had hats on, so he looked for somebody tall and thin. But there was nobody. "Maybe he's not on the ferry," Nick thought. "Maybe he's back in Vancouver."</p>

<p>Later, Nick walked around the ship again. Once, he thought he saw the man with white hair in the crowds, but he could not be sure.</p>

<p>Ninety minutes after leaving Tsawwassen, the ferry arrived at Swartz Bay on Vancouver Island, and Nick went back down to his car. Swartz Bay was twenty miles north of Victoria. Nick drove quickly, and again looked behind him every four or five minutes. Once, he saw a red car about two hundred yards behind him. "Did I see that car on the road from Vancouver to Tsawwassen?" he thought. He drove more slowly, but the red car still stayed two hundred yards behind him, and Nick couldn't see the driver's face or hair.</p>

<p>Soon, he was in the busy streets of Victoria, and Nick didn't see the red car behind him again.</p>

<p>Victoria was a city of gardens and beautiful old buildings. Nick liked Victoria very much, but today he wasn't interested in gardens or buildings. He found the Empress Hotel, went inside, and walked across to the desk.</p>

<p>"Can I help you?" a young man asked Nick.</p>

<p>"I'm meeting a friend here this afternoon," said Nick. "Miss Hudson."</p>

<p>"Hudson?" said the young man. "Wait a minute." He went away and came back. "Sorry, but there's no Miss Hudson staying here."</p>

<p>Nick took something from his pocket. It was the photograph of Meg and her father from the magazine. "This is her," he said. The young man looked at the picture.</p>

<p>"Oh, right," he said. "You mean Howard Hudson's daughter. She's not staying here, but I saw her ten or fifteen minutes ago. She was with somebody, a man. He asked me about the tea room."</p>

<p>"The tea room?" said Nick. "Where's that?"</p>

<p>The man with short white hair was tired. He couldn't sleep, and he couldn't eat. He thought about only one thing, all the time. He drove and he watched, and he waited, and he followed. When he drove into Victoria, the streets were busy, and suddenly he lost the blue car in front of him. Angrily, he drove around the city, passed all the big hotels.</p>

<p>"I must find him," he said. "I must do it today." Then he saw the Empress Hotel, and in the street outside it, a blue car. He drove past the hotel, left his car, and ran back down the street. He went across the road, and walked past the downstairs windows. There was a big room with tables and chairs, and a lot of people. He looked carefully at all the faces.</p>

<p>"There she is," he said suddenly. There were two men with the girl. He couldn't see their faces, only the backs of their heads, but one of the men was in a green shirt.</p>

<p>"Mr. Hollywood," the man said, and smiled. "Goodbye, Mr. Hollywood."</p>

<p>People in the street turned to look at him, but the man did not see them. He walked up to the doors of the hotel, and put a hand into his pocket. Inside, the gun was cold and hard.</p>

<summary>Chapter Six</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Six</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">A Tea Party</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">Nick looked through the doors of the tea room in the Empress Hotel. Meg Hudson sat at a table with a man. The man was about thirty, or maybe a year or two younger. He was tall and brown from the sun. He wore a white shirt, white trousers, and white shoes. He said something to Meg, and she laughed. She looked very happy.</p>

<p>A waiter came up to Nick. "Can I get you some tea?" he asked.</p>

<p>"No thanks," said Nick. "I'm with the two people over there." And he walked across to Meg's table.</p>

<p>"Hello, Mystery Girl," said Nick. "Remember me? We met at Whistler. Your name was Jan then, but maybe today it's Meg Hudson."</p>

<p>Meg Hudson looked up at him. "Oh," she said, and her face went red.</p>

<p>"Who is this, Meg?" asked the man.</p>

<p>"This is Nick," said Meg. "He's a writer. Nick, this is Craig Winters."</p>

<p>"Sometimes called Mr. Hollywood," said Nick.</p>

<p>"Maybe, but how did you know that?" asked Craig Winters.</p>

<p>"I guessed," said Nick. "And I think I'm beginning to understand. Can I ask you a question, Mr. Winters? Does somebody want to kill you?"</p>

<p>Craig Winters' face went white. "Kill me?"</p>

<p>"What are you talking about?" asked Meg.</p>

<p>"Before I tell you, answer this question please," said Nick. "You called me Mr. Hollywood in Whistler, and you wanted the man at the next table — the man with white hair — to hear you. Is that right?"</p>

<p>Meg Hudson did not answer at first. Then she said quietly, "Yes."</p>

<p>"Why?" asked Nick.</p>

<p>"I wanted him to follow you, and not me," she said.</p>

<p>"Why?" Nick asked again.</p>

<p>"I think he's a detective," said Meg. "And I think he's working for my father. I saw him soon after I left Toronto. He followed me." Meg put her hand on Craig Winters' arm. "My father doesn't like Craig. A month ago, he told me not to see Craig again. I'm not happy, and he knows that. I think he guessed that I'm meeting Craig. And now, he wants to find Craig, and stop him seeing me."</p>

<p>"Stop him," said Nick, "or kill him."</p>

<p>"No," Meg Hudson said. "Daddy doesn't..."</p>

<p>"The man with white hair pushed me in front of a car in Vancouver," Nick told her. "And he shot at me in Stanley Park."</p>

<p>"What?" said Meg.</p>

<p>"Tell me about this man with white hair," Winters said suddenly.</p>

<p>Nick looked at him. "He was about sixty, and he's tall and thin," he said.</p>

<p>"Do you know his name?" asked Winters.</p>

<p>"Vickers," said Nick.</p>

<p>Craig Winters suddenly looked ill. "Did he... did he follow you to Victoria? Did he follow you here?"</p>

<p>"I don't know," said Nick. He watched Winters. "You're afraid of him. Why? Why does this man, Vickers, want to kill you, Winters?"</p>

<p>Before Craig Winters could answer, Meg's face went white. "Oh no!" she said. "Look! Look over there! By the door!"</p>

<p>Nick and Craig Winters turned to look. At the door of the tea room stood the man with white hair. He looked up and down the room, and then he saw them, and began to walk across to their table. His hand was in his pocket.</p>

<p>For a second or two, the three people at the table did not move. Then Craig Winters jumped to his feet. "That's Mr. Hollywood!" he screamed. "That man there!" And he pointed at Nick.</p>

<p>The man's hand came out of his pocket, with a gun. "This is for Anna!" he shouted.</p>

<p>Nick moved very fast. The tea table went over, and Nick was down on the floor in a second. The shot went over his head, and Meg screamed. At the same time, Craig Winters shouted out, and put a hand on his arm. There was blood on his white shirt.</p>

<p>Then more people began to scream, and two waiters pulled the man with white hair down onto the floor.</p>

<p>"Get the police!" somebody shouted.</p>

<summary>Chapter Seven</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Seven</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">At the Police Station</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">It was 7.30 pm. Nick and Meg were in a room at the police station. The man called Vickers was in a different room, with three detectives. There was a doctor with him too. Craig Winters was at the hospital.</p>

<p>The door opened, and a detective came in with two cups of coffee. He put them down on the table, and turned to go out again.</p>

<p>"Detective Edmonds," Meg said. "Did the hospital call? Is Craig going to be alright?"</p>

<p>"Winters?" Detective Edmonds said. "Yes, he is going to be okay."</p>

<p>"Can I call the hospital now?" asked Meg.</p>

<p>"I'd like you to wait," said Edmonds. "Detective Keet is going to be here in a minute. He's just coming from the airport." He looked through the open door. "Ah, here he is now."</p>

<p>A second detective came into the room, and behind him was a tall man with dark hair. Meg stood up quickly.</p>

<p>"Daddy!" she cried. "What are you doing here?"</p>

<p>"The police called me," said Howard Hudson. "And I flew here at once. Detective Keet met me at the airport. Now sit down, Meg. I want you to listen to me." He did not look at Nick.</p>

<p>Meg sat down, and her father took her hands. "Meg, last week Johnny Vickers came to my house. He wanted to talk about his daughter. You remember Anna, Meg? Three months ago, she jumped off a bridge in Boston and died. She was young, beautiful, rich — and she didn't want to live. Why? Because she loved a man, and the man took her money, ran away, and left her. And the man was called..."</p>

<p>"No," said Meg. "No!"</p>

<p>"Yes, Meg. He was called Mr. Hollywood."</p>

<p>"No!" shouted Meg. She began to cry.</p>

<p>"That's right, Ms. Hudson," said Detective Keet quietly. "To you, he gave the name Craig Winters. When Anna Vickers knew him, he was Carl Windsor. But he liked all his, uh, girlfriends to call him Mr. Hollywood. He took nearly $50,000 from Anna Vickers, and there was a girl before that."</p>

<p>"No, it's not true!" Meg shouted.</p>

<p>"It is true, Meg," said her father. "Winters, Windsor — he gets all his money from rich men's daughters. Johnny Vickers loved his daughter. He went to her house in Boston after she died. He read her letters, and learned about the money, and the name Mr. Hollywood. And when he came to my house, I told him about you, Meg. I said, 'My daughter's got a new boyfriend, and she calls him Mr. Hollywood. I don't like him, but I can't stop her. She's going away to meet him next week, I think. What can I do?' Johnny put his hand on my arm, and he said, 'Don't be afraid for your daughter. I'm going to find that man, and stop him.'"</p>

<p>Meg said nothing. Her face was very white. For a minute or two, nobody spoke.</p>

<p>Then Detective Edmonds said, "Vickers told us all about it, Miss Hudson. He followed you to Whistler, and saw you with..."</p>

<p>Nick began to understand. "...with me in the café, and Meg called me Mr. Hollywood."</p>

<p>Howard Hudson looked at Nick. "You're the travel writer guy, right? Lortz?"</p>

<p>"Nick Lortz," said Nick. "Vickers nearly killed me. He shot at me twice, and..."</p>

<p>But Howard Hudson was not very interested in Nick. He looked at his daughter again. "How much money did you give him, Meg?" he said.</p>

<p>"I..." said Meg. "I gave him $25,000. Only for two or three months, he said, then he..." She began to cry again.</p>

<p>"Well, you can say goodbye to that money," said Hudson angrily.</p>

<p>"What's going to happen to Vickers?" Nick asked Detective Edmonds.</p>

<p>"Hospital, I think," said Edmonds. "Okay, he shot at you, and about fifty people saw him. But he's not a well man. The doctors are going to put him away in a hospital."</p>

<p>Howard Hudson stood up. "Okay, Meg. I'm going to take you home. My plane is waiting at the airport."</p>

<p>Meg followed her father to the door. Then she remembered Nick, and turned. "I'm sorry," she said. "I got you into all this. I called you Mr. Hollywood. That was wrong. But I didn't know."</p>

<p>"It's okay," said Nick. "You know everything now. And it's better to learn it now than later."</p>

<p>"$50,000 later," she said quietly.</p>
