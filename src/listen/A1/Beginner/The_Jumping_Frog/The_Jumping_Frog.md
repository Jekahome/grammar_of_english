<div class="book-page">

  <div class="chapter-title">The California Gold Rush</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">On January 24th, 1848, John Marshall discovered gold in the American River at Sutter's Fort, California. He wasn't looking for gold. He discovered it by chance.</p>

<p>This discovery changed the destiny of California. Suddenly this wild territory of the far west became world famous. During 1849 more than 80,000 people arrived in California looking for gold. They came from the United States, Europe, Central and South America and China. They were called gold miners, gold prospectors or simply 49ers.</p>

<p>A lot of 49ers were poor. Their dream was to find gold and strike it rich. A lot of them found gold but only a few became very rich and important.</p>

<p>With the hard-working gold miners there came saloon keepers, gamblers, dishonest people and outlaws. It was difficult to keep law and order in the far west.</p>

<summary>Chapter Two: A Letter from the East</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Two</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">A Letter from the East</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">One day a friend of mine from the east wrote me a letter. He asked me to visit an old man named Simon Wheeler. My friend wanted me to ask Simon Wheeler about a childhood companion — Leonidas W. Smiley.</p>

<p>I found Simon Wheeler sleeping by the stove of the old tavern in Angel's mining camp. I noticed that he was fat. His face was simple and peaceful. When he woke up I told him that a friend of mine wanted to know about a childhood companion named Leonidas W. Smiley.</p>

<p>Simon Wheeler blocked me with his chair in a corner of the tavern. Then very seriously he began to tell me the story that follows.</p>

<p>"There was once a fellow at the mining camp named Jim Smiley. It was the winter of 1849 or maybe the spring of 1850. He was an unusual man. He always bet money on anything that happened at the mining camp and he usually won. Jim Smiley was a very lucky man. When there was a horse race, a dog fight, a cat fight, or a chicken fight he always bet on them."</p>

<summary>Chapter Three: Bets and Races</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Three</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Bets and Races</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">Smiley owned an old horse that was slow and sick but he won money on her. The poor horse had asthma and other animal illnesses. At races she had a head start because of her health problems. Near the end of every race she became very excited and desperate. She galloped wildly. Kicked her legs in the air to the sides. Raised the dust, coughed and blew her nose. At the end of the race she was always the happy winner.</p>

<p>Smiley also had a small bulldog named Andrew Jackson. Andrew Jackson was a lazy dog and did nothing all day long but when Smiley bet money on him he was lively and ready to fight. When there was an important dog fight at the mining camp Andrew Jackson always grabbed the other dog by his back leg between his strong teeth. He didn't let go until Smiley won the bet.</p>

<summary>Chapter Four: Life in the Mining Camps</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Four</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Life in the Mining Camps</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">From 1848 many mining camps were established in the gold country near the American River and in the Sierra Nevada mountains. These camps were small settlements. They were organized quickly to meet the miners' everyday needs.</p>

<p>A mining camp usually had a saloon, a hotel, a stable, a general store, an undertaker, and a bank. Miners often paid for their food, drink, clothing, and other things with gold dust or gold nuggets. Brawls and fights were common and killings were not unusual. These were tough times and camps were rough places.</p>

<p>Today a lot of camps have become ghost towns. Others have grown into towns and cities such as Angels Camp, Auburn, Grass Valley, Jackson, Marysville, Nevada City, Murphys, Placerville, and Sonora. You can visit them and their abandoned mines when you go to California. Take U.S. State Highway 80 to Sacramento and then take California Highway 49 to the gold country.</p>

<summary>Chapter Five: Daniel Webster</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Five</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Daniel Webster</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">Smiley found a frog one day and named it Daniel Webster. He took it home and planned to teach it a lot of things. For three months Smiley sat outside his house and taught his frog how to jump far. Daniel Webster was a clever frog and learned quickly.</p>

<p>Smiley gave him a little push from behind and Daniel Webster flew into the air like a doughnut. He even turned one or two somersaults in the air. Smiley taught his frog to catch flies.</p>

<p>"Flies, Daniel, flies!" Smiley shouted. Daniel jumped up and caught the fly with his long tongue. Daniel Webster was the champion jumping frog of Calaveras County. Smiley won all bets with his handsome frog. He was very proud of Daniel Webster.</p>

<summary>Chapter Six: The Jumping Frog Jubilee</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Six</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">The Jumping Frog Jubilee</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">Do you have a pet frog? If you do, take it to the Jumping Frog Jubilee.</p>

<p>Every year in May at Angels Camp, California, there is an exciting frog competition. It is called the Jumping Frog Jubilee. Frogs from far and near can enter the competition. The frog that can jump the greatest distance is the winner.</p>

<p>A frog named Rosie the Ribitter is now the jumping champion of the world. In May 1986, she jumped 21 feet, 5 and 3 quarter inches — about 7 meters.</p>

<p>If you want more information, write to Calaveras Jumping Frog Jubilee, Post Office Box 489, Angels Camp, California 95222.</p>

<summary>Chapter Seven: A Stranger at Angels Camp</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Seven</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">A Stranger at Angels Camp</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">Smiley kept his frog in a box with small holes in it. One day, a stranger came to the mining camp. He saw Smiley holding the box.</p>

<p>"What's in that box?" asked the stranger. "Maybe a parrot, maybe a canary."</p>

<p>"No, it's a frog," answered Smiley.</p>

<p>The stranger took the box and looked at it carefully. "Yes, it is. Well, what's he good for?"</p>

<p>"Well," Smiley said, "he's good for one thing. He can outjump any frog in Calaveras County."</p>

<p>The stranger took the box again. He looked at the frog for a long time. Then he returned it to Smiley and said, "I don't see anything special about this frog."</p>

<p>"Maybe you don't," said Smiley. "Maybe you understand frogs, and maybe you don't understand them. But I'll bet $40 that he can outjump any frog in Calaveras County."</p>

<p>The fellow answered, "Well, I'm only a stranger here, and I don't have a frog. I can't bet."</p>

<p>"That's all right. That's all right. Hold my box a minute. I'll go to the river and find a frog for you," said Smiley.</p>

<p>The stranger took the box and bet his $40. Smiley bet his $40, too, and went to the river. The stranger sat down and waited for Smiley. He sat there a long time, and he began to think. Suddenly, he took Daniel Webster out of the box and opened his mouth. With a teaspoon, he put some shotgun pellets into the frog's mouth. Then he put him on the floor.</p>

<summary>Chapter Eight: Panning for Gold</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Eight</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Panning for Gold</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">The expression "panning for gold" was born during the California gold rush in 1848. The miners used a pan with a wire net on the bottom. They separated the gold from other materials by washing them with water. Miners usually pan for gold in the water of the rivers.</p>

<p>The water of the river filled their pan with sand, dirt, stones, and perhaps gold dust or gold nuggets. Other miners looked for gold in caves in the Sierra Nevada mountains, and they used a pickaxe. These caves often became gold mines.</p>

<summary>Chapter Nine: The Big Bet</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Nine</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">The Big Bet</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">After some time, Smiley returned holding a frog and said, "Now put him next to Daniel with his front paws in the same place. I'll give the word."</p>

<p>Then, Smiley said, "One, two, three, go!" He and the stranger pushed the frogs from behind. The new frog jumped forward, but Daniel didn't budge. He tried to move his shoulders, but he was anchored to the floor. Smiley was very surprised and unhappy. The stranger won the bet.</p>

<p>"Well," he said, "I don't see anything special about this frog." He took the money and left.</p>

<p>Smiley looked at Daniel for a long time. Finally, he said, "What has happened to Daniel? He looks terribly fat." He caught Daniel by the back of his neck and lifted him. "Cat's alive! You weigh five pounds!"</p>

<p>He turned Daniel upside down, and the frog spit out a big number of shotgun pellets. Now he understood what happened to Daniel. Smiley was furious. He looked for the stranger everywhere, but he never found him.</p>

<p>Simon Wheeler's story ended here. I didn't really want to hear another story about Smiley's bets, so I got up from my chair and left.</p>

<summary>Chapter Eleven: Curing a Cold — The First Sneeze</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Eleven</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">The First Sneeze</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">When the White House of Virginia City, Nevada burnt down, I lost my home, my happiness, my health, and my trunk.</p>

<p>The loss of my home and my happiness was not a problem. A home without a mother or a sister who care for you isn't really a home. The loss of my happiness was not a problem either. I'm not a poet and so I'm usually a happy person. But the loss of my good health and of my handsome trunk was a serious problem.</p>

<p>On the day of the fire, I caught a terrible cold. The first time I began to sneeze, a friend told me to put my feet in hot water and then go to bed. I did this.</p>

<p>The next day, another friend advised me to take a cold shower. I did this too.</p>

<p>After an hour, another friend told me to eat a big quantity of good food. So I went to a new restaurant and started to eat everything I saw. The owner of the restaurant asked me if the people of Virginia City often had colds. I answered that they did. He then went outside, took down the new sign and closed the restaurant.</p>

<p>That day, I met another good friend. He told me that I must drink a bottle of warm salt water. This was the only remedy to cure a cold. I tried it and the result was surprising. I threw up everything I had in my stomach. I will never drink warm salt water again. And I will never advise anyone to do so. I prefer being in a California earthquake than drinking a bottle of warm salt water.</p>

<p>This horrible remedy did not cure my cold and it made my stomach sick for some time. I continued to sneeze violently at shoe and shoe to blow my nose and to destroy handkerchiefs. My suffering grew.</p>

<summary>Chapter Twelve: The Remedies</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Twelve</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">The Remedies</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">One day, I met a woman who appeared to be 150 years old. She came from a deserted part of the far west where there were no doctors. She had years of experience helping sick people. She prepared a special mixture of molasses, aquafortis, oils and other strange drugs. She advised me to drink a glass of this mixture every 15 minutes.</p>

<p>I only drank one glass of the mixture. It was enough to make me crazy. I became extremely mean and dangerous. My mind was full of wild thoughts. My behavior was horrible. I was proud of being bad. After a few days, the effect of the horrible mixture passed. I felt a lot worse.</p>

<p>My cold passed from my head to my lungs. I coughed every moment of the day and of the night. It was impossible to sleep. The sound of my voice frightened me. I got worse every day.</p>

<p>An old friend recommended gin. I drank it. Then I drank gin with molasses. Finally, I added onions. So I had gin, molasses and onions. The smell of my breath was terrible.</p>

<summary>Chapter Thirteen: The San Francisco Remedy</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Thirteen</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">The San Francisco Remedy</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">I decided to travel to the countryside to improve my health. I went to Lake Bigler with my friend Wilson. We traveled in a comfortable pioneer coach. At Lake Bigler, we fished and sailed on the lake. We hunted for hours in the woods. In the evening, we danced. I enjoyed myself greatly. But my illness got worse.</p>

<p>A tourist at Lake Bigler recommended a cold sheet bath. I never refused a remedy. At midnight, when it was very cold, I undressed completely. I covered my body with a wet ice cold sheet. I kept the sheet on my body for a long time. It was the worst experience of my life. The wet ice cold sheet made my blood freeze and made my heart stop. I thought it was time for me to die.</p>

<p>Never take a sheet bath. Never. This is my advice to everyone.</p>

<p>My condition got a lot worse. Other people recommended other remedies. Not one of these remedies cured my cold. After a week at Lake Bigler, I decided to go to Steamboat Springs. I thought that the hot baths there were good for my health. They were not.</p>

<p>While I was at Steamboat Springs, I tried several different remedies. But I just got worse and worse. I was desperate.</p>

<p>I finally decided to visit the city of San Francisco. The day I arrived, a woman at the hotel told me to drink a bottle of whiskey every 24 hours. A dear friend who lived in San Francisco recommended exactly the same thing — a bottle of whiskey every 24 hours. That's two bottles of whiskey?</p>

<p>Well, I am happy to say that this San Francisco remedy finally cured my cold.</p>

<summary>Chapter Fourteen: A City Is Born</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Fourteen</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">A City Is Born</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">A Spanish explorer, Gaspar de Portolá, discovered San Francisco Bay in 1769 and he built a Presidio there. At the beginning of the 1800s, San Francisco, then called Yerba Buena, was a sleepy Spanish village. There was a Presidio, a Spanish church called Mission Dolores, and some simple homes. There were a few ships in the big harbor.</p>

<p>With the discovery of gold in 1848, everything changed. San Francisco suddenly became a busy city. In only one year, its population went from 1,000 to 30,000. Settlers came from all parts of the world. There was a population explosion in California.</p>

<p>The new settlers needed all types of things from the industries on the east coast. The sea route from New York to San Francisco became an important one. The city of San Francisco became the most important settlement on the Pacific coast. The city and its port were full of life. The gold miners often went to San Francisco to sell their gold, to buy supplies, and to have fun. Those were exciting times.</p>

## «The Celebrated Jumping Frog of Calaveras County»

* Кто рассказал рассказчику историю о Джиме Смайли?
  <details> 
  <summary>ответ:</summary>
  Саймон Уилер (Simon Wheeler) в старой таверне в лагере золотоискателей Энджелс.
  </details>

* Как звали человека, о котором рассказывал Саймон Уилер?
  <details> 
  <summary>ответ:</summary>
  Джим Смайли (Jim Smiley).
  </details>

* На что Джим Смайли любил заключать пари?
  <details> 
  <summary>ответ:</summary>
  Он заключал пари на любые события: скачки, собачьи бои, кошачьи бои, петушиные бои и другие соревнования.
  </details>

* Как звали старую больную лошадь Смайли, и почему она часто выигрывала?
  <details> 
  <summary>ответ:</summary>
  Её звали не указано. Она получала фору из-за своих проблем со здоровьем (астма и другие болезни), а ближе к финишу дико мчалась и часто побеждала.
  </details>

* Как звали бульдога Смайли, и в чём заключалась его техника борьбы?
  <details> 
  <summary>ответ:</summary>
  Эндрю Джексон (Andrew Jackson). Он хватал противника за заднюю ногу и не отпускал, пока Смайли не выигрывал пари.
  </details>

* Как звали лягушку Смайли и чему он её научил?
  <details> 
  <summary>ответ:</summary>
  Дэниел Уэбстер (Daniel Webster). Смайли научил его далеко прыгать, ловить мух и делать сальто в воздухе.
  </details>

* Что сделал незнакомец с лягушкой Дэниелом, когда Смайли пошёл искать другую лягушку?
  <details> 
  <summary>ответ:</summary>
  Он открыл лягушке рот и чайной ложкой насыпал туда дробинок (shotgun pellets).
  </details>

* Почему Дэниел не смог прыгнуть во время пари?
  <details> 
  <summary>ответ:</summary>
  Из-за дроби в желудке он стал очень тяжёлым и не мог сдвинуться с места.
  </details>

* Кто выиграл пари в $40?
  <details> 
  <summary>ответ:</summary>
  Незнакомец (stranger).
  </details>

## «Curing a Cold»

* Что случилось с домом рассказчика в Вирджиния-Сити?
  <details> 
  <summary>ответ:</summary>
  Дом сгорел дотла (burnt down).
  </details>

* Какую простуду рассказчик получил в день пожара?
  <details> 
  <summary>ответ:</summary>
  Он сильно чихал и у него был насморк.
  </details>

* Какие средства от простуды ему посоветовали друзья?
  <details> 
  <summary>ответ:</summary>
  Парить ноги в горячей воде, холодный душ, много еды, тёплая солёная вода, патока с селитрой, джин, джин с патокой и луком.
  </details>

* Что произошло, когда он выпил тёплую солёную воду?
  <details> 
  <summary>ответ:</summary>
  Его вырвало всем, что было в желудке.
  </details>

* Что посоветовала 150-летняя женщина и какой эффект это дало?
  <details> 
  <summary>ответ:</summary>
  Она посоветовала пить смесь патоки, азотной кислоты, масел и других странных лекарств каждые 15 минут. От одного стакана рассказчик сошёл с ума, стал злым и опасным.
  </details>

* Какое средство от простуды рассказчик называет худшим опытом в своей жизни?
  <details> 
  <summary>ответ:</summary>
  Холодная мокрая простыня (cold sheet bath) в полночь.
  </details>

* Какое средство в итоге вылечило простуду рассказчика?
  <details> 
  <summary>ответ:</summary>
  Бутылка виски каждые 24 часа (a bottle of whiskey every 24 hours).
  </details>
