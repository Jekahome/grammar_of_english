    <p class="first-para">Two hundred years ago New Orleans was quite a place to be. Lots of interesting people lived there or passed through. Musicians, gamblers, sailors. Some nights the French Quarter of the city was — well, let's say it was lively.</p>

<p>Down in Congo Square, or Place Congo as the people there called it, the scene was also pretty lively. It was a place where freed slaves used to go. A place where they worshipped a special kind of religion. This religion had a lot of different names — Vaudon, Ifa or Isha. Nowadays we call it Voodoo.</p>

<p>About that time there was a Voodoo Queen in New Orleans. Her name was Sanité Dédé and everyone was afraid of her. Lots of people used to ask her for favours. And she made a good living out of it.</p>

<p>"What do you want?"</p>
<p>"I need your help."</p>
<p>"Oh yeah?"</p>
<p>"I owe somebody a lot of money."</p>
<p>"I want to know if you have anything that belongs to him."</p>
<p>"Yes, I managed to get this from him."</p>
<p>"Nice handkerchief. Guess he must be pretty rich."</p>
<p>"Yes he is."</p>
<p>"How much do you owe him?"</p>
<p>"$5,000."</p>
<p>"Then you're one stupid son of a bitch."</p>
<p>"It wasn't my fault."</p>
<p>"This is going to cost you money."</p>
<p>"I'll pay you what I can."</p>
<p>"You'll pay me $500."</p>
<p>"$500? I can't —"</p>
<p>"$500. Take it or leave it."</p>
<p>"But I can't —"</p>
<p>"Okay, goodbye."</p>
<p>"No, open the door. Open the door! I'll pay you $500."</p>
<p>"Bring me half tomorrow."</p>

<p>Sanité Dédé was the number one Voodoo woman in New Orleans. But then, someone else came along. This is the story of Marie Laveau, the woman who became the new Voodoo Queen of New Orleans.</p>

<p>At the beginning of our story, Marie was just a simple hairdresser.</p>

<p>"Marie? Marie?"</p>
<p>"Hello, Blanche."</p>
<p>"Marie, do you have time to cut my hair today?"</p>
<p>"Not now, Blanche, no. Maybe tomorrow?"</p>
<p>"Okay, I'll see you tomorrow."</p>
<p>"Fine. Bye for now."</p>

<p>"Well, hello."</p>
<p>"Oh my God, you frightened me. I can't see anything with the blinds down. Who is it?"</p>
<p>"Well, Marie, don't you remember my voice?"</p>
<p>"Jacques, is it you? Show yourself."</p>

<p>"What do you think you're doing sitting here in the dark? What do you want?"</p>
<p>"Marie, what do you think I want?"</p>
<p>"I don't know, Jacques. I'm not a mind reader."</p>
<p>"Come on, Marie. Did you forget Friday night already?"</p>
<p>"Friday night was Friday night, Jacques. Today is another day."</p>
<p>"And today could be another day like Friday, Marie."</p>

<p>There was quite a party the day that Marie Laveau and Jacques Paris got married. Marie Laveau — fine looking woman, intelligent. And knew how to make an honest buck. Jacques Paris — well, he was fine looking too. But... well, let's say he wasn't a regular kind of guy, you know. The kind that gets up in the morning and goes to work. If he was up in the morning, it was because he'd been up all night drinking and gambling. A lot of people were very surprised when Marie married someone like Jacques.</p>

<p>"And do you, Marie, take Jacques to be your lawful wedded husband?"</p>
<p>"I do."</p>
<p>"And do you, Jacques, take Marie to be your lawful wedded wife?"</p>
<p>"Jacques. Jacques?"</p>
<p>"Huh?"</p>
<p>"Do you take this woman to be your lawful wedded wife?"</p>
<p>"Oh sure, preacher man."</p>
<p>"Jacques, just say 'I do'."</p>
<p>"Okay, I do."</p>
<p>"Good, I now pronounce you man and wife. You may kiss the bride."</p>
<p>"It's all right, Mr Preacher. I already did that."</p>

<p>"Hey Marie. Hi, Blanche."</p>
<p>"What's it like to be a married woman?"</p>
<p>"When I find out, I'll tell you."</p>

<p>"Hello, Jacques."</p>
<p>"Well, hello. Now, do I know you?"</p>
<p>"You sure do. Refresh my memory, pretty lady. My name is Gina. We met in Baton Rouge a year or so ago."</p>
<p>"Ah, yes. Very refreshing."</p>
<p>"So, Jacques, you went and got yourself a wife?"</p>
<p>"I sure did. Marrying Marie Laveau was the best thing I ever did."</p>
<p>"You think so?"</p>
<p>"Well, now, you trying to tell me something?"</p>
<p>"Well, yes I am. But here ain't the right place to tell you."</p>

<p>"Marie, what is it, Blanche?"</p>
<p>"I think you should go outside."</p>
<p>"Why should I do that, Blanche? I'm having a fine time here."</p>
<p>"Right. And Jacques is having a fine time out the back."</p>

<p>"Oh, Jacques. It's so good to see you again."</p>
<p>"Show yourself. Come here."</p>
<p>"Not here."</p>
<p>"Where then?"</p>
<p>"I have a room, second floor, in a rooming house on Rampart Street. Number 21. Why don't you come and see me there?"</p>
<p>"Sure thing."</p>

<p>"There they are."</p>
<p>"Oh my God."</p>
<p>"Jesus, how do I get out of here?"</p>
<p>"There's no way out of here except through the house."</p>
<p>"Get behind the tree."</p>
<p>"What?"</p>
<p>"Get behind the tree."</p>

<p>"Jacques? Are you out here, Jacques? Well, I'm Marie."</p>
<p>"Jacques, you with someone, Jacques?"</p>
<p>"Will someone shut the hell up? No, I'm just taking the air."</p>
<p>"There's someone here. I know there is."</p>

<p>"I think she's here."</p>
<p>"Wow, I'll pull all your hair, you bitch!"</p>
<p>"You're calling me a bitch?"</p>
<p>"Let go of me!"</p>
<p>"You come after my husband on my wedding day and you call me a bitch?"</p>
<p>"Let go of me!"</p>
<p>"Give me one good reason why I should do that."</p>
<p>"Stop it! Let me go!"</p>
<p>"Okay, I'll let go of you."</p>
<p>"You're crazy!"</p>
<p>"And now you're calling me names?"</p>
<p>"Don't you dare call me names!"</p>
<p>"Why you —"</p>
<p>"Get up! Don't hit me again!"</p>
<p>"Are you trying to kill me or something?"</p>
<p>"I'm not gonna touch you again, you piece of garbage. But if you ever go near my husband again, I really will kill you. Now get out of here!"</p>
<p>"You're a mad woman, Marie Laveau. You're gonna regret doing this."</p>

<p>"Marie, are you okay?"</p>
<p>"I'm fine, Blanche."</p>
<p>"Do you need any help?"</p>
<p>"No, go away. I want to talk to Jacques alone."</p>
<p>"Okay, but yell if you need me."</p>
<p>"Okay."</p>

<p>"Marie."</p>
<p>"Don't talk, Jacques. Just listen. This is our wedding day. And I've just found you with another woman."</p>
<p>"Marie, we were just talking. In fact, I was just telling —"</p>
<p>"Listen, you're my husband now. And from now on, you do things the way I want them."</p>
<p>"I was just —"</p>
<p>"Huh?"</p>
<p>"You hurt me, Jacques. We're man and wife. And we do things my way."</p>
<p>"Are you serious? We're gonna do things your way?"</p>
<p>"That's right."</p>
<p>"Uh-uh. No one tells me what to do, Marie. I'm a free man, remember? I ain't a slave. If you think you got yourself a slave when you married me, you're wrong. It doesn't make no difference that we're married. I'm gonna do things my way. And no one is gonna stop me."</p>
<p>"You're gonna do things your way?"</p>
<p>"I sure am."</p>
<p>"And that includes seeing other women?"</p>
<p>"Marie, I will go where I want. See who I want. And do what I want. And there ain't a damn thing you can do about it."</p>
<p>"Oh, Jacques. You should not have said that. You really shouldn't have said that. You have to learn — never, ever — to make me angry."</p>

<p>"Marie?"</p>
<p>"Yes?"</p>
<p>"Are you okay?"</p>
<p>"No, Blanche. I'm not okay."</p>
<p>"Where's Jacques?"</p>
<p>"I don't know. And I don't care."</p>
<p>"Marie, it's your wedding day. You just got married. And you don't know where your husband is and you don't care?"</p>
<p>"No, Blanche. I don't."</p>
<p>"Marie, what's happened to you?"</p>
<p>"What do you mean?"</p>
<p>"You look different. Your eyes —"</p>
<p>"I don't know what's happened to me, Blanche. But I sure as hell feel different. I feel angry. And — I don't know — I also feel strong. From now on, I'm not gonna take any nonsense from Jacques or anyone. And first of all, I'm gonna take care of that little tramp he was talking to."</p>
<p>"You know her name?"</p>
<p>"I — no, I don't know her name."</p>
<p>"I think you do, Blanche. Tell me."</p>
<p>"Marie —"</p>
<p>"Tell me."</p>
<p>"I think her name is Gina Baptiste."</p>
<p>"Do you know where she lives?"</p>
<p>"No, really I don't."</p>
<p>"Blanche, find out where she lives for me."</p>
<p>"Okay, but Marie —"</p>
<p>"Don't try to tell me what to do. It's too late for that."</p>
<p>"Okay, Marie. I'll find out where she lives."</p>

<p>A few days later, Gina was in her room at the rooming house.</p>

<p>"Who's there? Is that you, Jacques? Jacques? Jacques? Are you —"</p>
<p>"Oh!"</p>
<p>"Help! Someone help me!"</p>
<p>"What's going on up there?"</p>
<p>"This is disgusting!"</p>
<p>"What's all the fuss about?"</p>
<p>"Did you see anyone coming up to my door?"</p>
<p>"Lady, there's always people coming to your door."</p>
<p>"Someone just knocked on my door. Did you see anyone?"</p>
<p>"Well, I saw a woman going up the stairs."</p>
<p>"What did she look like?"</p>
<p>"I didn't see her face. Anyway, what you yelling about?"</p>
<p>"Look! My door is — there's blood everywhere!"</p>
<p>"What is it? Well, it was an animal. Chicken, I guess. Now it's just blood and feathers and bones and stuff. And look at my door! There's blood on my door!"</p>
<p>"Oh my God! What is it?"</p>
<p>"Someone wrote the letter M on your door."</p>
<p>"What does it mean?"</p>
<p>"Lady, you ain't from New Orleans, are you?"</p>
<p>"No! Why did they write M on my door?"</p>
<p>"Don't you know nothing? It's the first letter of the word mort — that's French — it means death. And it's written on your door in blood. Man, someone just put a curse on you."</p>

<p>Of course, it was Marie who wrote the letter M in blood on Gina's door. Afterwards, she went out into the street and ran in the direction of her house. But she didn't make it.</p>

<p>"I'm sorry, I didn't see you."</p>
<p>"No, just hold on there, ma'am. I'm sorry. I didn't mean to run into you. Now — if you don't mind, I'll just be going."</p>
<p>"I said hold on there. And calm down. Now, are you okay?"</p>
<p>"I'm fine."</p>
<p>"Ma'am, you have blood all over your hands and your clothes."</p>
<p>"Oh my God."</p>
<p>"I think you should tell me what's going on here."</p>
<p>"No, take your hands off me and let me go."</p>
<p>"Ma'am, maybe you don't know me. But I can help you."</p>
<p>"What are you talking about? Who are you?"</p>
<p>"My name is John Bayou."</p>
<p>"John Bayou?"</p>
<p>"Yes, but around here, people call me Dr John."</p>
<p>"Dr John. Oh Lord Jesus, you're a voodoo doctor."</p>
<p>"Ma'am, I'm just a preacher in a church for freed slaves. I don't really like the expression 'voodoo doctor'."</p>
<p>"But it's what you are. Leave me alone. I don't want to talk to you."</p>
<p>"Ma'am, you look like you're in big trouble."</p>
<p>"I don't want help from no voodoo doctor."</p>
<p>"Now you listen to me. You need help. I can smell death on you."</p>
<p>"I ain't killed no one."</p>
<p>"Just calm down and listen. Right now, I'm probably the only person in your life who can help you. Now come with me."</p>
<p>"Where? Where are you taking me?"</p>
<p>"Just across the street. Somewhere we can talk."</p>
<p>"Across the street? To that bar?"</p>
<p>"That's right."</p>
<p>"I'm not going into a bar. I'm a respectable woman."</p>
<p>"I know you're a respectable woman. But you need to clean up and we need to talk. You can't go anywhere looking like that."</p>
<p>"Oh God."</p>
<p>"There are people across the street who can help you. Now let's go."</p>

<p>"Here she is, Dr John. She's all cleaned up now."</p>
<p>"Thank you, Celeste."</p>
<p>"Anytime, Dr John. Anything else I can do for you?"</p>
<p>"No, thank you, Celeste. You've been more than helpful."</p>
<p>"Okay. Well, you know where I am if you need me."</p>

<p>"I don't like it here. People are looking at me."</p>
<p>"Only in your imagination. They've all got plenty of other things to think about."</p>
<p>"I want to say sorry. I wasn't very nice to you."</p>
<p>"You don't need to apologize. You just need to tell me what's going on. You could start by telling me your name."</p>
<p>"My name is Marie Paris."</p>
<p>"Marie Paris?"</p>
<p>"I'm married to a man named Jacques Paris. Maybe you've heard of him?"</p>
<p>"Oh my goodness. Yes, everyone in New Orleans knows Jacques Paris."</p>
<p>"I didn't realize he had such a fine — look — why —"</p>
<p>"Well, we've only been married for a week or so."</p>
<p>"But my — Jacques is not an easy man to be married to."</p>
<p>"Well, I'm sure you're right about that."</p>
<p>"Mr Bayou, this is not a laughing matter. I've just done something terrible."</p>
<p>"Okay, if you want me to help, you have to tell me what happened."</p>
<p>"Okay. There was a woman at our wedding party. I didn't know her. I don't know how she got there. Anyway, she knew my husband and — I think my husband was planning to see her, like, secretly."</p>
<p>"I see. Go on."</p>
<p>"I went to the place where she lives and —"</p>
<p>"And? What did you do, Marie?"</p>
<p>"I drew the letter M on her door. In blood."</p>
<p>"Ah."</p>
<p>"I just wanted to frighten her."</p>
<p>"Right. Well, I guess I can understand that. But there's something else?"</p>
<p>"What?"</p>
<p>"Well, I waited to see what happened. I sat on the stairs just above her room."</p>
<p>"Well, I was sitting there. I started to imagine things. I couldn't stop myself."</p>
<p>"What did you imagine, Marie?"</p>
<p>"I imagined that I was stabbing her. Like, with a knife."</p>
<p>"Well, you only imagined that."</p>
<p>"I know. But she screamed."</p>
<p>"She did what?"</p>
<p>"She screamed. I could hear her through the door. I imagined stabbing her with a knife. And I could hear her screaming."</p>
<p>"Ah! Ah! Ah! Ah! I can't stand it anymore. I've got to get out of here."</p>

<p>"Hello, Gina."</p>
<p>"You! It's you!"</p>
<p>"What are you doing to me?"</p>
<p>"I ain't doing nothing, Gina."</p>
<p>"You're killing me."</p>
<p>"I ain't putting a finger on you, Gina. But you're gonna die. I'm certain of that. You're a bad woman. And you deserve to die."</p>
<p>"Stop it! And let me out of here!"</p>
<p>"You ain't going nowhere, you tramp."</p>
<p>"Let me out of here!"</p>
<p>"You ain't leaving."</p>
<p>"I have to get out of here. The window. I can get out through the window."</p>
<p>"Someone help me!"</p>
<p>"You okay, ma'am?"</p>
<p>"I'm dying. I have to get out of here."</p>
<p>"Hey, ma'am, you be careful. You're gonna fall."</p>
<p>"Stop!"</p>
<p>"Stop!"</p>

<p>"Someone get a doctor!"</p>
<p>"It's too late. There ain't nothing a doctor can do now."</p>

<p>"So. She jumped from her balcony. And now she's dead. Are you sure that's what happened?"</p>
<p>"Yes. I came out of the rooming house and I saw her."</p>
<p>"Did anyone see you leave the rooming house?"</p>
<p>"No. Everyone was looking at her lying dead in the street."</p>
<p>"Oh no."</p>
<p>"I'm not sorry she's dead. She was a bitch and a tramp. But I didn't kill her. I just feel that suddenly I have this power. And I'm scared. I don't want it."</p>
<p>"Well, Marie. This ain't something you can choose. You don't go looking for this kind of power. The power finds you. Listen, you don't understand what's happening to you right now. But I do. You need help."</p>
<p>"What kind of help?"</p>
<p>"You need to come with me."</p>
<p>"To Place Congo?"</p>
<p>"Place Congo? I ain't going there. It's too dangerous. They're crazy people doing crazy stuff there. Crazy dancing, beating on drums. I'm too scared to go there."</p>
<p>"Marie, it's just a place where people go to worship the god they believe in."</p>
<p>"I ain't going."</p>
<p>"Okay. But if you change your mind, meet me there on Sunday night."</p>

<p>Soon after Marie arrived home, Jack walked through the door.</p>

<p>"Marie. Marie. Where are you?"</p>
<p>"I'm here. In the pantry. What do you want?"</p>
<p>"What the hell do you think you're doing?"</p>
<p>"I'm not doing anything, Jack. I'm just sitting here minding my own business."</p>
<p>"Minding your own business? Is killing someone minding your own business?"</p>
<p>"What are you talking about, Jack?"</p>
<p>"You know what I'm talking about."</p>
<p>"Has someone died?"</p>
<p>"You know damn well that someone has died. Gina is dead."</p>
<p>"Gina? Is that the woman I found you with at our wedding?"</p>
<p>"You know who I'm talking about."</p>
<p>"How did she die?"</p>
<p>"Stop playing games with me, Marie."</p>
<p>"I ain't playing games, Jack. Did she have an accident?"</p>
<p>"No, Marie. She didn't have an accident. Folks who were in the street say that she came out on her balcony screaming fit to wake the dead. She was yelling that someone was stabbing her with a knife."</p>
<p>"Was someone stabbing her, Jack?"</p>
<p>"No, Marie. No one was stabbing her. But she thought someone was stabbing her."</p>
<p>"And?"</p>
<p>"And she threw herself off the balcony. She's dead, Marie."</p>
<p>"And you expect me to feel sad about that?"</p>
<p>"Listen to me —"</p>
<p>"No, Jack, you listen to me. You wanted to go with that worthless tramp on our wedding day. On our wedding day, Jack. The day that should have been the most important day of my life. And you ruined it all."</p>
<p>"You didn't have to kill her."</p>
<p>"Jack, I didn't lay a finger on that whore. I'm glad she's dead. It's what she deserves. But I did not touch her."</p>
<p>"You killed her. You did it somehow. I know you did. And I ain't staying in the same house as you any longer."</p>

<p>"John?"</p>
<p>"Well, Marie. Nice to see you. Welcome to Place Congo."</p>
<p>"What's going on here? It's scary."</p>
<p>"It's just people having a good time, Marie. There ain't nothing to worry about."</p>

<p>"Hey, lady, come up here."</p>
<p>"You tell her, Matthew."</p>
<p>"Take your hands off me."</p>
<p>"Oh, come now, lady. I ain't gonna hurt you."</p>
<p>"You heard what the lady said, sir."</p>
<p>"Oh yeah? And who the hell do you think you are?"</p>
<p>"Hey, lay off, Matthew. That's John Bayou."</p>
<p>"John Bayou? Oh my God."</p>
<p>"Dr John. Sorry, Mr Bayou."</p>
<p>"I think you better say sorry to the lady as well, sir."</p>
<p>"Sorry, lady. Sorry I spoke to you."</p>
<p>"Come on."</p>

<p>"Well, John, you certainly have the power, too."</p>
<p>"Maybe."</p>
<p>"Anyway, you don't need to feel afraid."</p>
<p>"I don't feel afraid when I'm with you. But what's going on here?"</p>
<p>"Well, most people here are just here to talk, dance, be with their friends. But there's some religious stuff going on, too."</p>
<p>"John?"</p>
<p>"What is it?"</p>
<p>"That man over there, the one near the fire. What about him?"</p>
<p>"He's dancing and he's holding a snake. The snake is wrapped around his body."</p>
<p>"That's right."</p>
<p>"What's he doing?"</p>
<p>"Don't worry, Marie. He's a voodoo preacher. Snakes are very special creatures to voodoo people."</p>
<p>"But what's happening?"</p>
<p>"The voodoo service has started."</p>
<p>"And he's not afraid of the snake?"</p>
<p>"No, Marie, he ain't afraid of the snake. And you mustn't be afraid, either."</p>
<p>"What do you mean, I mustn't be afraid?"</p>
<p>"Marie, he may choose to give the snake to someone. It's part of the service. He may choose to give the snake to you."</p>
<p>"Give me the snake? Why?"</p>
<p>"Don't be afraid if he asks you to take the snake."</p>
<p>"Are you crazy? No way. I'm not going anywhere near a snake. Snakes scare me to death."</p>
<p>"Marie, you have nothing to fear from the snake. You have the power to control it."</p>
<p>"John, I can't do it. I can't stay here."</p>

<p>"Marie, there ain't no place you can go. If the preacher wants to find you, he'll find you."</p>
<p>"Why are you saying this? You said I should come here to get help. Then you scare me nearly to death."</p>
<p>"Marie, trust me."</p>
<p>"Why? Why should I trust you?"</p>

<p>"Damballah Weddo!"</p>
<p>"Damballah Weddo!"</p>
<p>"Damballah Weddo!"</p>
<p>"Damballah Weddo!"</p>
<p>"Damballah Weddo!"</p>
<p>"For God's sake, what did he say?"</p>
<p>"He's speaking to Damballah Weddo. He's the voodoo snake god."</p>

<p>"He is coming towards you, Marie."</p>
<p>"John, help me!"</p>
<p>"Lady with the long black hair."</p>
<p>"What do you want?"</p>
<p>"Take the snake."</p>
<p>"No!"</p>
<p>"Take the snake!"</p>
<p>"Take the snake!"</p>
<p>"Take the snake!"</p>
<p>"Take the snake!"</p>
<p>"Take the snake!"</p>
<p>"Take the snake!"</p>
<p>"Oh my God!"</p>
<p>"Feel the power, Marie! You have the power! And the power will save you!"</p>
<p>"Take it!"</p>
<p>"Stop!"</p>

<p>"Good, Marie! You're right, Marie!"</p>
<p>"Damballah Weddo. Damballah Weddo. I hear you. I hear you! Damballah Weddo! Damballah Weddo!"</p>

<p>Marie left Place Congo a changed woman. The next day she received a visitor.</p>

<p>"Hold on. Okay, okay, I'm coming. Can I help you?"</p>
<p>"Are you Marie Laveau?"</p>
<p>"Maybe. Who wants to know?"</p>
<p>"Let me in."</p>
<p>"Excuse me. Did I say you could come in?"</p>
<p>"I have something to tell you."</p>
<p>"You do?"</p>
<p>"Yes. I run the church for freed slaves. My name is Sanité Dédé. Some people call me Sanité Dédé."</p>
<p>"Aha! So you're Sanité Dédé. The voodoo queen."</p>
<p>"Well, well, well. So, why are you visiting Marie Laveau? I guess you don't want me to cut your hair."</p>
<p>"Don't you make fun of me, woman. Close the door."</p>
<p>"I heard about what you did last night in Place Congo."</p>
<p>"Is that right?"</p>
<p>"People are talking about you as if you're the voodoo queen of New Orleans."</p>
<p>"They are? They are!"</p>
<p>"And you ain't."</p>
<p>"Is that all you've come to tell me?"</p>
<p>"If it is, then maybe you'd listen to me, woman. And listen good. I heard you did something really bad."</p>
<p>"Really?"</p>
<p>"Yes. I heard you killed a woman called Gina Baptiste."</p>
<p>"See? Now you know you're in trouble. I think you don't want to believe everything you hear."</p>
<p>"This Gina woman — she was messing with your husband, right?"</p>
<p>"That's enough. I don't want you in my house anymore. Get out of here."</p>
<p>"Listen. You're the one that needs to get out. It's time for you to pack your bags and leave town."</p>
<p>"Leave town? I ain't going nowhere."</p>
<p>"I'm giving you 24 hours to get out of here and go someplace else. And if you don't — if you don't — I'm going to the sheriff's office. And I'm going to tell the sheriff that I know who killed Gina Baptiste."</p>
<p>"Oh, come on. Is that the best you can do? That little piece of scum killed herself. Either that or it was an accident. Everyone knows that. She either jumped off her balcony or she fell. There are plenty of witnesses who can testify to that."</p>
<p>"Witnesses, huh?"</p>
<p>"Yeah. Lots of people saw her."</p>
<p>"And you think that's what they're going to say in court? When you appear in court on trial for murder? Because when I tell the sheriff that you did it, you're going to be tried. And you can bring all the witnesses you like. They ain't going to save you."</p>
<p>"Is that right? And how do you know that?"</p>
<p>"Because they're going to say what I tell them to say. They're going to say they saw you attack Gina Baptiste with a knife. And then you'll be tried. Convicted. And hanged."</p>
<p>"I told you. You don't scare me one bit. So you ain't going to leave."</p>
<p>"Too right, I ain't going to leave. I'm staying right here."</p>
<p>"Well, you can't say I didn't give you a chance. I guess you have about 30 minutes before they come and get you. It's your last 30 minutes of freedom. Enjoy them."</p>
<p>"Wait."</p>
<p>"What?"</p>
<p>"Come back in here."</p>
<p>"So. Changed your mind, have you?"</p>
<p>"I said — come back in here."</p>
<p>"Good. You finally come to your —"</p>
<p>"What the hell do you think you're doing?"</p>
<p>"You dare to hit me? Well, sister, you are in real trouble now."</p>
<p>"You just listen to me, you old witch. You only have power over people because they're afraid of you. That's all. But I ain't afraid of you. Not one bit. You don't scare me and there's nothing you can do to me."</p>
<p>"You're making a big mistake."</p>
<p>"Really? Okay. Try me. Go to the sheriff's office."</p>
<p>"I will."</p>
<p>"You're a dead woman, Marie Laveau. Don't say I didn't warn you. You still have time to leave town."</p>
<p>"I don't need to leave town. All I need to do is think about you."</p>

<p>Sanité Dédé walked quickly down the street, pushing people out of her way.</p>
<p>"Hey, lady, what's the hurry?"</p>
<p>"Get out of my way."</p>
<p>"What was that?"</p>
<p>"Leave it, Jake. Leave it. Leave it."</p>
<p>"That crazy old woman nearly knocked me into the street."</p>
<p>"Leave it."</p>

<p>She walked down Bourbon Street and turned into the square, and then into Royal Street. She could see the sheriff's office on the other side of the street. She walked more quickly, laughing to herself. That woman is so stupid. She's dead meat. But Marie had other plans.</p>

<p>"All I need to do is think about you."</p>
<p>"What? What was that? Oh!"</p>
<p>"Think about you?"</p>
<p>"What the — oh!"</p>
<p>"And you did it."</p>
<p>"Stop! Stop doing this to me! You mad whore!"</p>
<p>"Hey, look out, lady! Look out!"</p>
<p>"Look out!"</p>

<p>They never made it to the sheriff's office. She was hit by a carriage that was travelling at high speed down Royal Street. Our witnesses said that the woman was screaming when she stepped into the road before the carriage hit her. They reckoned she was screaming so loud that she didn't hear the horses.</p>

<p>Back home, Marie Laveau put on her best dress, combed her hair, and walked out into the street. And everyone who saw her knew that things had changed. No one knew it at the time, but on that day, the new voodoo queen of New Orleans was crowned.</p>

  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">This is the story of Marie Laveau, the voodoo queen of New Orleans. She was a hairdresser when our story began, but her life changed one night in Place Congo. Congo Square, right in the centre of the French Quarter of the city. A voodoo preacher came up to her. He was carrying a snake.</p>

<p>"You, lady with the long black hair."</p>
<p>"What do you want?"</p>
<p>"Take the snake."</p>
<p>"No."</p>
<p>"Take it."</p>
<p>"Take the snake."</p>
<p>"Take the snake."</p>
<p>"Take the snake."</p>
<p>"Take the snake."</p>
<p>"Take the snake."</p>
<p>"Take the snake."</p>
<p>"Oh my God."</p>
<p>"Take it."</p>
<p>"Stop!"</p>
<p>"Go, baby!"</p>
<p>"Damballah Weddo. Damballah Weddo. I hear you. I hear you. Damballah Weddo! Damballah Weddo!"</p>

<p>Marie danced with the snake round her body. And after that, nothing was ever the same for her.</p>

<p>The next day seemed like a normal day. But then Marie received a visit from a woman called Sanité Dédé. Now, at the time, she called herself the voodoo queen of New Orleans.</p>

<p>"Hold on. Okay, okay, I said hold on. Can I help you?"</p>
<p>"Are you Marie Laveau?"</p>
<p>"Maybe. Who wants to know?"</p>
<p>"Sanité Dédé, the voodoo queen of New Orleans."</p>

<p>Sanité Dédé, the voodoo queen of New Orleans, wasn't going to give up her crown without a fight.</p>

<p>"I heard what you did last night in Place Congo."</p>
<p>"Is that right?"</p>
<p>"People are talking about you like you're the voodoo queen of New Orleans."</p>
<p>"They are. They are."</p>
<p>"And you ain't."</p>
<p>"I'm the head of the church of freed slaves in this town. And if you try to take my place, I'll make your life hell."</p>
<p>"You don't scare me one little bit."</p>
<p>"I also heard you did something really bad."</p>
<p>"Really?"</p>
<p>"Yes. I heard you killed a woman called Gina Baptiste."</p>
<p>"You don't want to believe everything you hear."</p>
<p>"Listen, woman. It's time for you to pack your bags and leave town."</p>
<p>"Leave town? I ain't going nowhere."</p>
<p>"If you don't, I'm going to the sheriff's office. And I'm going to tell the sheriff that you killed Gina Baptiste. You'll be tried, convicted, and hanged."</p>

<p>But Sanité Dédé never made it to the sheriff's office.</p>

<p>"All I need to do is think about you."</p>
<p>"What the —"</p>
<p>"Stop! Stop doing this to me! You mad whore!"</p>
<p>"Hey! Look out, lady! Look out!"</p>
<p>"Look out!"</p>

<p>Now Marie Laveau was the undisputed voodoo queen of New Orleans. People who believed in the power of voodoo started to come to her for help. Some of them were in real trouble.</p>

<p>"Mrs Laveau, you're going to help me. My boy Jacob is in jail for something he didn't do. They say that he killed someone. But he ain't ever killed anyone. And they're going to hang him."</p>
<p>"I'll do what I can."</p>
<p>"Please help me, Mrs Laveau. He's my only son."</p>
<p>"I said I'll do what I can. Just bring me something that belongs to him."</p>
<p>"Bring you something? Like what?"</p>
<p>"A piece of clothing. Something like that."</p>
<p>"A piece of clothing? You mean like a shirt?"</p>
<p>"Yes. Something like that."</p>
<p>"Don't worry. Your son won't hang. I'll make sure of that."</p>
<p>"Thank you, Mrs Laveau. Thank you."</p>

<p>Marie did everything she could to help people. At a cost. When she was successful, people paid her. In fact, she made a lot of money, so much money that she moved into a new house on Bourbon Street. Her only companion there was a black cat named Bayou.</p>

<p>"Bayou? Come here. I got you something real tasty."</p>

<p>Then one day she had a visitor who was looking for something a little more personal.</p>

<p>"Well, Bayou. Who can that be? Hold on. I'm coming."</p>
<p>"Well, well, well. Guess who's here?"</p>

<p>It was Jacques Paris, the no-good low-life who also happened to be Marie's husband.</p>

<p>"Jacques, what the hell do you want?"</p>
<p>"Now come on, Marie. Is that any way to welcome back your loving husband?"</p>
<p>"Take your hands off me."</p>
<p>"Oh, come on, Marie. This is no way for a wife to behave."</p>
<p>"Go away, Jacques. Now. And don't come back."</p>
<p>"I'll leave just as soon as I've said what I came to say."</p>
<p>"And what's that?"</p>
<p>"Well — do you mind if I come inside?"</p>
<p>"If you have something to tell me, you can tell me right here at the door."</p>
<p>"Now you listen to me, Marie —"</p>
<p>"You have five minutes to tell me what you want. And then I'm gonna call someone to throw you out."</p>
<p>"What? You gonna call the sheriff?"</p>
<p>"I'm sure he'll be very happy to help."</p>
<p>"I don't need the sheriff. I have friends who can turn you into dog meat if I ask them to."</p>
<p>"Don't threaten me, Marie."</p>
<p>"I'll threaten you all I want, Jacques. Now, what do you want?"</p>
<p>"Okay. Well, in case you forgot, we got married a few months back."</p>
<p>"Oh, that's right. We did."</p>
<p>"And in case you forgot, you hardly spent a week in my house before you disappeared."</p>
<p>"Yeah, well, that's right. But I don't even think about spending another night under your roof."</p>
<p>"Now hold on there, Marie. At the time, I thought that somehow you were responsible for the death of a friend of mine."</p>
<p>"If you're talking about that whore Gina Baptiste, you'd better go."</p>
<p>"It's okay, Marie. I now know I was wrong. Gina's death was an accident. So I've come back to apologize for what I said. And I thought maybe we could start over. Turn over a new leaf, you know. Wipe the slate clean."</p>
<p>"Huh. Wipe the slate clean. The only reason I would wipe a slate clean would be to break it over your head."</p>
<p>"Oh, come now, Marie."</p>
<p>"Jacques, marrying you was the biggest mistake I ever made. When you leave this house — in about one minute from now — you ain't coming back."</p>
<p>"Well, don't be too sure of that, Marie. Don't forget that you and me are still man and wife."</p>
<p>"That's nonsense, and you know it."</p>
<p>"It ain't nonsense, Marie. We're man and wife. So what's yours is also mine."</p>
<p>"What?"</p>
<p>"We're married, Marie. And that means till death us do part. What's yours is mine. Well, half mine. I own half of all of this."</p>
<p>"Are you crazy?"</p>
<p>"And you have a mighty fine place here, Marie."</p>
<p>"Get out of here, Jacques. I don't owe you anything. And you are not gonna get a silver dime out of me."</p>
<p>"Well, Marie, you may have forgotten. But in this city, there are laws about this kind of thing. I guess I'll just take my claim to court and see what they say."</p>
<p>"You can do what the hell you like. I ain't giving you a cent."</p>
<p>"Ah, now, this may be someone who can help you change your mind."</p>
<p>"What did you say?"</p>
<p>"There's someone who wants to talk to you. Open the door."</p>

<p>"Good morning. Can I help you?"</p>
<p>"You must be Mrs Paris."</p>
<p>"My name is Marie Laveau. Who are you?"</p>
<p>"My name is George Andrews."</p>
<p>"How can I help you, Mr Andrews?"</p>
<p>"Well, I think my client, Mr Paris, is here."</p>
<p>"You're what?"</p>
<p>"My client. I'm an attorney at law. I'm advising Mr Paris on his claim to half your estate."</p>
<p>"Why, you son of a bitch."</p>

<p>"Get out! Both of you!"</p>
<p>"Mrs Paris, you're making a big mistake here."</p>
<p>"Don't you call me Mrs Paris. My name is Marie Laveau. Now get out!"</p>
<p>"Mrs Paris —"</p>
<p>"Did you hear me? Get out!"</p>
<p>"Mrs Paris — since the day of your marriage to Mr Paris, he has been legally entitled to one half of your estate."</p>
<p>"I told you — don't call me Mrs Paris —"</p>
<p>"Jack —"</p>
<p>"That's it. I want you to leave now and take your fat friend with you."</p>
<p>"You can't get rid of the problem by throwing us out, Marie."</p>
<p>"You want to bet money on that? You can bet your life on it."</p>
<p>"Mrs Paris — I will instruct my client to press charges against you."</p>
<p>"Keep talking if you want me to hit you again."</p>
<p>"Fine. Okay, just hold on there."</p>
<p>"Goodbye, gentlemen. Have a nice day."</p>
<p>"Marie, you better think again about what you're doing. The next time we meet we'll be in court."</p>
<p>"The next time we meet, Jacques Paris, we'll be in hell."</p>

<p>Marie noticed that Jack had left his hat, and she thought about opening the door and throwing it out onto the street. But she didn't.</p>

<p>"Well, Bayou. What do you think we should do with Jack? Because he's gonna have to die. And die very soon."</p>

<p>She took a sharp knife and began to cut the hat into small pieces. Then she put the pieces into a bowl of water.</p>

<p>There's a lake near New Orleans called Lake Pontchartrain. A few days later, early in the morning before it was light, two fishermen were out on the lake in a boat.</p>

<p>"Hey, help me with this net."</p>
<p>"What's the problem?"</p>
<p>"I don't know. We got something real heavy in it."</p>
<p>"Hey, what's the matter with you? You losing your strength? You can't pull in the net without me helping you?"</p>
<p>"Yeah, let me help me with this net. It's too heavy."</p>
<p>"Hey, come on. What can be so heavy that you can't pull it in?"</p>
<p>"Just shut up and help me."</p>
<p>"Okay, let's do it. Pull."</p>
<p>"Jesus. You're right. What the hell did we catch today?"</p>
<p>"Oh, will you shut up and keep pulling?"</p>
<p>"Okay, here it comes."</p>
<p>"Stop it!"</p>
<p>"What's the matter?"</p>
<p>"Look. In the net. Is that — is that someone's arm?"</p>
<p>"Oh my God!"</p>
<p>"Jesus, Mary and Joseph. It's a man."</p>
<p>"Come on. Help me get him in the boat."</p>
<p>"In the boat? No way!"</p>
<p>"What you talking about? We have to take him back to the shore."</p>
<p>"I ain't going nowhere with a dead body in the boat."</p>
<p>"Come on, boy. What you thinking about? We can't leave his body here in the lake."</p>
<p>"We sure can."</p>
<p>"For pity's sake —"</p>
<p>"What is it?"</p>
<p>"I just saw his face. His eyes are staring at me."</p>
<p>"Push him back in the water. Let someone else find him."</p>
<p>"Okay."</p>
<p>"Jesus, man. That was terrible. His eyes were staring at me and his mouth was wide open. He looked like he was in fear of the devil when he died."</p>
<p>"Stop talking about it. I don't want to know. Let's get out of here."</p>
<p>"The worst thing is — I recognized him."</p>
<p>"You did?"</p>
<p>"Yes."</p>
<p>"Who was it?"</p>
<p>"His name? Jacques Paris."</p>

<p>The next day, Marie visited one of her hairdressing clients, a woman called Margaret Salinger. She lived in a fancy house in Dauphine Street, and her husband was a businessman. They seemed to have a lot of money. Mrs Salinger liked gossiping with Marie.</p>

<p>"You know something, Marie."</p>
<p>"What's that, Margaret?"</p>
<p>"You have a talent that no one else in the world has."</p>
<p>"I do? And what talent is that?"</p>
<p>"You're the only person in this city who can make my hair look good."</p>
<p>"Oh, come on. I'm nothing special."</p>
<p>"Not true. I do believe that without you, I would look like a witch."</p>
<p>"Margaret, stop it. You're a fine-looking woman."</p>
<p>"Well, I don't know about that. But have you seen my daughter Laverne recently? She just turned 19, and she's a real good-looking girl."</p>
<p>"I have. And you're right. She's a real beauty."</p>

<p>"Margaret, I want to — oh, I'm sorry. I didn't realize there was someone with you."</p>
<p>"What do you want, Henry?"</p>
<p>"Margaret, I need to speak to you privately for a moment."</p>
<p>"Marie is doing my hair. Can't it wait?"</p>
<p>"No, I'm afraid it can't."</p>
<p>"Is it something so secret that my hairdresser can't hear it?"</p>
<p>"Well —"</p>
<p>"Whatever it is, I'm sure I won't understand it. And I'm sure I'll forget it the minute I hear it."</p>
<p>"Yes, but if you don't mind — I need to talk to my wife alone."</p>
<p>"Oh, well, Marie — I guess I better listen to him."</p>
<p>"Perhaps you would like some lemon tea? Okay? I'll come back later."</p>
<p>"Go down to the kitchen, Marie. Betsy will look after you."</p>

<p>"Marie, how you doing?"</p>
<p>"Fine, Betsy."</p>
<p>"You want some iced tea?"</p>
<p>"That would be fine."</p>
<p>"Marie, I was so sorry to hear about Jacques."</p>
<p>"Yes, well, you know, we weren't living together any more."</p>
<p>"Well, yeah, I did hear that. But did they ever find out what happened to him?"</p>
<p>"Well, I just think he got drunk as a skunk and fell in the lake."</p>
<p>"Marie, that's a terrible thing to say —"</p>
<p>"Betsy, can I — oh, sorry."</p>
<p>"Oh, hi, Laverne. Laverne, you know Marie, don't you?"</p>
<p>"Yes, indeed. Hello, Mrs Paris."</p>
<p>"Laverne — how many times do I have to tell you? Call me Marie."</p>
<p>"Right. Okay. Marie. I'm sorry to hear about your husband."</p>
<p>"Thank you, Laverne."</p>
<p>"It must be so hard to lose someone you love."</p>
<p>"Yes, I guess it is."</p>
<p>"Young Laverne is in love. Ain't you, Laverne?"</p>
<p>"No, Betsy, stop it."</p>
<p>"Are you in love, Laverne?"</p>
<p>"Well, yes, I am."</p>
<p>"Who's the lucky boy?"</p>
<p>"His name is Tom Martin, and he works on one of the boats that sail to the islands. You know, the sugar boats."</p>
<p>"He works on a boat?"</p>
<p>"Yes. I know it doesn't sound like much of a job, but he loves the sea. He wants to be captain of his own ship one day."</p>
<p>"I guess that means he's away a lot."</p>
<p>"Yes, he is. But he's coming home soon."</p>
<p>"That's great, Laverne. I hope you have a fine time when he comes back."</p>
<p>"I sure will. If —"</p>
<p>"If what, Laverne?"</p>
<p>"Nothing."</p>
<p>"Well, the thing is — Dad wants me to marry someone who's going to make a lot of money. And Tom sure isn't going to do that."</p>

<p>"So, Henry, what is it that's so important it can't wait till I've had my hair done?"</p>
<p>"Margaret, there's something I have to tell you."</p>
<p>"What? What is it, Henry?"</p>
<p>"Margaret, we are in deep trouble."</p>
<p>"Why? What's happened?"</p>
<p>"I — I have lost a lot of money."</p>
<p>"How much?"</p>
<p>"A lot."</p>
<p>"How much?"</p>
<p>"Ten thousand dollars."</p>
<p>"Ten — you lost ten thousand dollars? I didn't know you had ten thousand dollars."</p>
<p>"I didn't. I borrowed the money, and then I lost every cent."</p>
<p>"But how?"</p>
<p>"I went into a land deal with a man — a terrible man called George Andrews — the man who was here this afternoon."</p>
<p>"That ugly little fat guy?"</p>
<p>"Yes. He's a lawyer. And he's a crook. He stole all the money."</p>
<p>"Okay, so he committed a crime. Go tell the sheriff."</p>
<p>"Can't do that. The deal wasn't completely legal. I didn't realize it at the time. I — I guess I didn't read the small print in the contract."</p>
<p>"Henry, what are we going to do? This house isn't even worth ten thousand dollars."</p>
<p>"There is — a solution."</p>
<p>"What's that?"</p>
<p>"Andrews will pay me the money back and pay off all my other debts."</p>
<p>"And why would he do that?"</p>
<p>"Well, first of all, he has agreed to buy the land we own out west of the city."</p>
<p>"That piece of land near the Atchafalaya Swamp?"</p>
<p>"Yes."</p>
<p>"He wants to buy that land for ten thousand dollars? That's great!"</p>
<p>"Not exactly. There's something else that he wants."</p>
<p>"What?"</p>
<p>"Well —"</p>
<p>"Henry, what is it that he wants?"</p>
<p>"This afternoon, he met Laverne."</p>
<p>"And?"</p>
<p>"He — well, he has asked me for her hand in marriage."</p>
<p>"What? Henry, Laverne is nineteen."</p>
<p>"Well, yes."</p>
<p>"And he is old and fat and disgusting. Henry, I am not going to allow that horrible man to marry my daughter."</p>
<p>"Margaret, things aren't as simple as that. I'll explain. The thing is —"</p>

<p>"Can I come in?"</p>
<p>"Yes."</p>
<p>"Margaret, what's the matter?"</p>
<p>"I just heard some terrible news."</p>
<p>"What? What's happened?"</p>
<p>"Henry just told me that someone wants to marry Laverne."</p>
<p>"You mean the young man who works on the sugar boat?"</p>
<p>"No, Marie. Not Tom. A horrible old man called George Andrews."</p>
<p>"George Andrews? Yes. I'm an attorney at law — I'm advising Mr Paris on his claim to half your estate."</p>
<p>"Why, you son of a bitch."</p>
<p>"Yes. He's a disgusting little man. I know George Andrews."</p>
<p>"You do?"</p>
<p>"Yes."</p>
<p>"In that case, you will understand why I'm so upset."</p>
<p>"You can't let that happen, Margaret. You cannot let that happen."</p>
<p>"Do you think I want it to happen?"</p>
<p>"If you don't want it, don't allow it."</p>
<p>"It isn't as easy as that, Marie. We're in deep trouble. Henry owes money to this man — a lot of money. Andrews can ruin us if he wants to."</p>
<p>"How?"</p>
<p>"When he asked Henry for permission to speak to Laverne about marriage, Henry said no. So Andrews asked him to think again, and he said if Henry still said no, he was going to ask for all the money that Henry owes him."</p>
<p>"How much does he owe him?"</p>
<p>"$10,000."</p>
<p>"Oh my God."</p>
<p>"If we have to pay him, we'll have nothing. No house, no business, nothing. We'll be out on the street."</p>
<p>"Oh, Margaret —"</p>
<p>"But if we agree to the marriage, Andrews will pay off all Henry's other debts as well. And he will also buy a useless piece of land that we own out near the Atchafalaya Swamp."</p>
<p>"I see."</p>
<p>"I wish I could kill that man. But, you know, that wouldn't help. He has three children by his first marriage. To be clear of the dead, I would have to kill all of them as well. You know what the best thing would be?"</p>
<p>"What's that, Margaret?"</p>
<p>"Have Laverne marry him. And then kill him."</p>
<p>"You know, that ain't such a bad idea."</p>
<p>"That's a crazy idea, Margaret, and you know it."</p>
<p>"Marie —"</p>
<p>"Yes."</p>
<p>"Could you do that for me?"</p>
<p>"What?"</p>
<p>"Kill a man."</p>
<p>"Margaret, are you out of your mind?"</p>
<p>"Don't be all surprised with me, Marie. I know about the things that you do."</p>
<p>"What are you talking about?"</p>
<p>"What am I talking about? You know darn well what I'm talking about, Marie. I know about you. And all of those voodoo ceremonies down in Congo Square."</p>
<p>"Marie?"</p>
<p>"Are you listening to me?"</p>
<p>"I'm listening."</p>
<p>"Will you help me?"</p>
<p>"Margaret, I don't normally work for white people."</p>
<p>"You don't, huh? Well, you're going to start now."</p>
<p>"Okay, Margaret. I'll try to help. You know I'd do anything for you."</p>
<p>"And —"</p>
<p>"And what?"</p>
<p>"I just remembered something. Something that George Andrews said to me just before Jack died."</p>
<p>"Mrs Paris — since the day of your marriage to Mr Paris, he has been legally entitled to one half of your estate."</p>
<p>"I have an idea. I think I know what we can do."</p>

<p>"Mother!"</p>
<p>"Laverne."</p>
<p>"Father just told me the most terrible thing. He wants me to marry Mr Andrews."</p>
<p>"I know."</p>
<p>"I won't do it, Mother. I'd rather cut my own throat than let that man touch me."</p>
<p>"Laverne, calm down."</p>
<p>"Calm down? Calm down? Mother, I won't do it. I love you and I love Dad. But I will not marry that man. The very thought of it makes me sick."</p>
<p>"Laverne, please. Just stop for a moment and listen to Marie."</p>
<p>"Listen to Marie? What for?"</p>
<p>"Laverne —"</p>
<p>"Just sit down for a moment and let me talk to you. I can help you."</p>
<p>"How? What can you do?"</p>
<p>"If you marry this man —"</p>
<p>"Marie, stop it. I don't want to hear it."</p>
<p>"Laverne!"</p>
<p>"Quit squawking and hear me out."</p>
<p>"Sorry."</p>
<p>"In fact, Marie. It is Laverne — you only have to marry him. Go through with the wedding and you will immediately inherit half of his estate."</p>
<p>"And?"</p>
<p>"And then — well —"</p>
<p>"I'll tell Marie."</p>
<p>"If you don't marry Andrews, we will lose everything. The house, the business, everything."</p>
<p>"Why didn't anyone tell me?"</p>
<p>"Laverne, I didn't know about it until today either."</p>
<p>"Well, you two, listen to my plan."</p>
<p>"Sorry."</p>
<p>"Laverne. As soon as you are married to Mr Andrews, I will make sure that — something happens to him."</p>
<p>"What? What's going to happen to him?"</p>
<p>"Trust me, Laverne. He won't be a problem for you. But I need your help."</p>
<p>"What can I do?"</p>
<p>"You must go to Mr Andrews' house and you must agree to marry him."</p>
<p>"Marie, I can't do that. I really can't."</p>
<p>"Hear me out. Agree to marry him. And bring me back something that belongs to him."</p>
<p>"What kind of thing?"</p>
<p>"The simplest thing would be a handkerchief."</p>
<p>"Look, Margaret, I have to go now. But I'll be back later. Trust me, Laverne. Everything will be okay."</p>

<p>"Mama, why am I agreeing to this? I can't bear the idea of that man touching me."</p>
<p>"Laverne, trust Marie. She knows what she's doing."</p>
<p>"You'd better be right. Because if I have to live with that man, I'm going to kill myself."</p>

<p>The next morning, Henry Salinger and his daughter Laverne visited George Andrews.</p>

<p>"Henry. Hello, George. So good to see you."</p>
<p>"Laverne."</p>
<p>"Hello, Mr Andrews."</p>
<p>"Boy, Laverne, you look good enough to eat."</p>
<p>"Can we come in, George?"</p>
<p>"A short thing, Henry. Come in."</p>
<p>"George, we've come to talk about your proposal. And I hope you'll agree it's a good idea."</p>
<p>"Because if you haven't, then it's the end of the road for the Salingers."</p>
<p>"Well, yes, Laverne has agreed to marry you."</p>
<p>"What did you do, Laverne? You just made the best decision of your life. Come here."</p>
<p>"Oh, Mr Andrews —"</p>
<p>"George —"</p>
<p>"Please, can we just discuss a few details?"</p>
<p>"It's okay, Laverne. I can wait. But not for long."</p>
<p>"Let me get you folks a drink to celebrate."</p>
<p>"George, it's 11 o'clock in the morning. Isn't it kind of early to be drinking?"</p>
<p>"Not when there's something like this to celebrate."</p>
<p>"No, George. I think —"</p>
<p>"Oh, no, Dad. I think that's a nice idea. My father would like to join you, Mr Andrews."</p>
<p>"Good. Well, I'm glad she has more sense than you, Henry. Even if she is just a woman."</p>
<p>"Hey, hold on there."</p>
<p>"I'll be back with some bourbon."</p>

<p>"Laverne, what are you thinking about?"</p>
<p>"Don't worry, Dad. I know what I'm doing."</p>
<p>"You know that I don't drink in the morning."</p>
<p>"Well, you're gonna have to start, Dad. I want you to accept a glass of bourbon from Mr Andrews. And then —"</p>

<p>"Here we are. A bottle of my best bourbon. It's 15 years old. Henry, have you any idea how much this stuff costs?"</p>
<p>"No, George. I don't know how much it costs."</p>
<p>"Don't you want to know how much it costs?"</p>
<p>"How much does it cost, George?"</p>
<p>"This beauty is $7 a bottle. $7, but worth every cent. I have about 60 bottles of it in the cellar."</p>
<p>"Can I see the bottle, Mr Andrews?"</p>
<p>"Oh, why, yes, Laverne. Thank you."</p>
<p>"Well, Laverne, I didn't think you would be a bourbon drinker. Let me pour you a glass."</p>
<p>"Oh, no. I don't want to drink it, Mr Andrews. I'm just interested in — in bottles."</p>
<p>"Oh! Be careful!"</p>
<p>"Laverne!"</p>
<p>"Oh my God. That table is worth a fortune."</p>
<p>"I'm so sorry, Mr Andrews. Let me clear up the glass."</p>
<p>"Don't be ridiculous, Laverne. I have servants to do this kind of stuff. Martha!"</p>
<p>"No, no. I broke it. I want to clear it up."</p>
<p>"No, no, Laverne. Stop it. Get up off the floor."</p>
<p>"It's okay, Mr Andrews. Here, take this piece."</p>
<p>"Gah!"</p>
<p>"Laverne, careful. You just cut Mr Andrews' hand."</p>
<p>"Jesus Christ, I'm bleeding to death. Martha!"</p>
<p>"Oh, no. I'm so sorry. Quick, let me help you. Sit down here and give me a handkerchief."</p>
<p>"I — I'll use this one in your top pocket."</p>
<p>"It's okay, Dad. Mr Andrews won't mind."</p>
<p>"Martha, where are you?"</p>
<p>"I'm right here, Mr Andrews."</p>
<p>"Oh, my goodness. What have you done to your hand?"</p>
<p>"What have I done to my hand? You can see with your own eyes. I'm bleeding to death."</p>
<p>"Come with me into the pantry, Mr Andrews. I'll clean that up."</p>
<p>"We'll come back later, George, when things have calmed down a little."</p>

<p>Later that day, Laverne took Mr Andrews' silk handkerchief to Marie's house.</p>

<p>"I got this, Marie."</p>
<p>"Well done, Laverne. How come it's covered in blood?"</p>
<p>"I dropped a seven-dollar bottle of bourbon on a glass coffee table. There was glass everywhere. Then I accidentally cut Mr Andrews' hand with a piece of broken glass. I really enjoyed it. Oh my God, what am I saying?"</p>
<p>"I understand."</p>
<p>"What are you going to do with it?"</p>
<p>"Look at this."</p>
<p>"The doll? What about it?"</p>
<p>"Laverne, this is going to save your life."</p>
<p>"But it's just a doll."</p>
<p>"Yes. And I'm going to put the handkerchief around the doll. Like this."</p>
<p>"And? How does that help, Marie?"</p>
<p>"Laverne, it means I can do something to Mr Andrews. Anytime I want."</p>
<p>"What do you mean? Do something?"</p>
<p>"Watch. First of all, I just put this needle into the doll. Like this?"</p>
<p>"And Mr Andrews is going to get a sharp pain. Right there. In his chest."</p>

<p>"Mother, do you know where to —"</p>
<p>"What's the matter, Mr Andrews?"</p>
<p>"Nothing. I just —"</p>
<p>"You okay? Sit down for a moment."</p>
<p>"I just got a pain here in my chest."</p>
<p>"It's okay. It's gone now."</p>
<p>"Mr Andrews, when are you going to take my advice and stop drinking so much bourbon?"</p>
<p>"Never, Martha, never. I like bourbon too much to ever stop."</p>

<p>A few weeks later, the wedding took place. After the church service, there was a big party in Andrews' house in Canal Street. Laverne was sick with worry.</p>

<p>"Congratulations, Laverne, and good luck."</p>
<p>"Thanks."</p>
<p>"My God, she needs all the luck she can get."</p>
<p>"Hello, Marie."</p>
<p>"Hi, Margaret. How are you feeling?"</p>
<p>"Terrible. But there is some good news. Andrews has already paid off Henry's debts to all the banks in town."</p>
<p>"That's wonderful."</p>

<p>"Marie."</p>
<p>"Hi, Laverne. You look lovely."</p>
<p>"Marie, what's going on? You said everything would be okay. Well, it doesn't feel okay to me."</p>
<p>"Don't panic. It will be okay. I promise."</p>

<p>"Ladies and gentlemen, please raise your glasses for a toast to George and Laverne."</p>
<p>"George and Laverne."</p>

<p>"Marie, are you — oh, where's Marie?"</p>
<p>"Laverne, did you see where Marie went?"</p>
<p>"No."</p>

<p>No one knew where Marie was, but she was nearby. And she had the doll covered with the blood-stained silk handkerchief. And she had a sharp needle.</p>

<p>"Damballah Weddo. Hear my words."</p>

<p>"Now, ladies and gentlemen, pray silence for the groom, Mr George Andrews."</p>
<p>"Thank you. Thank you very much. Well, I'm so glad that you could all come to my wedding. And I hope the food and drink are to your satisfaction. Well, they should be. This whole thing cost me a huge pile of money. And let's be honest. Money is what this is all about. I worked hard all my life. I made a lot of money. I mean a lot of money. And now I get my reward. I have a beautiful young bride and —"</p>

<p>"Damballah Weddo."</p>

<p>"I — what was that?"</p>
<p>"What was what, George?"</p>
<p>"A voice. Kinda spooky voice."</p>

<p>"Damballah Weddo."</p>

<p>"There it is again. Didn't anyone hear it?"</p>
<p>"No one heard anything."</p>
<p>"You didn't hear a voice saying something foreign?"</p>
<p>"No, George. The only voice we can hear is yours."</p>
<p>"Right. Well, as I was saying, I have a beautiful young bride —"</p>

<p>"Damballah Weddo."</p>

<p>"George, you're right. I did hear something. Of course I'm right. It's my wedding day. What was I saying? I have always believed that what you —"</p>

<p>"Damballah Weddo."</p>

<p>"George, sit down, man. You look terrible."</p>
<p>"Leave me alone, damn you. This is my wedding party, which I have paid for. Most people would expect the bride's father to pay for all this. But he didn't."</p>

<p>"Damballah Weddo."</p>

<p>"Get out the way. Let him get some air."</p>
<p>"Oh my God!"</p>

<p>George was dead. The doctor said he had a heart attack. Laverne was a widow before her wedding day was over. But I guess inheriting $100,000 of Andrews' money helped her to recover quickly.</p>

<p>And Marie? Well, she was most definitely the Voodoo Queen of New Orleans.</p>
