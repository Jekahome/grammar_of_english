<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter One</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">My Story Begins</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">In 1825, I was ten years old. My father and mother were dead. I lived with my aunt and uncle, Mr. and Mrs. Reed. Their house was called Gateshead Hall. The house was in Yorkshire, in the north of England.</p>

<p>My aunt and uncle Reed had two children: a boy, John, and a girl, Eliza. I liked my uncle Reed, and he liked me. But in 1825, my uncle died. After that, I was very unhappy. My aunt Reed did not like me, and John and Eliza were unkind to me.</p>

<p>It was a cold, rainy day in December. All of us were in the house. I wanted to be alone. I wanted to read. I opened a book. Then I heard my cousin John's voice.</p>

<p>"Jane, Jane Eyre, where are you?" John shouted. He came into the room, and he saw me.</p>

<p>"Why are you reading my book?" he asked. "Give it to me."</p>

<p>John took the book. He hit my head with it. I screamed. John hit me again. I pulled his hair, and I kicked him.</p>

<p>"Help! Help! Mamma!" John shouted. "Jane Eyre is hurting me!"</p>

<p>Aunt Reed ran into the room. She pulled me away from John.</p>

<p>"John hit me with a book," I said. "I hate him, and I hate you too."</p>

<p>"You are a bad girl, Jane," my aunt said. "Why do you hit me? You don't like me," I replied. "John and Eliza are unkind to me. I want to leave Gateshead Hall."</p>

<p>"You want to leave?" Aunt Reed said. "Where will you go? Your parents are dead. You cannot live alone."</p>

<p>Aunt Reed thought for a moment. "My friend, Mr. Brocklehurst, is the owner of a school," she said. "I will send you to Mr. Brocklehurst's school."</p>

<p>A few days later, Mr. Brocklehurst came to Gateshead Hall. He was a very tall man. His eyes were dark, and his face was cruel.</p>

<p>"Jane Eyre," he said to me. "God does not like bad children. God punishes bad children, Jane Eyre."</p>

<p>"God will punish John Reed," I replied. "John Reed hits me, and he shouts at me."</p>

<p>"That is not true. You are a liar, Jane Eyre," Mr. Brocklehurst said. "You must not tell lies, and you must not live here with your cousins. You will come to Lowood School. You will become a good girl."</p>

<p>"I want to come to your school, sir," I said. "I want to leave this house."</p>

<p>"Bad girls are punished at my school, Jane Eyre," Mr. Brocklehurst said. "The girls work very hard at Lowood."</p>

<p>"I will work hard. I will be a good pupil, Mr. Brocklehurst," I said.</p>

<p>Two weeks later, I left Gateshead Hall. I went to Lowood School.</p>

<summary>Chapter Two</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Two</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Lowood School</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">It was the month of January. I arrived at Lowood School at night. A servant took me up some stairs and into a big bedroom. There were many beds in the room. The girls in the beds were asleep. The servant took me to an empty bed. I put on my night clothes, and I got into bed. Soon, I was asleep too.</p>

<p>I woke up very early. A loud bell was ringing. The bedroom was dark and cold. I watched the other girls. They washed in cold water, and they dressed quickly. There was a plain brown dress next to my bed, and there was a pair of ugly, heavy shoes. I washed quickly. Then I put on my new clothes. I was very hungry.</p>

<p>I followed the other girls down the stairs. We sat down at long tables in a large dining room. Our food was terrible.</p>

<p>"The food is bad again," one of the girls said.</p>

<p>"Stand up!" a teacher shouted. "Don't talk!"</p>

<p>We stood up. We did not speak. We walked into a big school room, and we sat down. There were about eighty girls in the school room, and there were four classes. The oldest girls were in the fourth class. I was in the first class.</p>

<p>Four teachers came into the room, and we began our lessons. The lessons were not interesting. First, we read some pages in a book. Then, our teacher asked us questions about those pages. After four hours, we went outside. It was very cold. Very soon, a bell rang. Lessons started again.</p>

<p>Three weeks passed. One afternoon, the head teacher came into the school room. The head teacher's name was Miss Temple. Mr. Brocklehurst was with her. We all stood up. I stood behind an older girl. I did not want Mr. Brocklehurst to see me.</p>

<p>Mr. Brocklehurst walked slowly around the room. Everybody was very quiet. And then, I dropped my book. Mr. Brocklehurst stopped walking. He looked at me.</p>

<p>"Ah, the new girl," he said. "Come here, Jane Eyre." Then he pointed at two of the older girls. "You two girls, put Jane Eyre on that high chair," he said. "Look at Jane Eyre, everybody. This child is bad. She is a liar. She will be punished. Miss Temple, teachers, girls, do not talk to this child."</p>

<p>Then he spoke to me again. "Jane Eyre, you must stand on that chair for two hours," he said. "You are a bad girl."</p>

<p>That evening, I cried and cried. But Miss Temple was kind to me.</p>

<p>"You are a good pupil, Jane," she said. "And you are not a bad girl. I am your friend, Jane."</p>

<p>"Thank you, Miss Temple," I said.</p>

<p>Lowood School was in an unhealthy place. The buildings were wet and cold. Mr. Brocklehurst owned the school. He was a rich man. But he did not buy warm clothes for us. And he did not buy good food for us. Everybody hated him.</p>

<p>In the spring, many of the girls became sick. Some of them left the school. They never came back. Many of the girls died. That spring was a terrible time. We had no lessons. Miss Temple and the other teachers took care of the sick pupils. Mr. Brocklehurst had to buy better food for us. And he had to buy warm clothes for us. Mr. Brocklehurst never came to the school.</p>

<p>The next year, Lowood School moved to a better place. It was a healthier place. There were new school rooms, new bedrooms, and a new dining room. The new buildings were bright and clean. The teachers were happy. After that, I was happy at Lowood School too.</p>

<p>I was a pupil at Lowood School for six years. Then, I became a teacher. I was a teacher at the school for two years. But I never returned to Gateshead Hall. And the Reeds never wrote to me.</p>

<summary>Chapter Three</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Three</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Thornfield Hall</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">In 1833, I was eighteen years old. In the summer, Miss Temple left Lowood School. She got married. I wanted to leave Lowood too. I wanted a new life.</p>

<p>"I will be a governess," I thought. I put an advertisement in a newspaper.</p>

<p>"A young woman wants to teach one or two children in their home. She teaches English, arithmetic, geography, religion, French, drawing and music. J.E."</p>

<p>I had a reply to my advertisement. The reply was from Mrs. Fairfax of Thornfield Hall near Milcote. Milcote was about seventy miles from Lowood School. Mrs. Fairfax wanted a governess for a little girl. I wrote to Mrs. Fairfax immediately. I was going to be a governess at Thornfield Hall.</p>

<p>I travelled to Milcote in a coach. At Milcote, a servant met me. He took me to Thornfield Hall. At Thornfield Hall, another servant opened the door. She was smiling. She took me into a small, warm room. A lady was in the room. She was sitting by the fire.</p>

<p>"Are you Mrs. Fairfax?" I asked her.</p>

<p>"Yes, my dear," she said. "And you are Miss Eyre. Are you cold? Sit by the fire, Miss Eyre. A servant will bring you some food."</p>

<p>"Mrs. Fairfax is very kind," I said to myself. "I will be happy here."</p>

<p>"Will I see Miss Fairfax tonight?" I asked.</p>

<p>Mrs. Fairfax looked at me. She smiled. "Miss Fairfax? No, no," she said. "Your pupil's name is not Miss Fairfax. Your pupil is Adèle Varens. Adèle's mother was a French woman. Adèle is Mr. Rochester's ward. He takes care of her."</p>

<p>"Mr. Rochester? Who is Mr. Rochester?" I asked.</p>

<p>"Mr. Edward Rochester is the owner of Thornfield Hall," Mrs. Fairfax said. "I am his housekeeper. I take care of Thornfield Hall. Mr. Rochester is not here now. He does not like this house. He is often away from home."</p>

<p>I was very tired. Mrs. Fairfax took me up the wide stairs. She took me to my room. I went to bed immediately and I slept well.</p>

<p>The next morning I woke early. The sun was shining. I put on a plain black dress. I opened my bedroom door. I walked along a corridor and down the wide stairs. I walked out into the sunny garden. I turned and I looked up at my new home. Thornfield Hall was a beautiful house with many large windows. The garden was beautiful too.</p>

<p>After a few minutes Mrs. Fairfax came into the garden. She spoke to me. "Good morning, Miss Eyre," she said. "You have woken early. Adèle is here. After breakfast you must take her to the school room. She must begin her lessons."</p>

<p>A pretty little girl walked towards me. She was about eight years old. She spoke to me in French and I replied in French. After breakfast I took Adèle to the school room. We worked all morning. Adèle enjoyed her lessons and I was happy.</p>

<p>In the afternoon Mrs. Fairfax took me into all the rooms of Thornfield Hall. We looked at the paintings and at the beautiful furniture. We walked along the corridors.</p>

<p>"Come up onto the roof," Mrs. Fairfax said. "You will see the beautiful countryside around Thornfield Hall."</p>

<p>We walked up many stairs. At last we were at the top of the house. We walked along the top corridor. Mrs. Fairfax opened a small door and we walked onto the roof.</p>

<p>"Look, Miss Eyre," Mrs. Fairfax said. "You can see for many miles."</p>

<p>We stood on the roof for a few minutes. Then we went back into the house. We walked carefully towards the stairs. The top corridor was narrow and dark. Suddenly I heard a strange laugh.</p>

<p>"Who is that, Mrs. Fairfax?" I asked.</p>

<p>Mrs. Fairfax did not reply. She knocked on a door. "Grace," she said. The door opened. Behind the door was a small room. A servant was standing at the door.</p>

<p>"Be quiet, Grace, please," Mrs. Fairfax said. The woman looked at Mrs. Fairfax. Then she closed the door.</p>

<p>"That was Grace Poole," Mrs. Fairfax said. "She works up here. Sometimes she laughs and talks with the other servants. Don't worry about Grace. Please come downstairs now, Miss Eyre."</p>

<summary>Chapter Four</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Four</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Mr. Rochester</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">Three months passed. I had not met the owner of Thornfield Hall. Mr. Rochester had not come home.</p>

<p>One January afternoon I went out and I walked towards the road. I was going to the village of Hay. I was going to post a letter in the village. Hay was two miles from Thornfield Hall. The day was fine but it was very cold. I walked quickly and soon I was near the village.</p>

<p>Suddenly a big black and white dog ran past me. A moment later a man on a black horse followed the dog. Then I heard an angry shout. The dog ran past me again. It was barking loudly. I turned round. The horse had fallen on the icy ground and the man had fallen from the horse. I walked towards them.</p>

<p>"Can I help you, sir?" I asked.</p>

<p>"My horse fell. I've hurt my foot," the man said. The horse stood up. The man tried to stand up too but he could not stand. He fell onto the ground again.</p>

<p>The man was about thirty-five years old. He was not handsome but he had a strong face. He had dark eyes and black hair. He was not very tall but his body was powerful.</p>

<p>"I'll bring somebody from Thornfield Hall," I said.</p>

<p>"Do you live at Thornfield?" the man asked.</p>

<p>"I am the governess," I replied.</p>

<p>"Ah yes. The governess," the man said. "Help me, please."</p>

<p>The man stood up very slowly and he put his hand on my shoulder. He walked slowly towards his horse. I helped him. He pulled himself onto the horse.</p>

<p>"Thank you. Now go home quickly," the man said. And he rode away.</p>

<p>I walked on to the village and I posted my letter. Then I returned to Thornfield Hall. Bright lights were shining in the big house. I went inside. A big black and white dog walked towards me. It came from the dining room. I had seen the dog before.</p>

<p>"Whose dog is that?" I asked a servant.</p>

<p>"It's Mr. Rochester's dog," the servant replied. "Mr. Rochester has come home — but he has hurt his foot. His horse fell on some ice."</p>

<p>I smiled. The owner of Thornfield Hall had returned. But I did not see Mr. Rochester again that day.</p>

<p>I saw Mr. Rochester the next day. He sent for me in the evening. I put on a clean dress. I brushed my hair carefully.</p>

<p>Mr. Rochester was in the large sitting room. He was sitting in a big chair. His right foot was on a small chair. Mrs. Fairfax and Adèle were sitting with him.</p>

<p>"This is Miss Eyre, sir," Mrs. Fairfax said. Mr. Rochester looked at me. He did not smile.</p>

<p>"Sit by the fire, Miss Eyre," he said. "Where have you come from?"</p>

<p>"From Lowood School," I replied. "I was there for eight years."</p>

<p>"Eight years?" Mr. Rochester said. "That is a long time. Who are your parents?"</p>

<p>"I have no parents, sir," I answered. "They are dead."</p>

<p>"But where is your home, Miss Eyre?" Mr. Rochester asked.</p>

<p>"I have no home, sir. I have no family," I said.</p>

<p>"Why did you come to Thornfield Hall?" Mr. Rochester asked.</p>

<p>"I wanted to leave Lowood, sir," I replied. "I put an advertisement in a newspaper. Mrs. Fairfax replied to my advertisement."</p>

<p>"Yes, I did," Mrs. Fairfax said. "Miss Eyre is a good teacher, Mr. Rochester."</p>

<p>Mr. Rochester smiled for the first time. "You are very young, Miss Eyre," he said.</p>

<p>"I am eighteen, sir," I replied. Mr. Rochester smiled again. He did not ask me more questions.</p>

<p>After that evening, I did not see Mr. Rochester for a few days. Then, one night, he sent for me again.</p>

<p>"Sit near me, Miss Eyre," he said. "Mrs. Fairfax will talk to Adèle."</p>

<p>I sat down quietly, but I did not speak. The fire was very bright. I saw Mr. Rochester's face clearly. I saw his large, dark eyes. He was smiling. He was happy. After a minute, Mr. Rochester spoke.</p>

<p>"Miss Eyre," he said. "You are looking up to me very carefully. Am I a handsome man?"</p>

<p>"No, sir," I said.</p>

<p>"You speak the truth, Miss Eyre," Mr. Rochester said. "Look at me again. Am I a kind man?"</p>

<p>"No, sir," I said again. "You are smiling now, but you are not always kind."</p>

<p>"That is true," Mr. Rochester replied. "I have had a difficult life. I have met bad people. I have been a bad person myself. Now, Thornfield Hall is my home. But I hate this house. You are very young, Miss Eyre. You cannot understand me."</p>

<p>"You are right. I don't understand you, sir," I said. I stood up.</p>

<p>"Where are you going?" Mr. Rochester asked.</p>

<p>"It is late. Adèle must go to bed," I said.</p>

<p>"Are you frightened of me, Miss Eyre?" Mr. Rochester asked.</p>

<p>"No, sir," I replied. "But you say strange things, sir."</p>

<p>Mr. Rochester smiled. "Take Adèle to her bedroom now, Miss Eyre," he said. "We will talk again tomorrow."</p>

<p>After that night, we talked together many times. Mr. Rochester was an interesting man. But he was a strange man, too. I often thought about him. "Why does Mr. Rochester hate Thornfield?" I asked myself. "Thornfield Hall is a beautiful place. But Mr. Rochester is not happy."</p>

<summary>Chapter Five</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Five</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Fire</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">It was March. One night, I was in bed. But I was not asleep. The house was quiet. Suddenly, I heard a sound in the corridor outside my room.</p>

<p>"Who's there?" I said. Nobody answered. Then, I heard a strange laugh. I got out of my bed, and I went quietly to the door. I listened. I heard another sound. Somebody was walking up the stairs to the top corridor. Then, I heard somebody close a door.</p>

<p>"Was that Grace Poole?" I said to myself. "Yes, it was Grace. Why was she laughing? And why is she walking in the house at night? Is she mad? I must tell Mrs. Fairfax about this. I will speak to her now."</p>

<p>I put on some clothes, and I opened the door. There was a candle on the floor outside my room. The candle was burning. There was thick smoke in the corridor. I went into the corridor. I looked around me. The door of Mr. Rochester's bedroom was open. And the smoke was coming from Mr. Rochester's room. I ran into the room.</p>

<p>"Wake up, sir."</p>

<p>"What happened, Jane?"</p>

<p>"There was a fire, sir. Grace Poole tried to kill you."</p>

<p>"Stay here, Jane. Open the window. I'll go upstairs."</p>

<p>I sat in a chair by the window. Time passed. At last, Mr. Rochester returned.</p>

<p>"Please don't worry, Jane," he said. "Grace Poole is a strange woman, but she won't hurt anybody tonight."</p>

<p>I stood up. "Good night, sir," I said. Mr. Rochester held my hand. He looked at me, and he smiled.</p>

<p>"Thank you, my dear friend," he said. "You saved my life tonight, Jane."</p>

<p>"Good night, sir," I said again.</p>

<p>I went back to my bed. I was very tired. But at first, I could not sleep. Suddenly, I understood something. I loved Mr. Rochester. He had smiled at me. He had held my hand. Did he love me? I did not know. But I thought about Mr. Rochester for a long time.</p>

<p>I did not see Mr. Rochester the next day. He did not send for me. In the evening, I went down to Mrs. Fairfax's sitting room. The housekeeper was looking out of the window.</p>

<p>"The weather has been good today," Mrs. Fairfax said. "Mr. Rochester had a good day for his journey."</p>

<p>"His journey? Where has he gone?" I asked. I was surprised.</p>

<p>"He has gone to Ingram Park," Mrs. Fairfax replied. "Mr. Rochester will stay there for a week or more. He has many friends. All his friends will be at Ingram Park this week."</p>

<p>"Will there be any ladies at Ingram Park?" I asked.</p>

<p>"Yes," Mrs. Fairfax said. "There will be many ladies there. Miss Blanche Ingram will be there. Mr. Rochester has known her for many years."</p>

<p>"Is Miss Ingram beautiful?" I asked.</p>

<p>"She is very beautiful," Mrs. Fairfax said.</p>

<p>"Will Mr. Rochester marry her?" I asked. Mrs. Fairfax smiled. "I don't know, Miss Eyre," she replied. "I don't know."</p>

<p>I was very unhappy. I went up to my bedroom. I looked in my mirror.</p>

<p>"Jane Eyre," I said to myself. "You are not pretty. And you are poor. Mr. Rochester will never marry you. He will marry Miss Blanche Ingram. She is a rich lady. You are a poor governess. Forget Mr. Rochester, Jane Eyre. Forget him."</p>

<summary>Chapter Six</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Six</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Guests at Thornfield Hall</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">Two weeks later, a letter arrived for Mrs. Fairfax.</p>

<p>"Mr. Rochester will return on Thursday," Mrs. Fairfax said. "Some of his friends will come here with him. There will be many guests at Thornfield Hall."</p>

<p>On Thursday evening, Mrs. Fairfax, Adèle and I were in Adèle's bedroom. Mrs. Fairfax was looking out of the window.</p>

<p>"The guests are arriving now," Mrs. Fairfax said. I went to the window and I looked out. There were three carriages. Two people were riding horses. Mr. Rochester was riding his big black horse. A beautiful young woman was riding a white horse. Mrs. Fairfax pointed to the young woman.</p>

<p>"That is Miss Ingram," the housekeeper said. Then she went downstairs. Adèle wanted to go downstairs too.</p>

<p>"No, Adèle," I said. "We cannot go downstairs tonight. Mr. Rochester is talking to his guests."</p>

<p>The next day, Mrs. Fairfax came into the school room. "Mr. Rochester wants you to meet his guests tonight, Miss Eyre," she said. "Adèle must meet them too."</p>

<p>Later, Adèle and I went quietly into the sitting room. And soon, eight ladies came into the room. One of them was tall, dark and very beautiful. She was Blanche Ingram. Adèle ran towards her.</p>

<p>"Good evening, beautiful lady," she said in French.</p>

<p>"What a pretty little girl," Blanche Ingram said. Miss Ingram spoke to the other ladies and she spoke to Adèle, but she did not speak to me.</p>

<p>Half an hour later, the gentlemen came into the room. I looked at Mr. Rochester. He saw me, but he did not speak to me.</p>

<p>Miss Ingram pointed at Adèle. "Why does not this little girl live at school, Mr. Rochester?" she asked.</p>

<p>"Adèle learns her lessons at home," Mr. Rochester replied. "She has a governess."</p>

<p>"Oh, yes, that small woman by the window," Miss Ingram said. "I had many governesses. I hated all of them. They were all ugly and stupid."</p>

<p>Later, Miss Ingram and Mr. Rochester sang some songs together. Mr. Rochester had a fine voice. I listened to the songs. Then I left the room. Mr. Rochester followed me.</p>

<p>"What is wrong, Jane?" he asked.</p>

<p>"Nothing is wrong, sir," I said. "But I am tired. I am going to my room. Good night, sir."</p>

<p>"You are tired," Mr. Rochester replied. "And you are unhappy too. There are tears in your eyes. Rest now, Jane. But please come and meet my guests tomorrow evening. Don't forget, my... Don't forget, Jane."</p>

<p>The guests stayed at Thornfield Hall for two weeks. Every evening, I went to the sitting room with Adèle. Nobody spoke to me. Mr. Rochester and Miss Ingram were always together.</p>

<p>One afternoon, Mr. Rochester went to Milcote. He returned late in the evening. I met him at the front door.</p>

<p>"Another guest has arrived, sir," I told him. "His name is Mr. Mason. He has come from the West Indies."</p>

<p>Suddenly, Mr. Rochester's face was pale. He held my hand tightly. "Mason? The West Indies? Mason," he said.</p>

<p>"Are you ill, sir?" I asked.</p>

<p>"Jane, my little friend. I've had a shock," he said. "Bring me a glass of wine, please."</p>

<p>I went quickly to the dining room. I returned with a glass of wine, and I gave it to Mr. Rochester.</p>

<p>"What are my guests doing?" he asked.</p>

<p>"They are eating and laughing, sir," I replied. "Mr. Mason is talking to the other guests."</p>

<p>"One day, they will all hate me," Mr. Rochester said. "Now go into the dining room again. Tell Mason to meet me in the library."</p>

<p>I gave Mr. Mason the message. Then I went to my bedroom. I got into my bed. Later, I heard Mr. Rochester coming up the stairs with Mr. Mason. They were laughing and talking. Soon, I was asleep.</p>

<summary>Chapter Seven</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Seven</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">A Terrible Night</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">Some hours later, I woke up. A terrible cry had woken me. The moon was bright. Its light was shining through my window. I listened. Then, I heard somebody shouting.</p>

<p>"Help! Help! Rochester! Help me!"</p>

<p>The voice came from the top corridor. "Help!"</p>

<p>I got out of bed, and I put on a dress and some shoes. I opened my door. All the guests were in the corridor outside the bedrooms. They were all asking questions.</p>

<p>"What happened?" they asked. "Is there a fire? Who is hurt? Where is Mr. Rochester?"</p>

<p>"I am here," Mr. Rochester said. "Mr. Rochester, I am here."</p>

<p>Mr. Rochester was walking down the stairs from the top corridor.</p>

<p>"What is wrong, Mr. Rochester?" Miss Ingram asked. "What has happened?"</p>

<p>"Nothing is wrong," Mr. Rochester replied. "One of the servants has had a bad dream. Go back to bed."</p>

<p>I went back to my room. But something was wrong. I did not get into my bed. I waited. Soon, somebody knocked on my door. I opened the door. Mr. Rochester was standing in the corridor.</p>

<p>"Jane, follow me," Mr. Rochester said. "Do not make a sound."</p>

<p>We went up to the top corridor. Mr. Rochester unlocked a door, and we went inside a room. Mr. Mason was sitting on a chair in the room. His face was pale, and his shirt was covered with blood. Then I heard a terrible laugh. The sound came from the next room.</p>

<p>"Grace Poole is a mad woman," I thought. "Why does Mr. Rochester have a mad servant?"</p>

<p>Mr. Rochester spoke quietly to Mr. Mason. "I am going to bring a doctor, Richard," he said. Then he spoke to me. "Stay here, Jane. Wash Mr. Mason's arm. But do not speak to him."</p>

<p>Mr. Rochester left the room. I washed Mr. Mason's arm. We waited for Mr. Rochester and the doctor. Mr. Mason did not speak to me. And I did not speak to him. After two hours, Mr. Rochester returned. The doctor was with him. The doctor looked at Mr. Mason's arm.</p>

<p>"She bit me," Mr. Mason said. "I came up here. I wanted to see her. I wanted to help her. But she bit me."</p>

<p>"Be quiet now, Richard," Mr. Rochester said quickly. The doctor put a bandage on Mr. Mason's arm. Mr. Rochester put Mr. Mason's coat round the injured man's shoulders. Then he spoke to me again.</p>

<p>"Run downstairs, Jane. Unlock the small door at the side of the house," he said. "We will follow you."</p>

<p>I went quickly downstairs and I opened the door. Outside the door, a servant was waiting with the carriage. Mr. Mason and the doctor came out of the house. They got into the carriage. Then Mr. Rochester came out of the house too. Mr. Mason spoke to him through the window of the carriage.</p>

<p>"Help her. Be kind to her, Rochester," he said.</p>

<p>"Yes, I will, Mason," Mr. Rochester said. The servant drove the carriage away.</p>

<p>"Will you walk in the garden with me, Jane?" Mr. Rochester asked. "I do not want to sleep now."</p>

<p>"Yes, I will, sir," I said. Soon it was morning. The birds were beginning to sing. The flowers had a sweet smell.</p>

<p>"It has been a strange night, Jane," Mr. Rochester said. "Were you frightened?"</p>

<p>"I am frightened of Grace Poole," I said. "She will hurt you one day."</p>

<p>"I am stronger than she is. She will not hurt me," Mr. Rochester said. He looked at me for a few moments. "Are you my friend, Jane?" he asked me.</p>

<p>"Yes, sir," I replied. "I will be your friend forever."</p>

<p>"Thank you, my dear," Mr. Rochester said. "I have made mistakes. Now I want to be happy. That is not wrong, is it, Jane?" He stopped speaking for a minute. Then he said, "Go into the house. I will talk to you tomorrow."</p>

<p>But the next day, I had a letter from Gateshead Hall. The letter was from my cousin Eliza.</p>

<p>"Dear Jane Eyre, My brother, John, is dead. My mother is very ill. She wants to speak to you. Please come quickly, Jane. Your cousin, Eliza Reed."</p>

<p>I started the journey to my aunt Reed's house immediately. I arrived there the next day. My aunt Reed was very, very ill. She could not move. And she did not speak to me. I wanted to return to Thornfield Hall. I wanted to see Mr. Rochester. But Eliza wanted me to stay at Gateshead Hall.</p>

<p>After three weeks, my aunt spoke to me at last. She spoke very slowly.</p>

<p>"Are you, Jane Eyre?" she asked.</p>

<p>"Yes, Aunt Reed," I replied. "I am Jane Eyre."</p>

<p>"There is a letter for you," Aunt Reed said. "It is in my desk. Go, Eliza, please. She will get the letter."</p>

<p>Eliza came into the room. She opened the desk and she gave me a letter.</p>

<p>"Read the letter, Jane," my aunt said.</p>

<p>The letter had come from Madeira, but it was three years old.</p>

<p>"Dear Madam, I am Jane Eyre's uncle. I am a very rich man. I have no children. One day, Jane will have all my money. Tell her about me. She must write to me. John Eyre."</p>

<p>"I answered that letter," Aunt Reed said. "I hated you, Jane. I did not want you to have your uncle's money. I wrote to John Eyre. I wrote, 'Jane Eyre is dead. She died at Lowood School.' I am sorry, Jane. I was wrong."</p>

<p>Mrs. Reed died that night. I left Gateshead Hall a few days later. I took my uncle's letter with me.</p>

<p>Mr. Rochester met me at Thornfield Hall. "Welcome back to my house," he said. "This is your home, Jane."</p>

<p>"Thank you, sir," I said. "I am very happy here."</p>

<p>Mr. Rochester's guests had left. No other visitors came to Thornfield Hall. Every day, Mr. Rochester and I talked together. And every day, I loved him more.</p>

<summary>Chapter Eight</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Eight</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">In the Garden</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">In June, the weather was hot. One evening, I walked into the garden. Mr. Rochester was there too.</p>

<p>"Do you like this house, Jane?" he asked.</p>

<p>"Yes, sir," I replied.</p>

<p>"Soon, Adèle will go to live at a school, Jane," he said. "Then I will not want a governess here. Will you be sad then, Jane? Will you leave Thornfield Hall?"</p>

<p>"Leave?" I said quickly. "Must I leave Thornfield?"</p>

<p>"My dear..." Mr. Rochester stopped. He was silent for a moment. Then he said, "I am going to be married soon."</p>

<p>"Oh, sir," I said. "Then I must go far away. Far away from Thornfield, far away from you, sir." I started to cry.</p>

<p>"I will always remember you, Jane," Mr. Rochester said. "Will you forget me?"</p>

<p>"No, sir," I replied. "I will never forget you. I don't want to leave Thornfield, sir. I don't want to leave you."</p>

<p>"Don't leave, Jane," Mr. Rochester said. "Stay here." He smiled at me.</p>

<p>"I must not stay here, sir," I said. "You are going to marry Miss Ingram. I am poor. I do not have a pretty face. But I have a heart. It is a loving heart, sir."</p>

<p>"Jane, I am not going to marry Miss Ingram," Mr. Rochester said. "She is rich. She is beautiful. You are poor. You are not beautiful. But I want to marry you. Will you marry me, Jane?"</p>

<p>For a moment, I could not speak. At last, I asked, "Do you love me, sir?"</p>

<p>"I do," he replied.</p>

<p>"Then, sir, I will marry you," I said. And Mr. Rochester kissed me.</p>

<p>"My dearest Jane," he said. "Nothing can stop our marriage now. We will be married in a month, Jane."</p>

<p>We kissed again. Then, I said goodnight, and I went into the house. I went upstairs to my room. Later, I remembered my Uncle John Eyre's letter. "I will write to him in Madeira," I said to myself. "I will tell him about my marriage to Mr. Rochester. I am very happy. My uncle will be happy too."</p>

<p>Four weeks passed. Mr. Rochester was going to buy me many beautiful things. He was going to give me many presents. But I did not want these things.</p>

<p>"No, Edward," I said. "I am not beautiful. I don't want beautiful things. I want you, Edward."</p>

<p>It was the month of July, two days before our wedding day. Mr. Rochester went away. "I will return tomorrow," he said. "I love you, Jane."</p>

<p>That night, I went to my bedroom early. My wedding dress and my wedding veil were in my room. I looked at them. "In two days, I will be Jane Rochester," I said to myself. Then I went to bed. But I did not sleep well.</p>

<p>The next day, Mr. Rochester returned. He looked at me carefully. "What is wrong, Jane?" he asked. "Your face is pale. Are you frightened?"</p>

<p>"I had a very strange dream last night," I said. "It was a dream about this house. But in my dream, Thornfield Hall had no roof. The walls were burnt. They were black. In my dream, I tried to find you, but you were not in the house."</p>

<p>"Are you afraid of a dream, Jane?" Mr. Rochester asked.</p>

<p>"No, Edward," I replied. "But I woke up from my dream. There was a woman in my room. She was tall and heavy. She had long black hair. The woman was holding a candle," I said. "She put the candle by my mirror. She put my wedding veil over her head, and she looked in the mirror. Then I saw her face. It was a strange, terrible face, Edward," I said. "Suddenly, the woman tore my veil into two pieces. She threw the pieces on the floor."</p>

<p>"What happened next?" Mr. Rochester asked.</p>

<p>"The woman held her candle near my face," I replied. "She looked at me, and she laughed. Then she went away."</p>

<p>"This happened in your dream, Jane," Mr. Rochester said.</p>

<p>"It did not happen in my dream, Edward," I said. "This morning, my wedding veil was on the floor of my room. It was torn. It was in two pieces."</p>

<p>"But the woman did not hurt you, Jane," Mr. Rochester said. "Sleep in Adèle's room tonight, my dear. You will have no more bad dreams."</p>

<summary>Chapter Nine</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Nine</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Mr. Rochester's Wife</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">It was our wedding day. We were going to be married in a church near Thornfield Hall. After the marriage, we were going to travel to London. I got up early. I put on my wedding dress, and I went downstairs. Mr. Rochester was waiting for me. At eight o'clock, we walked together to the church.</p>

<p>The clergyman was standing by the door of the church. There were two other people inside the church, two men. They were sitting in a dark corner. I could not see them very well.</p>

<p>The clergyman started to speak. At every marriage, the clergyman asks an important question. He asks the people in the church, "Is there a problem about this marriage?" The clergyman spoke loudly. He asked this question, and he waited. There was silence for a moment. And then, one of the men in the dark corner stood up. He spoke loudly.</p>

<p>"There is a problem. These two people must not be married," he said.</p>

<p>"There is not a problem," Mr. Rochester said to the clergyman. "Please go on with the marriage."</p>

<p>"No, I cannot go on with the marriage," the clergyman replied. He spoke to the man in the corner. "What is the problem, sir?" he asked.</p>

<p>"Mr. Edward Rochester has a wife."</p>

<p>Mr. Rochester turned and looked at the man. "Who are you?" he asked angrily. "What do you know about me?"</p>

<p>"My name is Briggs, sir. I am a lawyer," the man replied. "I know many things about you. Fifteen years ago, you were married in the West Indies. Your wife's name is Bertha Mason. She is alive. She lives at Thornfield Hall."</p>

<p>"How do you know that?" Mr. Rochester shouted.</p>

<p>The other man in the dark corner stood up. He walked towards us. It was Richard Mason.</p>

<p>"Bertha Mason is my sister," he said. "I saw her at Thornfield Hall in April."</p>

<p>Mr. Rochester's face was pale. For a minute, he was silent. Then he spoke quietly. "It is true," he said. "My wife is living at Thornfield Hall. She is mad. Come to the house, all of you. Come and see Mrs. Rochester. Come and see the mad woman."</p>

<p>We all left the church. Nobody spoke. At Thornfield, Mrs. Fairfax and Adèle were waiting for us. They were smiling happily.</p>

<p>"Nobody will be happy today," Mr. Rochester said. "We are not married."</p>

<p>Briggs, Mr. Mason, the clergyman and I followed Mr. Rochester. We followed him up the stairs. He took us to the top corridor. He unlocked the door and we went into a small room. I had seen this room before. We walked through the room to another door. Mr. Rochester unlocked this door and we saw a larger room. Grace Poole was sitting in the room. But another woman was there too. She was tall and heavy. Her dark hair was in front of her face. The woman turned and looked at us. I knew that terrible, mad face. I had seen it in my bedroom two nights before.</p>

<p>The mad woman saw Mr. Rochester. She screamed and she ran towards him.</p>

<p>"Be careful, sir," Grace Poole said. The mad woman was very strong. She screamed and she hit Mr. Rochester. But Mr. Rochester held her arms.</p>

<p>"This woman is my wife," Mr. Rochester said angrily. "I wanted to forget about her. I wanted to marry this young girl, Jane Eyre. Was I wrong?" He was silent for a few moments. Then he spoke quietly. "Yes, I was wrong," he said. "I love Jane Eyre, but I was wrong. Now go. All of you. I must take care of my mad wife."</p>

<p>I went slowly downstairs. Mr. Briggs, the lawyer, spoke to me. "I am sorry for you, Miss Eyre," he said. "You did nothing wrong. Your uncle, John Eyre, is sorry for you too. He read your letter and then he met Richard Mason in Madeira. Your uncle is dying, Miss Eyre. He could not come to England. He sent me here. He wanted me to stop this marriage."</p>

<p>I did not answer. I went to my room and I locked the door. I took off my wedding dress. I put on a plain black dress. I lay down on my bed.</p>

<p>"I am Jane Eyre today," I thought. "I will be Jane Eyre tomorrow. I will never be Jane Rochester. I must leave Thornfield Hall. I must never see Mr. Rochester again. My life here is finished."</p>

<p>Many hours later, I got off the bed. I unlocked my door. Mr. Rochester was waiting outside my room.</p>

<p>"You are unhappy, Jane," he said. "I am very, very sorry. Jane, we will leave Thornfield. We will go to another country. We will be happy again."</p>

<p>"I cannot be your wife. I cannot live with you," I said. "I must leave you, Edward."</p>

<p>"Listen, Jane," Mr. Rochester said. "My father wanted me to marry Bertha Mason. Her family was very rich. I married her. My father was happy. But I was not happy. Bertha was mad and she was a bad woman. Nobody told me about her. She was married to me, but she met other men. She was drunk every day. She tried to kill me many times. After four years, I brought Bertha here to Thornfield Hall," Mr. Rochester said. "Then I went away. Grace Poole took care of Bertha. I met other women. One of them was a French singer. She was Adèle's mother. Adèle is my daughter, Jane. But I did not love the French singer. I did not love anybody. I came home to Thornfield Hall. Then you came here — and I loved you. I will always love you. Please stay with me, Jane."</p>

<p>"No, Edward," I said. "I am going away. We will be unhappy, but we must not be together. Goodbye, Edward."</p>

<p>"Oh, Jane, Jane, my love," Mr. Rochester said. "Don't leave me."</p>

<p>I kissed Mr. Rochester. "God will help you, Edward," I said. Quickly, I went into my room. I put some clothes into a bag. Later, I heard Mr. Rochester go into his room. Very quietly, I went downstairs. I opened the small door at the side of the house. I left Thornfield Hall and I walked to the road. It was dark. Soon, a coach came along the road. I gave all my money to the driver of the coach. I got into the coach.</p>

<p>Many hours later, the coach stopped. It was ten o'clock in the morning.</p>

<p>"You must give me more money now," the driver said.</p>

<p>"I have no more money," I said.</p>

<p>"You have no more money? Then you must get out of the coach," the driver said. I got down onto the road. The coach moved away quickly. But I had left my bag in the coach.</p>

<p>I looked around me. I was on a cold, empty moor. I was tired and hungry. I walked and walked. I had no money. I had no food. I walked until the evening came. At last, I lay down on the ground. I fell asleep immediately.</p>

<summary>Chapter Ten</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">Chapter Ten</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Moor House</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para">The next morning, I woke late. I walked along the road for many miles. It started to rain. Soon, my clothes were wet. I saw no one. I walked on the moor all day. In the evening, I was very tired again.</p>

<p>"I must sleep soon," I thought. "Where shall I sleep?" Then, I saw a light. I walked slowly towards it. The rain was falling heavily. I saw a house near the road. I walked up to the house. I knocked on the door. I waited, but nobody opened the door. I stood outside the house. I was very cold and very tired. I could not move.</p>

<p>"I am going to die here," I said. Then I heard a young man's voice. The man was standing behind me.</p>

<p>"No, you will not die at Moor House," the man said. Then, he unlocked the door of the house. He took me into the house. He took me into a warm sitting room.</p>

<p>"Please sit down," he said. Two pretty young women came into the room.</p>

<p>"Give this poor woman some food, Diana," the young man said. "Give her some dry clothes, Mary." Then he spoke to me again. "My name is St. John Rivers," he said. "These are my sisters, Diana and Mary. What is your name, young woman?"</p>

<p>"My name is Jane Elliot," I said. I closed my eyes.</p>

<p>"Jane is very tired," Diana said. "She must go to bed now."</p>

<p>I stayed in bed at Moor House for three days. Diana and Mary Rivers were governesses. They were staying at Moor House for a few days. St. John, their brother, was a clergyman. They were very kind to me. Soon, we were good friends.</p>

<p>One day, St. John asked me about my life. "I was a governess too," I told him. And I told him about Lowood School. But I did not tell him about Thornfield Hall. I did not tell him about Mr. Rochester.</p>

<p>"I want to work, St. John," I said. "Will you help me?"</p>

<p>"I have a plan," St. John said. "A few miles from here, there is a village. Many of the girls in the village cannot read or write. I am going to pay for a girls' school in the village. But I must find a teacher for these girls."</p>

<p>"I will teach them, St. John," I said.</p>

<p>"Good," he said. "There will be a small house next to the school. You will live there."</p>

<p>Three days later, a letter arrived for St. John. "Diana, Mary, our uncle John is dead," he told his sisters. "But we will not have any of his money." He gave the letter to his sisters. They read it.</p>

<p>"Uncle John was our mother's brother," Diana told me. "He was very rich, but he has given all his money to another niece. We do not know her."</p>

<p>Soon, I went to live in the village. I lived in the house next to the school. Every day, I taught the girls. My pupils worked hard. But I was not happy. Every day, I thought about Edward Rochester. "Does he think about me?" I asked myself.</p>

<p>Four months passed. One day, St. John Rivers came to my house. He was holding a letter. He was worried.</p>

<p>"What is wrong?" I asked.</p>

<p>"I want to ask you three questions, Jane," he replied. "Is your name Jane Elliot? Do you have another name? Do you know Jane Eyre?"</p>

<p>I looked at him for a moment. I did not speak.</p>

<p>"I have some news for Jane Eyre," St. John said. "Jane Eyre was a pupil at Lowood School, and she was a teacher there. Then she was a governess at Thornfield Hall, the home of Mr. Edward Rochester."</p>

<p>"How do you know this?" I asked. "What do you know about Mr. Rochester? How is he?"</p>

<p>"I don't know," St. John said. "This letter is from a lawyer. The lawyer tells a story about Mr. Rochester. Mr. Rochester had a mad wife, but he tried to marry Jane Eyre. She left Thornfield. Now this lawyer, Mr. Briggs, is trying to find her."</p>

<p>"I will tell you the truth, St. John," I said. "My name is not Jane Elliot. My name is Jane Eyre, and I was a governess at Thornfield Hall. I know Mr. Rochester. Did Mr. Briggs write anything about Mr. Rochester?"</p>

<p>"No. The letter is about you, Jane," St. John said. "Your uncle, John Eyre, is dead. John Eyre has given you twenty thousand pounds. You are rich, Jane."</p>

<p>"But why did Mr. Briggs write to you?" I asked.</p>

<p>"My mother's name was Eyre," St. John said. "She was your father's sister, Jane. Then you, Diana and Mary, are my cousins," I said. I thought carefully for a moment. "Write to Diana and Mary," I said. "They must come home. I will give all of you some of Uncle John's money."</p>

<p>The next day, I wrote to Mr. Briggs. I gave St. John, Diana and Mary five thousand pounds each. I wrote to Mrs. Fairfax, too. But she did not reply. Six months passed. I heard nothing from Thornfield Hall. I heard nothing about Mr. Rochester.</p>

<p>Then, one day, I was walking on the moor. Suddenly, I heard a voice. There was nobody on the moor. But the voice was calling my name. "Jane, Jane, Jane."</p>

<p>"That is Mr. Rochester's voice," I said to myself. Then I shouted. "I am coming, Edward. I am coming."</p>

<p>I ran to Moor House. I spoke to my cousins. "I am going to Thornfield Hall tomorrow," I told them. I began my journey the next day.</p>

<p>I was standing on the road near Thornfield Hall. I ran across the fields. Was Mr. Rochester at Thornfield? Was he ill? And then I saw the house. The house had no roof. Its walls were burnt and black. Nobody was living there. I looked at the burnt black house. I had seen this before. I had seen it in a dream. I was frightened. Where was Edward Rochester?</p>

<p>I went to the village of Hay. I asked about Thornfield Hall. I asked about Mr. Rochester.</p>

<p>"Three months ago, there was a fire at Thornfield Hall," a man told me. "The madwoman burnt the house. She was Mr. Rochester's wife."</p>

<p>"Was Mr. Rochester in the house?" I asked.</p>

<p>"Yes," the man replied. "He was there. He tried to save his wife's life. He went into the burning house. But the madwoman jumped from the roof. She died."</p>

<p>"Was Mr. Rochester hurt?" I asked quickly.</p>

<p>"Yes, he was badly hurt. He is blind. He can't see. And he has only one hand."</p>

<p>"Where is he?" I asked. "Where is he?"</p>

<p>"He is living at Ferndean. It is an old house, about thirty miles away," the man said.</p>

<p>"Do you have a carriage?" I asked. "I must go to Ferndean immediately."</p>

<p>I got out of the carriage near Ferndean. I walked to the house. I knocked on the door. A servant opened it. I knew her.</p>

<p>"Oh, Miss Eyre! You have come!" she said. "Mr. Rochester has been calling your name."</p>

<p>A bell rang in another room. "That is Mr. Rochester's bell," the woman said. "He wants some candles." There were two candles on a table near the door. The woman lit them and she picked them up.</p>

<p>"Mr. Rochester is blind," she said, "but he always burns candles in his room in the evenings."</p>

<p>"Give the candles to me," I said. "I'll take them to him."</p>

<p>I opened the door of Mr. Rochester's room. His black and white dog was sitting by the fire. The dog jumped up and ran towards me.</p>

<p>"Who is there?" Mr. Rochester said.</p>

<p>"Don't you know me, Edward?" I asked. "Your dog knows me." I put the candles on a table. I held Mr. Rochester's hand.</p>

<p>"I know that voice and I know this little hand," Mr. Rochester said. "Is that you, Jane?"</p>

<p>"Yes, sir," I said. "I have found you at last. I will never leave you again." Then I told Mr. Rochester my story.</p>

<p>"Why did you leave your cousins, Jane?" Mr. Rochester asked. "Why did you come back to me? I am blind. I have only one hand."</p>

<p>"I will take care of you, Edward," I said.</p>

<p>"But I don't want a servant," Mr. Rochester replied. "I want a wife."</p>

<p>"You will have a wife, Edward," I said. "I will be your wife. I will marry you. I loved you very much at Thornfield Hall. Now, I love you more."</p>

<p>Mr. Rochester and I got married. After a time, his eyes were better. He could see a little. He saw the face of our first child. My dear Edward and I are very happy.</p>
