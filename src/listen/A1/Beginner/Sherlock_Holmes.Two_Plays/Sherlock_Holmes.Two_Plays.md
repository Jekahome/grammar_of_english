<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">The Red-Headed League</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Scene 1: An Interesting Advertisement</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para"><em>The inside of Wilson's shop. It's a little shop with a lot of clocks, pictures, watches, etc. Wilson is writing at a table. Vincent Spalding is looking at the Morning Chronicle newspaper.</em></p>

<p><strong>Spalding:</strong> This is interesting.</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> What is?</p>

<p><strong>Spalding:</strong> This advertisement. It is interesting for you, Mr Wilson, because you have red hair.</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> Go on.</p>

<p><strong>Spalding:</strong> 'The Red-Headed League want to find someone new.' Do you know about them? Listen. 'To all Red-Headed Men, 21 years old or more. The Red-Headed League can now take a new man. We pay four pounds a week to do easy work. Come to the League offices at Seven Pope's Court, Fleet Street, London, on Monday at eleven o'clock. You can get two hundred pounds a year.'</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> Really? Tell me about it.</p>

<p><strong>Spalding:</strong> A rich American with red hair began the League. When he died, he gave a lot of his money to the Red-Headed League to give easy work to men with red hair. It's very good wages for only a little work. Why don't you go along to Pope's Court?</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> But millions of red-headed men want to get into the League.</p>

<p><strong>Spalding:</strong> You need to live in London, but don't worry. You must have very red hair. You have very red hair, Mr Wilson.</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> Monday. Eleven o'clock. It's Monday today, and it's nearly eleven o'clock. Come on!</p>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">The Red-Headed League</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Scene 2: Some Easy Work for Wilson</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para"><em>The office at Pope's Court. A room with only a table and two chairs. Duncan Ross, a man with very red hair, is sitting behind the table. We can hear the noise of a lot of people out in the street.</em></p>

<p><strong>Ross:</strong> This is Mr Jabez Wilson, and he wants to be in the League.</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> And he has a good head of hair. I can't remember when I saw a better head.</p>

<p><strong>Ross:</strong> Ah! ... Stop it!</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> I'm sorry, but we must be careful. Of course it is your hair. I can see that now. Please sit down.</p>

<p><strong>Ross:</strong> My name is Duncan Ross. Have you got a wife, Mr Wilson? And children?</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> No. I have no wife and no family.</p>

<p><strong>Ross:</strong> Oh, dear. Some of the men in our League have lots of red-headed children. We'd like that.</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> I'm sorry.</p>

<p><strong>Ross:</strong> It's all right. You have that wonderful head of hair. When can you begin work here?</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> It's not easy. I have a shop and I can stay in the shop, Mr Wilson. What hours must I work?</p>

<p><strong>Ross:</strong> Ten o'clock to two o'clock.</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> And the wages?</p>

<p><strong>Ross:</strong> Four pounds a week.</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> What must I do?</p>

<p><strong>Ross:</strong> You must work here in the office all the time. That is very important. You cannot go out between the hours of ten o'clock and two o'clock.</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> And the work?</p>

<p><strong>Ross:</strong> You must copy from this large book. And you must bring paper and a pen. Can you begin tomorrow?</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> Yes, I can.</p>

<p><strong>Ross:</strong> Then goodbye, Mr Jabez Wilson.</p>

<summary>The Red-Headed League - Scene 3</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">The Red-Headed League</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Scene 3: Wilson Tells His Story</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para"><em>Sherlock Holmes's room. Holmes and Wilson are sitting in chairs. Holmes is smoking his pipe. Wilson has the Morning Chronicle in his hands.</em></p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Most interesting, Mr Wilson. Most interesting.</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> Holmes? I'm sorry.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Come in, my dear Watson. This is Mr Jabez Wilson. He is telling me a very strange story. This is Dr Watson. He's a good friend. Give him the advertisement to read.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> He does a lot of writing, Watson. Do you see?</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> Oh, how did you know that, Mr Holmes?</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> The right arm of your coat is different from the left. Your arm is on the table when you write.</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> Yes, that's right.</p>

<p><strong>Watson:</strong> It's very strange, Holmes.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> It is strange, isn't it? Do you see the day and month of the newspaper? It's the Morning Chronicle of the 27th of April, 1890 — two months ago. Now, Mr Wilson, tell us about your shop.</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> It's in Saxe-Coburg Square, Mr Holmes. It's a little shop, and it doesn't make much money.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Does somebody help you in the shop?</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> Yes, a young man. Well, he's not very young, but he works for half wages.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Does he? What's his name?</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> Vincent Spalding. He's very good at his work. He likes to take photographs when he's not working. And he makes the pictures in my dark cellar, but he works well when he's in the shop.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> How did you first see the newspaper advertisement? Tell us that.</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> Spalding saw it eight weeks ago, and we went to Pope's Court and saw Mr Duncan Ross. I told you about the visit a few minutes ago. What happened next?</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> Well, I was very pleased, Mr Holmes. I went home feeling very happy, but that evening I began to think again. Copying from a book is strange work for a man to do, I thought.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Very strange, yes.</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> But the next morning I went to Pope's Court with my pen and some paper. Mr Ross was there, but he soon went away. Then I began my work. Well, I went in every morning at ten o'clock and went home at two. And on Saturday Ross was in the office again and gave me my four pounds.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> How long did you work there?</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> Eight weeks. But today I went to the office at ten o'clock and the door was locked. This was on the door: 'The Red-Headed League is finished. June the 23rd, 1890.'</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Then what did you do?</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> I went to the office in the next building and I asked the man, 'What happened to the Red-Headed League?' 'Who are they?' he asked. 'The man at number seven?' I asked. 'The man with the red hair,' he said, 'his name is William Morris. He moved out yesterday. He has new offices at 17 King Edward Street.'</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Well, I went to King Edward Street, Mr Holmes.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> You did not find Mr William Morris or Mr Duncan Ross, am I right?</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> You are right.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> What did you do then?</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> I went home to Saxe-Coburg Square.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> What does Spalding say about all this?</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> He says, 'Wait for a letter. You're going to hear something.'</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> What do you think?</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> I need your help, I think.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> You are right, Mr Wilson. I'm happy to help you. But first, I wanted to ask you one or two questions. Mr Spalding was the first to see the advertisement, yes?</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> Yes.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> When did he begin to work for you?</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> About three months ago.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> How did he come?</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> He answered an advertisement in the newspaper.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Was he the only man to answer it?</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> No. Twelve people answered it.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Why did you take him?</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> Because he was cheap. He is happy to work for half the usual wages.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Yes. Tell me about him — this Vincent Spalding.</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> He's small and a good, quick worker. He's about thirty years old, I think.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Where is he now?</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> He's at the shop.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> That is all, Mr Wilson. Today is Saturday. I'm going to have an answer for you before Monday.</p>

<p><strong>Wilson:</strong> Thank you, Mr Holmes.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> We must work quickly, Watson. Put on your hat. We are going to Saxe-Coburg Square.</p>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">The Red-Headed League</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Scene 7: Holmes and Watson</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para"><em>Sherlock Holmes's room. Holmes and Watson are sitting in chairs. Holmes is smoking his pipe.</em></p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> They needed the Red-Headed League to get Mr Wilson out of his shop. Do you understand that?</p>

<p><strong>Watson:</strong> Yes, I think. Um, yes.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> They wanted Wilson away from the shop for some hours every day. But how could they do it? Then Clay saw the colour of Wilson's hair and thought of a Red-Headed League. When Wilson answered their newspaper advertisement, he went to work in their office every day. Then they had time to make their tunnel.</p>

<p><strong>Watson:</strong> Very clever.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> So, Spalding is John Clay, the famous thief. When did you first know that? Spalding was happy to work for half wages — why, I thought. Spalding often went down to the cellar. What's he doing down there, I thought? Suddenly, I thought about tunnels. Is Spalding making a tunnel, I thought? To another building? Watson, you saw me hitting the ground with my stick outside the shop.</p>

<p><strong>Watson:</strong> Yes.</p>

<p><strong>Watson:</strong> Now I understand. Was the cellar in front of the shop? You wanted to know that.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Yes, I did. And it wasn't. The cellar was behind the shop.</p>

<p><strong>Watson:</strong> Then I saw the man Spalding. Did you see his trousers?</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Yes, I saw them.</p>

<p><strong>Watson:</strong> They were dirty, Watson. Why?</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Because making a tunnel is dirty work.</p>

<p><strong>Watson:</strong> Very clever, Holmes.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Then I went into the next street at the back of the shop. And what did I see? The City Bank.</p>

<p><strong>Watson:</strong> The bank, yes, of course.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Why is that young man making a tunnel, I thought? To get into the bank's strongroom.</p>

<p><strong>Watson:</strong> But why tonight? How did you know?</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Because they closed the offices of the Red-Headed League. The tunnel must be ready, I thought. And Saturday is a good day. The bank does not open on Sunday. Two days for the thieves to get away.</p>

<p><strong>Watson:</strong> Ha ha! But they didn't get away, Holmes. You're very clever.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> That's very true, Watson.</p>

<summary>The Three Students - Scene 1</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">The Three Students</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Scene 1: Hilton Soames's Story</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para"><em>Sherlock Holmes is sitting at a table, writing. It is evening. Watson comes in with Hilton Soames.</em></p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Who was at the door, Watson?</p>

<p><strong>Watson:</strong> This is Mr Hilton Soames, Holmes. He teaches at St Luke's College.</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> Can you give me some of your time, Mr Holmes? Today something happened at the college, and I was very worried. Then I thought, 'The famous detective Sherlock Holmes is visiting our town. Perhaps he can help.'</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> I have a lot of work to do, Mr Soames. Perhaps the police...</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> No, no. I can't go to the police.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Well, tell me your story, Mr Soames.</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> Tomorrow is the first day of an important examination, and I'm one of the examiners. Today, at three o'clock, the three exam papers arrived for me to read. Of course, every student would like to see these papers before the exam.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Ha ha.</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> Of course. We are always very careful with them. I began to read them, but at 4.30 p.m. I went out to have tea in a friend's rooms.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Where did you leave the papers?</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> They were on my writing table. I locked my door and was away for an hour. When I arrived home, I saw a key in my door. 'Is it my key?' I thought. No, that's in my pocket. Ah, it's Bannister's key. Bannister is my servant.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Why did he go to your room this afternoon?</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> He brings my tea in every afternoon.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> But you were at your friend's rooms. Did he forget that?</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> Yes, and so his key was in my door. He is usually careful, but...</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Tell me about the examination papers. Where were them when you went back to your room?</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> One was on a small table next to the window. One was on the floor, and one on my writing table.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> The first paper on the floor, the second in the window, and the third on the writing table.</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> Yes, how did you know that?</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Please, tell us more of your very interesting story, Mr Soames.</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> First, I thought, did Bannister look at the papers? So I asked him, but he said, 'No, I did not.' And he is an honest man, Mr Holmes.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> What happened next?</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> Bannister suddenly began to feel ill when I told him about the papers. He sat down on a chair, and I gave him a strong drink. Then I looked carefully at the table next to the window.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> What did you find?</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> Small pieces of pencil.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Did somebody break and sharpen a pencil?</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> Yes, perhaps. Yes, that was it.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Good. That helps. Two more things.</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> I have a new writing table, but now there's a little cut on it. And there was a piece of black mud on the table.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Is that all?</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> Yes. Please help me, Mr Holmes.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> It's an interesting story, Mr Soames. Tell me, did any student visit you in your room after the papers arrived this afternoon?</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> Yes. Daulat Ras, a student from India.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> And the papers were on your writing table?</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> Yes, but he couldn't read them. He was two or three metres away.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> But they were exam papers. Could he see that?</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> Perhaps.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> The examination papers were in your room. Did anyone know that?</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> No, nobody.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Did Bannister know that?</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> No, nobody.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Where is Bannister now?</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> When I ran out to find you, Mr Holmes, he was on the chair in my room. He looked very ill.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Did you leave your door open?</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> Yes. But I locked the papers up first.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Come then. We must go to your room.</p>

<summary>The Three Students - Scene 2</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">The Three Students</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Scene 2: Holmes Visits the College</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para"><em>Soames, Holmes and Watson come into Soames's living room. Holmes walks to the window and looks out.</em></p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> That window does not open, Mr Holmes. Nobody can enter the room through there.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Your servant isn't here now. Perhaps he's feeling better. Which chair did you leave him on?</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> The chair by the window.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Near this little table. Yes, yes, of course. The man comes in. He takes the three papers from the writing table to carry them across to the window. Because then he can see you when you come across the garden to the front door. But he didn't see me because I walked in through the back door.</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> Ah, that helps.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Can I see the three papers now? ... He carries this first paper across to the table near the window and begins to copy it. It takes him ten minutes?</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> Fifteen, I think.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> All right. Fifteen. Then he puts it down on the floor, gets the next paper and begins to copy that. Then he hears you coming in the back door, Mr Soames. He must be quick. He leaves the second paper on the table next to the window and runs.</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> Where?</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Did you hear him running upstairs?</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> No, I didn't.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> He's writing quickly because he breaks his pencil and needs to sharpen it again. This is interesting. The pencil was long and dark blue. Look at these pieces. And do you see the gold letters 'NN'? It's at the end of a word. Do you know Johann Faber pencils?</p>

<p><strong>Watson:</strong> I do, Holmes. I write with them.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> This is the piece of black mud. I can see the cut. What is that door?</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> It opens into my bedroom.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Did you go into your bedroom before you ran out to find me?</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> No.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> A man can hide behind this curtain. What's this?</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> It's mud again.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Your visitor was in this room, Mr Soames. But why? Think for a minute. He hears you coming. What can he do? He must hide. So he runs into your bedroom and hides behind this curtain.</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> Oh! Then he was in here when Bannister and I were in the next room.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Yes. That's right. Tell me, how many students live upstairs and go past your door?</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> Three. All taking this examination.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Who are they?</p>

<summary>The Three Students - Scene 8</summary>

<div class="book-page">
  <div class="chapter-label">The Three Students</div>
  <div class="chapter-title">Scene 8: All the Answers to All the Questions</div>
  <div class="chapter-divider"><span> ~ ~ ~ </span></div>
  <div class="book-body">
    <p class="first-para"><em>Soames's room. It is morning. A very worried Soames is bringing Holmes and Watson into the room.</em></p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> Well, Mr Holmes, can we have the examination later this morning? Or must I stop it?</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> You can have the examination, Mr Soames. But the young man isn't going to take it.</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> You know him?</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Yes, I do. Please call Bannister.</p>

<p><strong>Watson:</strong> You have the answer, Holmes?</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Yes, my dear Watson. Look. There were only two pieces of mud yesterday. Where did the third piece come from? ... Now, Bannister. I want the true story. What happened yesterday?</p>

<p><strong>Bannister:</strong> I told you everything, sir. I have nothing more to say.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Then I must tell you. When Mr Soames called you into the room yesterday, you saw something on that chair, and you sat on it before Mr Soames could see it.</p>

<p><strong>Bannister:</strong> No, sir.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Yes. And when Mr Soames went out to find me, you opened the door and said, 'You can come out now' to the man hiding behind the curtain.</p>

<p><strong>Bannister:</strong> There was no man, sir.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> That isn't true. But please go and stand next to the bedroom door. Soames, please go and ask young Gilchrist to come down.</p>

<p><em>Gilchrist comes in.</em></p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Mr Gilchrist, there are only the five of us to hear your answer to my question. Why did you copy the examination papers yesterday?</p>

<p><strong>Gilchrist:</strong> No, Mr Gilchrist, sir. I never said anything.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> No. But you are helping us now, Bannister. Tell us, Mr Gilchrist. You did something very wrong, Gilchrist. But you're sorry now. I can see that. I'm going to tell you the story, Soames. Mr Gilchrist first saw the papers through the window.</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> But nobody can get in through the window.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> That's true. But a very tall man can look through it from the garden. I could not understand this cut, but then I remembered. Gilchrist likes running.</p>

<p><strong>Watson:</strong> But how does that help, Holmes?</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Think, Watson. This young man went running yesterday afternoon. When he walked home, his running shoes were in his hands. And running shoes have spikes, of course. Yes, Gilchrist went past this window and looked in. He's a tall young man, you see. He saw the exam papers on the writing table. Then he went past Soames's door and saw the key in it. And he went into the room and put his shoes on the writing table. And a spike cut into it.</p>

<p><strong>Watson:</strong> Yes, Watson. What did you put on that chair near the window?</p>

<p><strong>Gilchrist:</strong> My watch.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> He puts his watch on the chair, then begins to copy one of the papers. 'I'm going to see Mr Soames when he comes back across the garden,' he thinks. But you walk in through the back door, Soames. Then what? Gilchrist hears you coming. Quickly takes his running shoes and runs into your bedroom to hide. One shoe leaves some black mud behind the curtain. Is this all true, Mr Gilchrist? I found the same black mud on your shoes.</p>

<p><strong>Gilchrist:</strong> Yes, sir. It's true.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Have you nothing more to say?</p>

<p><strong>Gilchrist:</strong> I have a letter here, Mr Soames. Early this morning I began to write to you. It says, 'I'm not going to take the examination. I'm going to South Africa to work for the police there.'</p>

<p><strong>Soames:</strong> I see. You're not going to take the examination now. I'm pleased to hear that.</p>

<p><strong>Gilchrist:</strong> Mr Bannister talked to me. This is the right thing to do. I understand that now.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Gilchrist was in the bedroom, Bannister, and you didn't tell Mr Soames. When you saw Gilchrist's watch, you sat on it to hide it. Why?</p>

<p><strong>Bannister:</strong> Well, sir, I was once a servant to Sir Jabez Gilchrist, this young gentleman's father. When he gambled his money away, I moved to the college to be a servant. But I always remembered Sir Jabez. He was good to me. So I did everything to help his son here. And yesterday, when Mr Soames called me, I saw Mr Gilchrist's watch on the chair. He looked at the examination papers, I thought. And I quickly sat down on the watch before Mr Soames saw it. Then Mr Soames left. Yes, sir. And I called young Mr Gilchrist. 'You looked at the exam papers,' I said to him. 'Did you copy them?' 'Yes,' he said. 'I did.' 'Then you cannot take the exam,' I said. 'Think of your father. He was an honest man, and you must be honest too.' I wanted to stop him, Mr Holmes.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> You were right, Bannister. Well, Soames, you have your answers now. And you, young man, did wrong. But you're going to put things right. You can thank Bannister for that.</p>

<p><strong>Gilchrist:</strong> I do thank him, sir.</p>

<p><strong>Holmes:</strong> Come, Watson. Our work is finished here.</p>

<p><strong>Watson:</strong> Yes, Holmes.</p>
